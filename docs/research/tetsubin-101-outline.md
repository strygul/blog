# Tetsubin 101 Detailed Article Outline

This is the Stage 1 drafting contract for an object-first field guide aimed at advanced gongfu tea practitioners. The eventual article must paraphrase independently, place inline Markdown links beside material factual claims, and preserve the dossier's evidence categories. It must not invent first-person use, make medical or nutritional-treatment claims, rank products or makers, quote current prices, expose source/claim IDs to readers, or assume that a rights-gated image will become available.

## Article Promise and Opening

- **Reader question answered:** Why should a gongfu practitioner examine the kettle as carefully as the tea and brewing vessel, and what will this guide help them decide?
- **Main conclusion:** A tetsubin is a working hot-water tool whose construction, condition, source water, and service pattern matter more than its aura. The article will teach readers to identify the object, read its fitness for use, evaluate water claims by evidence category, operate it conservatively, and buy without treating craft prestige as proof of better tea.
- **Claim IDs permitted in this section:** CLM-001, CLM-017, CLM-018, CLM-023, CLM-024, CLM-025.
- **Draft sequence:** Open with the practical mismatch between the familiar promise—rounder, sweeter, or mellower water—and the narrower question the evidence can answer: what one documented kettle does with one source water under one protocol. Establish that source water already affects extraction and sensory perception. Promise an object-first path from definition and making to condition, water, use, and purchase. Keep the tone curious rather than debunking; reported sensory language is a reason to test, not a conclusion.
- **Necessary caveats:** Attribute sensory language as reported, never as the author's experience. Do not imply that a tetsubin improves every tea, fixes unsuitable water, or guarantees a repeatable change. Do not preview medical benefits, Fe(II) dosing, maker rankings, or product recommendations.
- **Planned supporting-image IDs:** None. Keep the opening text-first; do not reserve space for an unavailable supporting image.

## What a Tetsubin Is

- **Reader question answered:** What distinguishes a tetsubin from an enameled cast-iron teapot, a *kyūsu*, or a *chanoyu-gama*, and what does “unlined” safely mean?
- **Main conclusion:** A tetsubin is a spouted cast-iron vessel with a lid and overhead handle, intended to heat and pour water. Function, maker-stated heating permission, and documented interior treatment are the decisive checks; exterior silhouette and retail wording are not.
- **Claim IDs permitted in this section:** CLM-001, CLM-002, CLM-003, CLM-005, CLM-025, CLM-035.
- **Draft sequence:** Define the body, spout, lid, handle, and water-boiling job in one compact passage. Contrast that job with tea infusion in a commonly enamelled iron teapot and with ladling water from a *chanoyu-gama*. Explain the article's operational use of “unlined”: non-enamel and maker-approved for boiling, while an oxide film or interior lacquer may still be present. End with a three-part identification check: intended use, exact interior, exact heating instructions.
- **Necessary caveats:** Do not say that every dark interior is bare iron, magnetite, or safe for heat. Maker-specific exceptions require explicit documentation. Nambu is a regional tradition, not the definition of the object. English retailer categories are not authoritative.
- **Planned supporting-image IDs:** IMG-001 may appear only after the original-art copyright assignment is recorded; otherwise omit it. IMG-005 and IMG-006 form one comparison and may appear only if both written permissions are recorded; if either remains blocked, omit the pair rather than reserving space for it.
- **Transition to the next section:** Once the category boundary is clear, separate the age of the vessel form from the older casting traditions that produced it.

## How Tetsubin Developed

- **Reader question answered:** When did the recognizable tetsubin emerge, and how do Nambu, Yamagata, Kyoto, and Takaoka fit without collapsing them into one origin story?
- **Main conclusion:** The distinct spouted, overhead-handled iron kettle is documented in the eighteenth century and emerged from older Japanese casting and kettle traditions during the Edo period. Nambu is the best-documented case, but it is neither the whole category nor a single ancient lineage.
- **Claim IDs permitted in this section:** CLM-004, CLM-005, CLM-006, CLM-036, CLM-037, CLM-042, CLM-043, CLM-044, CLM-045, CLM-046.
- **Draft sequence:** Begin with the eighteenth-century Kyoto museum object as secure object evidence. Step back to seventeenth-century Morioka ironworking and *chanoyu*-kettle production as predecessor history, then identify the attributed Koizumi lineage without presenting it as a settled invention. Explain the distinct Morioka and Oshu/Mizusawa histories and their 1959 organizational union under the Nambu name. Place Yamagata beside Nambu as a separate designated casting tradition, and use Takaoka briefly to demonstrate geographic breadth. Close with the 1975 Traditional Craft designations as an administrative milestone, not a universal quality certificate.
- **Necessary caveats:** SRC-023 and SRC-024 are two pages from the same association and do not independently corroborate each other. Keep the regional-tradition clock separate from the vessel-form clock; reject the “400-year-old vessel form” shortcut. The first appearance and inventor remain unsettled. Do not turn a concise 101 history into a maker catalogue, signature chronology, or connoisseurship guide.
- **Planned supporting-image IDs:** None. The section can rely on concise inline links to the museum and institutional records rather than a rights-gated historical illustration.
- **Transition to the next section:** The historical lineages matter because they transmit techniques, but a finished kettle becomes legible only when its mold, core, casting, and finishing sequence are understood.

## How One Is Made

- **Reader question answered:** How does a hollow cast-iron kettle emerge from molds and a temporary core, and which production details are traditional examples rather than universal rules?
- **Main conclusion:** In one well-documented traditional Nambu sequence, separate body, bottom, core, lid, spout, lug, and knob elements are prepared; the mold/core gap establishes the wall; iron is cast; the core is broken out; the object is fettled, oxidized, checked, colored, and fitted with its handle. Workshops and regions legitimately vary the recipe, materials, inspection points, and finish.
- **Claim IDs permitted in this section:** CLM-007, CLM-008, CLM-009, CLM-010, CLM-011, CLM-032, CLM-038, CLM-040, CLM-047, CLM-048, CLM-049, CLM-059.
- **Draft sequence:** Introduce the principal parts through the outer mold and *nakago* relationship. Walk through Horie's fired-loam sequence: modeled accessory parts, successively finer mold layers, hand-impressed *arare*, two-part sand/clay core, smoking, assembly, and casting. Continue through core removal, interior sand removal, seam fettling, oxidation, leak inspection, exterior coloring, and handle fitting. Explain Horie's attributed 800–1000 °C *kama-yaki* treatment and Oigen's approximately 900 °C current variant without blending them. Close with variation in mold systems, lid materials, handle construction, oxidation, lacquer, and cashew finishes.
- **Necessary caveats:** This is a documented sequence, not the definition of every tetsubin. A visible *arare* pattern cannot prove hand impressing, and a faint parting line cannot prove age or method. Do not rank handmade above industrial production on water performance, universalize Fe3O4 treatment, or infer interior chemistry from color.
- **Planned supporting-image IDs:** IMG-007 may appear only after written permission for the named Iwachu frames and editorial treatment is recorded; otherwise omit the process strip. IMG-001 can support the mold/core explanation only if its original-art assignment has cleared and it was not already used in the definition section.
- **Transition to the next section:** Process knowledge gives the reader a vocabulary for inspection, but visible features remain signals to test rather than authenticity verdicts.

## How to Read a Tetsubin

- **Reader question answered:** Which visible and serviceable features help assess workmanship, condition, provenance, and fitness for heat, and which appearances are misleading?
- **Main conclusion:** Read a kettle first as a load-bearing, venting, pouring hot-water vessel: purpose, finish, cracks and leakage, lugs and handle, lid and steam path, spout, capacity, mass, and repair history come before marks, motifs, patina, or prestige.
- **Claim IDs permitted in this section:** CLM-011, CLM-012, CLM-025, CLM-028, CLM-033, CLM-034, CLM-039, CLM-040, CLM-041, CLM-046, CLM-059, CLM-060, CLM-061, CLM-062, CLM-063.
- **Draft sequence:** Start with maker-stated boiling purpose and exact interior treatment. Move around the object: cold leak check at base and spout root; lug and handle security; hand clearance and filled balance; lid fit plus a safe steam path; unobstructed spout and controllable cutoff; working capacity, empty mass, and water mass. Distinguish practical cleanup from authenticity claims: burrs and blocked passages are concerns, while a cleaned seam or aligned *arare* is not proof of method. Separate expected adherent oxide/scale from loose material, deep pitting, seepage, unstable attachments, persistent colored/off water, and unknown repairs. End with marks and provenance as research leads, not verdicts.
- **Necessary caveats:** A photograph can reveal some rejection signs but cannot identify an interior layer, test leakage or load, measure iron release, or authenticate workshop, age, or repair. Thin/light casting is a documented Yamagata aim, not a universal quality rank. Copper and iron lids, fixed and folding handles, and solid and hollow constructions can all be legitimate. Escalation language must remain conservative rather than diagnosing safety remotely.
- **Planned supporting-image IDs:** IMG-002 is approved under CC BY-SA 3.0 and may support exterior feature reading with creator credit, license link, crop/change notes, and ShareAlike handling. IMG-003 may supplement material variation only after museum permission is recorded; IMG-004 may support condition categories only after Iwachu permission is recorded. Do not plan either gated image as available in the draft.
- **Transition to the next section:** An object can be sound and well made without producing a predictable water effect, so the next section moves from visual evidence to measured chemistry.

## What It May Do to Water

- **Reader question answered:** What has actually been measured in tetsubin-heated water, which mechanisms are plausible, and what remains only reported or unsupported for gongfu tea?
- **Main conclusion:** An individual tetsubin may change water, but direction and magnitude depend on the kettle, finish, scale, source water, evaporation, and heating protocol. The best direct study is useful precisely because it shows small changes in four tested new Nanbu kettles and much larger changes in two unidentified objects; it cannot establish a universal tetsubin effect or a quality ranking.
- **Claim IDs permitted in this section:** CLM-013, CLM-014, CLM-015, CLM-016, CLM-017, CLM-018, CLM-019, CLM-020, CLM-021, CLM-022, CLM-023, CLM-024, CLM-050, CLM-051, CLM-052, CLM-053, CLM-054, CLM-055, CLM-066, CLM-067.
- **Draft sequence:** Establish the water-to-tea side first: controlled non-iron brewing research shows that pH, conductivity, mineral/ion composition, and whole-water differences can alter extraction, color, turbidity, stability, and sensory response, with tea-dependent outcomes. Then describe SRC-018's design before its results: six new kettles, low-hardness Hiroshima tap water starting at pH 7.00 and hardness 19.00 mg/L, four Oigen-supplied Nanbu objects, two foreign objects of unknown manufacture, and no inert heated control. Report the relatively small Nanbu changes, evaporation-corrected hardness interpretation, and maximum discussed total-iron value of about 0.03 mg/L; then report the larger two-object results and the authors' desorption proposal without turning origin into a cause. Treat the matcha extraction result as chemistry, not blinded taste. Finish with four explicit categories: demonstrated object-specific measurements; plausible chemistry-dependent transfer; reported words such as “rounder” and “sweeter”; unsupported universal softening, unique chlorine removal, consistent Fe(II), superior heat retention, best-tea pairing, and medical benefit.
- **Necessary caveats:** Say “total iron,” not Fe(II), dissolved iron, bioavailable iron, or a useful dose. Bicarbonate is not a full alkalinity titration. Ordinary heating changes pH and residual chlorine, and free chlorine is not interchangeable with chloramine. SRC-018 is small, asymmetric, and not a repeated-infusion gongfu or blinded sensory study. Pipe evidence supports mechanisms, not kettle magnitude or safety. Do not use a current model, nationality, craft method, or price as a proxy for water performance.
- **Planned supporting-image IDs:** None. Do not turn the six-object study into a decorative chart unless a later, separately reviewed visual specification is approved.
- **Transition to the next section:** Because the evidence does not supply a universal taste answer, the useful practitioner response is controlled service: know the water, follow the exact kettle, and change one variable at a time.

## Using One at the Gongfu Table

- **Reader question answered:** How should an advanced gongfu practitioner prepare, heat, place, pour, dry, monitor, and compare a tetsubin without universalizing one maker's routine?
- **Main conclusion:** Use the tetsubin as the hot-water supply beside the brewing vessel. Follow the exact maker's finish, fill, heat, first-use, and care instructions; the shared conservative core is moderate heat, roughly 70–80% working fill unless otherwise specified, a clear steam path, controlled pouring, prompt emptying, complete drying, and no interior scrubbing.
- **Claim IDs permitted in this section:** CLM-001, CLM-017, CLM-018, CLM-023, CLM-024, CLM-026, CLM-027, CLM-028, CLM-029, CLM-030, CLM-031, CLM-033, CLM-041, CLM-053, CLM-056, CLM-057, CLM-058, CLM-059, CLM-060, CLM-061, CLM-062, CLM-065, CLM-067, CLM-068.
- **Draft sequence:** Begin with a cold inspection and maker identification. Explain the conflicting first-use routines, then give only the shared conservative preparation core. Cover model-level heat compatibility, moderate power, working fill and water mass, lid/steam management, filled balance and controlled pouring, and the kettle's boil-and-pour role outside the leaf-infusion vessel. State the dry-boil emergency rule plainly: turn off heat, allow natural cooling, and never refill while hot. After service, empty promptly, remove or offset the lid, and dry fully according to maker guidance. Advise against routine interior scrubbing or detergent, and defer any more aggressive or abrasive cleaning to the exact maker's guidance. Distinguish intact oxide, adherent scale, active loose corrosion, and structural/coating failure; attribute tannin care and hard-water conditioning to named workshops. Give a practitioner comparison protocol: same source water, tea, dose, infusion schedule, and temperature at the leaf; compare blind against an inert heated control and treat sensory words as observations.
- **Necessary caveats:** The 70–80% range and lid positions are convergent trade practices, not independent engineering limits. Heat-source support, first-use repetition, hard-water conditioning, dry-reheat practice, cleaning, and storage packaging vary by model or maker. Do not prescribe a universal cleaning, restoration, or tannin routine. Clear water and stable appearance do not override cracks, leakage, loose material, deep pitting, unstable lugs, unknown coatings, or repairs. A home comparison cannot establish a general tetsubin effect.
- **Planned supporting-image IDs:** IMG-008 may appear only after direct written permission is recorded; otherwise omit the gongfu-table photograph. IMG-004 may appear here instead of the reading section only after Iwachu permission is recorded; do not duplicate it or assume clearance.
- **Transition to the next section:** The operating checks reveal what the buyer must know before money changes hands: intended job, working load, documented finish, compatible heat, condition, and support.

## A Compact Buying Framework

- **Reader question answered:** How can a gongfu practitioner shortlist a working kettle without current product rankings, price tables, or authenticity shortcuts?
- **Main conclusion:** Buy for a defined service job. Prioritize documented boiling purpose, exact interior, model-level heat compatibility, working capacity and filled weight, sound structure, controllable handling and pour, disclosed construction, provenance, and a credible repair or return path.
- **Claim IDs permitted in this section:** CLM-003, CLM-010, CLM-011, CLM-012, CLM-025, CLM-031, CLM-032, CLM-033, CLM-034, CLM-039, CLM-040, CLM-041, CLM-046, CLM-054, CLM-058, CLM-061, CLM-062, CLM-063, CLM-064, CLM-065.
- **Draft sequence:** Present the checklist in this order: intended job; exact interior treatment; exact heat source; working capacity and estimated total in-use mass based on the empty kettle plus the intended water load; cracks, seepage, repairs, pitting, and water condition; secure handle/lugs, lid control, steam path, spout, and filled balance; wall/mass relative to capacity; disclosed mold and finish method; provenance beyond a stamp; repair and return route. Follow with three non-price use cases—documented working kettle, craft-focused kettle, and artist/collector kettle—while keeping heat suitability independent of prestige. Explain that labor-intensive molds, decoration, fitting, handle work, finish, authorship, rarity, provenance, and support may justify cost without proving sweeter water or superior heat retention.
- **Necessary caveats:** Do not name recommended products, rank makers or regions, or state current prices. “Japanese,” “Nambu,” “handmade,” “old,” and a stamp are not sufficient. A seller photograph cannot establish safety or authenticity; require object-level leak and pour checks plus specialist assessment where appropriate. No universal ideal capacity, wall thickness, mass, or heat source exists.
- **Planned supporting-image IDs:** None. The framework should remain durable and product-neutral rather than becoming a shopping gallery.
- **Transition to the next section:** A well-chosen kettle can improve service fit and invite controlled water experiments, but it cannot compensate for every upstream problem or settle claims the evidence has not tested.

## What a Tetsubin Cannot Fix

- **Reader question answered:** Which attractive promises should the reader reject even after choosing and caring for a good kettle?
- **Main conclusion:** A tetsubin cannot make unsuitable source water analytically soft, guarantee better tea, supply a known Fe(II) dose, treat iron deficiency, uniquely remove disinfectant, authenticate itself through appearance, make every heat source safe, or convert craft value into measured water performance.
- **Claim IDs permitted in this section:** CLM-012, CLM-019, CLM-020, CLM-021, CLM-022, CLM-024, CLM-030, CLM-031, CLM-032, CLM-042, CLM-052, CLM-054, CLM-055, CLM-059, CLM-060, CLM-061.
- **Draft sequence:** Use a compact synthesis rather than a long myth list. Group the limits into four paragraphs: water language (“soft” versus measured hardness and bicarbonate versus alkalinity); causal claims (ordinary heating, disinfectant, total iron versus Fe species); tea and heat claims (tea-dependent responses, no best pairing, no matched cooling evidence); and object claims (color, marks, handmade status, history, and IH compatibility). Close by returning to the article's practical standard: a tetsubin is worthwhile when its object qualities and service behavior justify it even before a taste promise is made.
- **Necessary caveats:** Unsupported claims may be named only to reject or qualify them. Do not repeat medical marketing in a way that reads as an open possibility. Do not dismiss reported sensory experience; position it as a hypothesis for controlled comparison. Keep “400 years” attached only to attributed regional or workshop lineage, never the distinct vessel form.
- **Planned supporting-image IDs:** None. The conclusion should end on a clear evidence boundary, not an illustrative claim that introduces new rights or sourcing work.
- **Transition to the next section:** Give readers a short path to the museum, craft, workshop, and technical sources behind the definition, construction, care, and water conclusions.
