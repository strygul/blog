# Tetsubin 101 Research Dossier

Research date: 2026-07-21
Article audience: Advanced gongfu tea practitioners

## Scope and Evidence Rules

This dossier supports an object-first introduction to tetsubin: what the object is, where it sits among Japanese casting traditions, how it is made, how to evaluate and use one, and what evidence supports claims about water and tea. It is a research record, not an article draft and not a record of personal use.

Source priority follows the approved design:

1. Japanese government, prefectural, municipal, museum, and craft-institution sources for terminology, history, and regional traditions.
2. Established workshops for their own methods, product specifications, and care instructions. These are **trade sources**, not independent validation of quality, taste, or health claims.
3. Peer-reviewed research and public-health technical reviews for iron release, corrosion, scale, water chemistry, and tea extraction.
4. Practitioner and seller observations only as subjective reports. Retail repetition is not treated as corroboration.

Evidence rules used throughout:

- Water and brewing claims are classified only as **Demonstrated**, **Plausible**, **Reported**, or **Unsupported**.
- “Demonstrated” requires direct measurement for the exact proposition. Results from a small set of tetsubin are not generalized to every kettle.
- “Plausible” means that established chemistry or adjacent-vessel evidence supports a mechanism, while direct tetsubin or gongfu testing is absent or inadequate.
- “Reported” means a subjective observation, even if it recurs in workshop, government-promotion, or practitioner material.
- “Unsupported” means available evidence does not establish the claim, contradicts it, or cannot separate the proposed mechanism from ordinary boiling or source-water effects.
- Evidence from cast-iron pipe or cookware can support a corrosion or transfer mechanism but cannot prove the magnitude, safety, or sensory result for a tetsubin.
- Workshop practices are attributed to the named maker. A finish, mold, first-use routine, heat-source claim, or care method is not universal unless independent sources establish it.
- Conflicting credible accounts remain visible. Historical “origin” stories are phrased as traditions or attributions, not settled dates.
- SRC-023 and SRC-024 are two pages published by the same Association for the Promotion of Traditional Craft Industries. They may clarify different parts of that association's account, but they do not count as independent corroboration of one another.
- No medical, iron-deficiency, nutritional-treatment, or therapeutic conclusion is permitted. The article should not convert measured iron release into a health recommendation.
- Stable IDs in the source ledger and claim matrix are the citation layer for Tasks 2–6.

## Terminology

- **Tetsubin (鉄瓶):** a cast-iron kettle with a body, pouring spout, lid, and overhead handle, made to heat and pour water. Museum records describe it as a tool for boiling and pouring water, and an eighteenth-century Kyoto National Museum example has an iron-cast body, spout, cast-in handle lugs, and a copper handle [SRC-005; SRC-007].
- **Tetsu kyūsu / iron teapot (鉄急須):** a vessel for infusing and serving tea. In current production it is commonly glass-enamelled inside; Iwachu applies enamel to teapot interiors, and Oigen warns that its enamelled iron teapots should not be heated because the enamel may crack [SRC-011; SRC-013]. “Cast-iron teapot” and “tetsubin” must not be used interchangeably.
- **Unlined:** for this article, “unlined tetsubin” means **not glass-enamel-lined and intended by its maker for boiling water**. It does not necessarily mean visually bare metal. A kettle may have a heat-grown oxide film, interior lacquer, or another maker-specific surface treatment [SRC-010; SRC-012; SRC-014].
- **Kama (釜):** a broader pot or cauldron category, not a synonym for tetsubin. Sources on the craft distinguish tetsubin from *chanoyu-gama* and from other pots and pans [SRC-009; SRC-023].
- **Chagama / chanoyu-gama (茶釜 / 茶の湯釜):** an iron kama used in chanoyu. Unlike the familiar tetsubin form, it lacks the tetsubin's pouring spout and permanently fitted overhead handle; water is ladled out. Historical accounts connect tetsubin development to chagama but do not establish a single uncontested invention event [SRC-008; SRC-009].
- **Nambu tekki (南部鉄器):** the government-designated Iwate ironware tradition associated with Morioka and Oshu. It includes tetsubin, chagama, cookware, and other ironwares; it is not a synonym for every Japanese tetsubin [SRC-003; SRC-009].
- **Yamagata imono (山形鋳物):** a separate government-designated casting tradition whose products include chagama, tetsubin, iron cookware, bronze vessels, and other cast work [SRC-004].
- **Yakigata / mane-gata (焼型 / 真土型):** a fired loam/sand mold method used for traditional iron-kettle production. Horie calls the loam material *mane* and explains that firing the mold is why the method is also called *yakigata* [SRC-009].
- **Nakago (中子):** the internal core. The gap between it and the outer mold defines the wall thickness and hollow interior [SRC-009; SRC-010].
- **Tane-mono (種物):** separately modeled elements such as the spout, handle lugs, and lid knob, incorporated into the body or lid mold before casting [SRC-009].
- **Tsuru (鉉 / 弦):** the overhead handle. Materials and fabrication vary; examples include copper, steel, and hollow forged-sheet handles [SRC-005; SRC-012; SRC-014].
- **Kan-tsuki (鐶付):** the pair of lugs cast into the shoulders to receive the handle [SRC-005; SRC-009].
- **Kama-yaki / kanake-dome (釜焼き / 金気止め):** high-temperature oxidation treatments intended to form an iron-oxide surface. Horie describes roughly 800–1000 °C for about 20 minutes and identifies magnetite, Fe3O4; Oigen describes its own approximately 900 °C process and calls the result an oxidation film [SRC-009; SRC-011]. The terminology and exact process should be attributed.
- **Arare (霰):** a raised “hailstone” pattern. In the documented traditional process, the negative pattern is impressed into the damp mold point by point [SRC-009; SRC-010]. A visible arare pattern alone does not prove that this manual method was used.
- **Urushi, cashew, ohaguro, cha-jiru (漆, カシュー, おはぐろ, 茶汁):** exterior or maker-specific finishing materials. Iwachu documents baked lacquer followed by iron-vinegar or tea-liquid coloring; Oigen documents both natural lacquer and modern cashew coating [SRC-010; SRC-011].
- **Yugaka (湯垢):** mineral scale deposited during water heating, commonly involving calcium and magnesium compounds. Workshop sources treat a stable white film as desirable; technical pipe literature shows that scale composition and protectiveness depend on water chemistry and cannot be inferred from color alone [SRC-017; SRC-019].

### How consistent are these boundaries?

The located Japanese museum records, craft-institution accounts, and current workshop pages use a functionally consistent core: *tetsubin* names a spouted, handled water-boiling vessel, while *kyūsu* names a tea-infusing vessel and *chanoyu-gama* a ladled tea-water cauldron [SRC-005; SRC-006; SRC-009; SRC-011; SRC-013; SRC-023]. They are not completely consistent about interior treatment: current heat-intended kettles may be described as oxidized, lacquered, or otherwise finished [SRC-010; SRC-011; SRC-014]. The article should therefore classify an object by intended function, heating permission, and documented interior treatment, not by the English listing terms “cast-iron teapot” or “unlined” alone.

## History and Regional Traditions

### What can be stated securely

- Tetsubin existed by the eighteenth century: Kyoto National Museum catalogues an unsigned Japanese iron-cast kettle from that century with a side spout, cast-in shoulder lugs, and copper handle [SRC-005].
- The Association for the Promotion of Traditional Craft Industries likewise places the start of the famous Nambu tetsubin in the eighteenth century, after the seventeenth-century establishment of the Morioka ironworking and tea-kettle tradition [SRC-023; SRC-024]. These are two different clocks: the age of a regional craft lineage and the age of the distinct vessel form.
- The object was not exclusive to Iwate. A Takaoka City Museum record identifies a late-Edo-to-Meiji iron kettle by Kanamori Sahei and describes it as a tool for boiling and pouring water [SRC-006]. Yamagata's designated casting tradition also explicitly includes tetsubin [SRC-004].
- “Nambu ironware” now brings together two regional histories. Iwate Prefecture and Horie distinguish Morioka, where domain patronage and tea-kettle casting developed in the seventeenth century, from the Oshu/Mizusawa lineage, traditionally traced to late-Heian casting and later everyday ironware [SRC-003; SRC-009]. Horie says the two associations were joined under the Nambu name in 1959 [SRC-009].
- Both Nambu ironware and Yamagata cast metal were among the first crafts designated under the Traditional Craft Industries law on 1975-02-17 [SRC-002; SRC-004; SRC-009]. The designation describes a regulated regional tradition; it does not mean that every object sold with regional language is certified, handmade, or suitable for boiling [SRC-001].

### Origin accounts and conflicts

- The California Academy research archive says the date of the first tetsubin is unclear and presents a relationship with the spread of sencha as a hypothesis [SRC-008].
- Horie gives the documented Morioka chagama lineage, including Koizumi Goroshichi Kiyoyuki's appointment in 1659, then relays the tradition that third-generation Koizumi Nizaemon reduced a tea kettle and added a spout and handle, with the name evolving from *tetsu-yakan* through other terms to *tetsubin* over roughly a century [SRC-009].
- Iwate Prefecture compresses the Morioka beginning to “early seventeenth century” and separately gives the Oshu origin tradition [SRC-003]. These accounts operate at different levels—regional ironware, tea-kettle production, and the tetsubin form—and should not be merged into a single precise invention date.
- The article can say that tetsubin emerged from older casting and kettle forms during the Edo period, in a tea and domestic-hot-water context. It should not say that one named person “invented the tetsubin in year X” as settled fact.

### Restrained working timeline

1. **Before the tetsubin form:** older kama, handled water vessels, and regional casting traditions supplied techniques and possible formal precedents. In Morioka, the securely sourced institutional chronology begins with seventeenth-century domain-supported ironworking and chanoyu-kettle production; Yamagata and Oshu have separate, much older regional origin traditions [SRC-003; SRC-004; SRC-009; SRC-023]. These dates are not dates for the tetsubin itself.
2. **Eighteenth century:** the distinct spouted, overhead-handled iron kettle is documented by an extant Kyoto National Museum object, and the craft association dates Nambu tetsubin development to this century [SRC-005; SRC-024]. The California Academy museum synthesis links its emergence to expanding sencha practice but explicitly says the first appearance is unclear [SRC-008].
3. **Eighteenth to early nineteenth century:** the museum synthesis describes a dual use: an ordinary household tool for heating water and preparing tea, alongside increasingly ornamented and status-bearing examples ranging from plain kitchen kettles to elaborate art objects [SRC-008]. Treat this as a secondary museum synthesis rather than a precisely dated national sequence.
4. **Meiji into the early twentieth century:** Horie reports that maker marks became common around mid-Meiji in connection with exhibitions and responsibility for authorship; later design and research institutions encouraged art names and new techniques [SRC-009]. This supports a clearer authored/art-kettle market, not the claim that earlier unsigned kettles lack attribution or value.
5. **Modern period:** government designation in 1975 formalized Nambu ironware and Yamagata cast metal as distinct regional craft industries, while museum collections and current production encompass working, decorative, and collectible kettles [SRC-002; SRC-004; SRC-005; SRC-006; SRC-009]. Intended heat use still has to be established object by object.

### Testing the “400-year history” formula

Workshop and promotional language can legitimately count a family or Morioka ironworking lineage from the early seventeenth century—Suzuki Morihisa, for example, dates its own workshop to 1625 [SRC-015]. But the located institutional sources place Nambu tetsubin development in the eighteenth century, and the earliest museum object in this dossier is also eighteenth century [SRC-005; SRC-024]. “A roughly four-century regional ironworking tradition” is therefore defensible with attribution; “the tetsubin is a 400-year-old vessel form” collapses regional ironworking, chanoyu-kettle production, a workshop lineage, and the later spouted form into one unsupported date.

### Article-safe regional framing

Use Nambu as the best-documented regional case, not as the whole category. Put Yamagata beside it as an independently designated tradition with its own thin-wall, surface, and mold vocabulary [SRC-004; SRC-012]. Briefly use the Kyoto and Takaoka museum objects to show that the broader history is geographically richer than a single label [SRC-005; SRC-006].

## Anatomy and Construction

### Principal parts

1. **Body — *dō* / *hontai* (胴 / 本体):** the water-bearing cast shell. Horie's process distinguishes the main body mold (*dō-gata*, 胴型) from the bottom mold [SRC-009].
2. **Bottom/base — *shiri* (尻):** the heat-contact area, formed by a separate *shiri-gata* (尻型) in the documented traditional sequence; Horie describes the central casting gate at this bottom section [SRC-009].
3. **Mouth — *kuchi* (口), and shoulder:** the opening receives the lid; the shoulders carry the handle lugs.
4. **Spout — *sosogiguchi* (注ぎ口):** modeled separately in the traditional sequence with its own small core, then incorporated into the body mold so the spout casts with the body [SRC-009].
5. **Handle lugs — *kan-tsuki* (鐶付):** the cast-in pair that receive the overhead handle [SRC-005; SRC-009].
6. **Overhead handle — *tsuru* (鉉 / 弦):** commonly fitted late in production. It may be copper or steel, and solid-bar and hollow forged-sheet constructions are both documented [SRC-005; SRC-014; SRC-023].
7. **Lid — *futa* (蓋), and knob — *tsumami* (つまみ):** made in separate upper/lower lid molds in Horie's process. Materials need not match the body: documented current examples include cast-iron and copper lids [SRC-009; SRC-012; SRC-014].
8. **Internal core — *nakago* (中子):** the temporary sand/clay form that establishes the hollow interior. Its clearance from the outer mold establishes the cast wall thickness; it is broken out after casting [SRC-009; SRC-010; SRC-023].

### Traditional sequence documented by Horie

Horie divides traditional Nambu kettle production into design/pattern making; separately modeled spout, lugs, and knob; molds for body, bottom, core, and lid; melting and pouring; fettling and kama-yaki; coloring; and handle fitting [SRC-009]. The described body mold uses successively finer fired loam, receives hand-impressed surface work while damp, and is itself fired above about 800 °C. The core is formed from sand and clay-water in two halves, joined, and dried. The finished mold is smoked before assembly and casting [SRC-009].

After the iron cools, the documented institutional sequence opens the molds, breaks out the core, removes adhering interior sand, knocks or files away metal flash at mold parting lines, and wire-brushes the surface. It then applies the high-temperature oxide treatment, removes the exterior oxide as required, checks for leakage, colors the exterior, and fits the separately forged handle [SRC-023]. Horie confirms the same broad order but does not place a separate leak-test stage in his abbreviated list [SRC-009]. Treat the inspection point and tools as documented variants, not a mandatory sequence for every workshop.

For arare work, Horie reports about 2,000 individual impressions on one example and emphasizes alignment and depth. Iwachu likewise describes impressing each point with a conical brass tool [SRC-009; SRC-010]. This evidence supports how documented traditional arare is made; it does not let a buyer authenticate technique from pattern appearance alone.

Horie describes a post-casting treatment at approximately 800–1000 °C for about 20 minutes, forming an Fe3O4 film over the object. The exterior film is then brushed back before coloring with lacquer, iron-vinegar, and tea liquid; the interior oxide is retained as a corrosion-resistant surface [SRC-009]. Oigen's maker-specific version uses approximately 900 °C and leaves most of its kettle interiors in that oxidized state [SRC-011].

## Reading Condition and Workmanship

Read a tetsubin first as a working hot-water vessel, then as a signed or decorative object.

### Fitness for use

- **Maker-stated purpose:** confirm that the exact model is sold for boiling, not merely as an iron teapot. A smooth, glass-like enamel interior points toward teapot construction and should not be put on heat unless its maker explicitly permits it [SRC-011; SRC-013].
- **Interior treatment:** ask for the maker's wording—kama-yaki, oxidized, interior lacquer, enamel, resin, or other coating. Dark color alone cannot identify the treatment [SRC-011; SRC-014].
- **Cracks and leakage:** inspect the base, spout root, shoulder/lug junctions, and any old repair. Iwachu advises checking for cracks and handle security before first use [SRC-017]. For a used piece, obtain a seller-authorized cold-water leak test or maker inspection; do not make an undocumented antique the first boiling experiment.
- **Handle and lugs:** check firm attachment, adequate hand clearance, and balance when full. A fixed handle is not a fault; if the maker designed it to fold, movement should be smooth without looseness or levering the lugs. Hollow forged-sheet and solid-bar handles are both documented, so apparent thickness is not a reliable guide to weight or quality [SRC-014; SRC-023]. Every metal handle should still be treated as hot.
- **Lid and steam path:** a close fit is useful, but the vessel must vent. Oigen and Iwachu warn that boiling with the lid closed can cause spitting or overflow, and advise removing or offsetting it [SRC-014; SRC-017].
- **Spout:** look for an open passage, a clean lip, and no crack at the root. The craft-institution account explicitly identifies filing the spout as consequential to pouring, but claims of a “perfect pour” remain workshop or seller claims unless tested with the individual kettle [SRC-023].
- **Capacity, mass, and wall:** compare usable fill, not brimful capacity, and include one kilogram per liter for the water. Core clearance establishes wall thickness, while Yamagata sources specifically value thin, light casting [SRC-004; SRC-009; SRC-012]. Neither low mass nor thick walls alone rank workmanship across traditions.

### Workmanship signals, not verdicts

- **Wall and stance:** a stable base, a visually even rim, and mass consistent with the stated capacity and workshop method are useful screening signals. Exact wall uniformity cannot be authenticated from weight or an interior photograph alone.
- **Casting seams and cleanup:** traditional multi-part molds create parting lines and excess metal that are removed during fettling [SRC-023]. Sharp burrs, deep grinding gouges, or incompletely opened passages are practical concerns; a faint cleaned line is not by itself proof of poor casting or machine production.
- Arare alignment and depth can show mold skill in documented hand work [SRC-009; SRC-010], but a decorative motif does not authenticate origin or production method.
- Thin walls can reduce mass and are a stated Yamagata/Chobundo strength [SRC-004; SRC-012], but thinness is not automatically more durable or better for every heat source.
- Copper versus iron lids, lacquer versus cashew exteriors, and fixed versus folding handles are design choices, not a universal ranking [SRC-011; SRC-012; SRC-014].

### Condition categories for a working kettle

- **Expected/stable surface:** an intact maker-documented oxide or lacquer treatment, or adherent mineral scale, can be normal. Color alone cannot identify which surface is present [SRC-011; SRC-014; SRC-017].
- **Monitor rather than authenticate:** small stationary orange points, patina, a faint fettled seam, and mineral deposition are not by themselves age, origin, or failure tests. Judge them alongside water clarity, odor, leakage, pitting, and maker guidance [SRC-017; SRC-019; SRC-023].
- **Stop-use signals:** loose or actively flaking interior material, persistent red/brown water, off odor, deep pitting, a blocked spout, crack, seepage, or movement at a lug/repair needs maker or qualified-restorer assessment before further heating [SRC-015; SRC-017; SRC-019].
- **Repairs:** obtain the repair material, location, date, and repairer if known. A structural patch or recoated interior may affect heat suitability even when cosmetically neat; marks and patina cannot resolve that uncertainty. Suzuki Morihisa treats loose handles, rust, and damaged exterior finish as workshop-assessment/repair matters and limits its service to its own products [SRC-015].

### Marks, age, and provenance

Horie reports that maker marks on Nambu kettles became common around the middle Meiji period, first as freehand surnames and later with workshop names and art names pressed into molds [SRC-009]. The eighteenth-century Kyoto National Museum kettle has no recorded inscription [SRC-005], while a current Oigen page warns that even its own products may lack a stamp depending on model or year [SRC-014]. Therefore:

- a mark can be evidence to research, not proof by itself;
- absence of a mark does not prove age or low quality;
- a famous name, “Japan,” “Nambu,” “old,” or “handmade” needs documentary provenance and object-level examination;
- signatures and repairs fall outside a beginner article's ability to authenticate remotely.

### What the direct kettle study does and does not show

The strongest direct evidence is narrower than common marketing language. In SRC-018, the four tested Nanbu kettles did **not** substantially transform the low-hardness Hiroshima tap water over the first 30 minutes. After 30 minutes the Marunambu examples reached pH 8.35 (lacquered) and 7.76 (unlacquered), and hardness 25.0 and 28.1 mg/L from a starting 19.0 mg/L. The paper's evaporation correction attributed most of that hardness rise to concentration; the two uncoated Nanbu vessels showed only a small excess consistent with some Ca/Mg increase. The uncoated Marunambu kettle's total iron reached at most 0.03 mg/L in the reported comparison.

The two unknown foreign kettles reached pH 9.40 and 9.00, hardness 44.0 and 53.0 mg/L, and bicarbonate 24.6 and 30.4 mg/L after 30 minutes. Distilled-water rinse controls led the authors to propose release of tap-water calcium and magnesium previously adsorbed during rinsing, rather than leaching those elements from the kettle alloy. That is a proposed mechanism for two objects, not evidence that origin or price predicts water behavior. The authors also acknowledge that sequential sampling substantially reduced water volume after 30 minutes [SRC-018].

The matcha experiment found lower amino-acid and tannin extraction with the two foreign-kettle waters than with the four Nanbu-kettle waters. That is a measured extraction result, not a blinded taste result, and not proof that “Nanbu water improves tea.” It compares six individual objects after 30 minutes of heating and uses matcha rather than repeated gongfu infusions [SRC-018].

Residual chlorine dropped to near zero after roughly 20–30 minutes in the Nanbu-kettle curves, whether the interiors were lacquered or not [SRC-018]. Because the study did not include an inert kettle boiled on the same schedule, it cannot separate ordinary heating from an iron-specific mechanism. A separate non-kettle heating study found that heating conditions themselves changed tap-water pH and residual chlorine [SRC-026]. Health Canada's review establishes that Fe(II) can react with oxidants including chlorine, but that adjacent chemistry does not demonstrate that a tetsubin is the cause of chlorine loss in normal tea service [SRC-019]. “Removes chlorine” also collapses free chlorine and chloramine into one claim even though a water utility's disinfectant regime matters.

For gongfu, the useful causal chain is therefore limited: controlled tea evidence connects source-water pH, conductivity, and mineral/ion composition—or whole waters in which those variables covary—to extraction and sensory differences. Residual disinfectant is another covarying source-water factor, and its concentration changes with heating, but the located tea studies do not isolate a disinfectant-to-extraction or taste effect. An individual kettle may change some water variables, but the located evidence does not connect a named tetsubin finish or a stable amount of transferred iron to a repeatable sensory outcome. A practitioner comparison should first hold source water, dose, infusion schedule, and serving temperature constant. If the kettle changes time-to-boil or cooling rate, match temperature at the leaf rather than treating thermal history as taste evidence.

### Water claims by category

- **Demonstrated:** some actual tetsubin can release measurable total iron into water; magnitude depends on the exact kettle, treatment, water, and protocol. SRC-018 measured only a small amount in its tested new Nanbu vessels.
- **Demonstrated:** source-water mineral composition can change extraction, color, turbidity, and sensory perception of tea [SRC-021; SRC-022].
- **Demonstrated:** water chemistry and scale state affect corrosion and iron release from unlined cast iron [SRC-019; SRC-020].
- **Demonstrated:** ordinary heating conditions can change tap-water pH and residual chlorine without establishing a tetsubin-specific cause [SRC-026].
- **Plausible:** a tetsubin's oxide/scale surface and a practitioner's source water can change iron transfer and other ions enough to affect some tea extractions. The mechanism is consistent with the evidence, but kettle-by-kettle gongfu measurements are missing.
- **Plausible but untested:** residual disinfectant chemistry may contribute to differences among source waters, but the located tea studies do not isolate its independent effect on extraction or taste [SRC-021; SRC-026].
- **Reported:** “rounder,” “softer,” “sweeter,” “thicker,” or “less sharp” water and tea. Iwate's promotional page itself uses “it is said” wording [SRC-003]. These descriptors are useful as test prompts, not guaranteed outcomes.
- **Unsupported:** a tetsubin universally lowers measured hardness or literally “softens” water. The direct study's four Nanbu kettles showed small hardness increases, not softening [SRC-018].
- **Unsupported:** iron ions are proven to be the unique reason chlorine disappears during tetsubin boiling. The direct study lacks a non-iron boiling control, and adjacent research demonstrates that heating itself changes residual chlorine [SRC-018; SRC-026].
- **Unsupported:** all tetsubin add a useful, consistent dose of Fe(II). The direct kettle study measured total iron, not species, and adjacent pipe studies cannot supply a kettle dose [SRC-018; SRC-019; SRC-020].
- **Unsupported:** tetsubin as a class retain useful heat longer than matched alternative kettles. The direct study measured heating, not cooling, and no controlled kettle comparison was located.
- **Unsupported:** a tetsubin always improves every tea, or can fix unsuitable source water. Tea research instead shows water effects vary by water and tea [SRC-021; SRC-022].

### A future gongfu test that would resolve more

An adequate test would use the same source water, matched temperature/time/evaporation, an inert-kettle control, multiple identified tetsubin with documented finishes and use histories, blinded randomized tasting, repeated infusions of more than one tea category, and measurements of pH, conductivity, alkalinity, bicarbonate/DIC, hardness, Ca, Mg, chloride/sulfate, disinfectant type and residual, total iron, dissolved iron, and Fe(II)/Fe(III). Kettle mass, working fill, time-to-boil, and cooling curve should be recorded separately so extraction temperature is not confounded with water composition. The current literature located for this dossier does not provide that complete design.

## Preparation, Heating, Care, Rust, and Scale

### First use: maker instructions conflict

There is no single evidence-backed universal conditioning ritual:

- Suzuki Morihisa says to fill about 70%, boil on medium-or-lower heat, and discard once or twice until the water is clear [SRC-015].
- Iwachu says to rinse, fill about 80%, boil on medium heat, discard, and repeat two or three times until clear [SRC-017].
- Oigen recommends a maker-specific scale-building routine: water around 300 mg/L hardness, about 80% full, boil around 20 minutes without the lid, empty/dry, and repeat three times [SRC-016].

The article should therefore say: follow the maker's instructions for that exact finish. For an undocumented used kettle, begin with maker or conservator identification, a leak/condition check, and conservative rinse/boil/discard cycles; do not prescribe aggressive restoration.

The shared conservative preparation core is narrower: inspect the cold body, spout, lugs, handle, and lid; rinse with water without scrubbing the interior; use the maker's working fill; heat moderately with a steam escape; discard the first water until it is clear and without off odor; empty and dry completely. Hard-water pre-scaling is Oigen's product practice, not a universal prerequisite [SRC-015; SRC-016; SRC-017; SRC-025].

### Heating and daily operation

- **Fill:** use the individual maker's working line. The located instructions cluster at roughly 70–80% full, not brimful; Oigen also warns against extremely low volume. This leaves expansion and steam space but is trade guidance, not a universal calibrated line [SRC-015; SRC-017; SRC-025].
- **Heat source:** match the exact model and the appliance. One Oigen kettle accepts gas, cassette flame, wood, charcoal, solid fuel, radiant electric, and coil heat but explicitly rejects IH and ovens; Chobundo lists IH-compatible pieces; small bases may not trigger some IH units [SRC-012; SRC-014; SRC-015]. No fuel or appliance category is universally suitable.
- **Power:** use medium or lower heat unless the exact maker says otherwise. Suzuki Morihisa warns that strong IH vibration may contribute to cracking; Oigen warns that rapid IH heating may deform a base; Iwachu warns against boil-over [SRC-015; SRC-017; SRC-025].
- **Steam path:** leave headspace and heat with the lid removed or offset as instructed so steam can escape. A closed lid can send boiling water through the spout [SRC-014; SRC-017].
- **Handling and pour:** assume the body, lid, fixed handle, knob, and steam are burn hazards even if a hollow handle is advertised as slower to heat. Use a dry heat-resistant mitt, keep the spout away from people, establish lid control before tilting, and pour only at a rate the spout can cut off cleanly [SRC-014].
- **After service:** empty promptly; do not use the kettle to store hot, cold, or merely warm water [SRC-015; SRC-017; SRC-025]. Set it on a stable heat-safe trivet, remove the lid, and let residual heat dry the interior.
- **Dry heating:** a brief gentle reheat is permitted by some makers, while others prefer residual heat alone. Excessive dry-boiling is a documented cause of damage and corrosion trouble [SRC-015; SRC-017; SRC-025]. If the kettle has been dry-boiled, turn off the heat and let it cool naturally. Do **not** add water to a hot empty kettle: Oigen warns of violent steam and cracking from rapid cooling. Once cold, inspect and test cautiously; abnormal water or damage goes to the maker [SRC-025].
- **Storage:** confirm that body and lid are completely dry, keep them in a low-humidity ventilated place, and protect the exterior from cooking oil. Suzuki Morihisa says wrapping is unnecessary; Oigen recommends separately wrapping body and lid in paper for long storage and changing damp paper. This is a real maker difference, so the shared rule is dry, ventilated storage rather than one packaging ritual [SRC-015; SRC-025].

### Cleaning and scale

Workshop guidance consistently says not to scrub the interior, use detergent, or remove a stable scale/oxide film [SRC-015; SRC-017; SRC-025]. The independent technical basis is only partial: public-health pipe evidence shows that high-calcium-carbonate water can form a thinner, hard, adherent scale while low-alkalinity water can form thick, loose corrosion scale [SRC-019]. That makes a protective role plausible, but it does not prove that every white kettle deposit is beneficial.

These are material/condition categories, not color names:

| Condition | What is known | Observable service clues | Conservative action |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Maker-applied or heat-grown oxide** | Horie documents an Fe3O4 film intentionally formed and retained inside one traditional process; Oigen documents a related maker-specific oxidized finish [SRC-009; SRC-011] | Usually adherent and dark, but black color alone cannot identify magnetite because lacquer, enamel, resin, mixed oxides, and old deposits can also appear dark | On a documented kettle, leave an intact film alone. Do not scrub to expose bright metal |
| **Mineral scale (*yugaka*)** | Maker sources describe mainly Ca/Mg mineral deposits; pipe evidence shows mineral and corrosion phases can intergrow and their protectiveness varies with water [SRC-016; SRC-019; SRC-025] | Often pale/white and adherent. Loose pale fragments can also be shed when layers grow; color alone is not composition | Leave an adherent film under maker guidance. Discard a large loose piece and rinse gently as Oigen directs; seek advice if shedding persists or obstructs the spout |
| **Active red/orange corrosion or iron release** | “Red rust” is not one pure compound. Technical sources identify Fe(III) oxides/oxyhydroxides and particulates in corrosion scales, while dissolved Fe(II) can oxidize and precipitate [SRC-019; SRC-020] | New or spreading powdery orange/red material, red-brown water, loose particles, metallic/off water, or progressive pitting is more concerning than an isolated stable stain | Stop routine tea service when water is persistently colored/off or material is loose; use only maker-approved gentle treatment or professional assessment |
| **Structural or coating failure** | Cracks, seepage, loose handle lugs, unstable repairs, and failing/unknown coatings are not answered by rust chemistry | Wet exterior seam, leak under a cold test, moving handle/lug, deep cavity, lifting coating, or undocumented interior repair | Do not heat or pour. Return/reject for working use or obtain maker/restorer assessment |

Health Canada's drinking-water aesthetic objective for total iron is ≤0.1 mg/L; ferric iron can be visibly reddish-brown around 0.3 mg/L and particulates can be noticed lower [SRC-019]. Those values explain why clear-looking water does not prove zero iron and colored water is a useful warning; they are **not** a home-photo safety threshold for kettles.

A photograph cannot establish whether a dark layer is magnetite, lacquer, resin, enamel, mixed corrosion products, or mineral scale; it also cannot leak-test a base, measure iron in water, or reveal an unstable repair. Remote triage may identify obvious rejection signs, but it cannot declare a kettle safe.

Avoid routine acid descaling in the article. It may strip oxide and scale, alter lacquer/coatings, and expose active metal; no maker-independent tetsubin protocol was located. Descaling should be maker-specific.

### Rust treatment

Iwachu describes simmering used green tea so tannins react with iron/corrosion products and darken the affected surface [SRC-017]. Oigen gives a different, longer routine: simmer tea residue for about 20 minutes, top up and leave it about half a day, then repeat if red areas remain [SRC-025]. These are documented product practices and chemically plausible, but this search did not locate a controlled tetsubin study that quantified durability or cross-finish suitability. Darkening is not proof that structural corrosion has stopped.

Use this escalation ladder:

1. **Continue and monitor:** documented finish intact; only stable adherent oxide/scale or isolated non-powdery spots; no crack, seepage, loose material, obstruction, or persistent change in boiled water.
2. **Gentle maker-approved care:** cool-water rinse, exterior dry wipe, removal of a loose large scale fragment without scraping, or the exact maker's tannin routine. Never generalize one workshop's recipe to an unknown coating.
3. **Professional assessment:** repeated colored/off water after maker-approved conditioning, spreading powdery corrosion, persistent scale shedding, deep pitting, unknown coating/repair, loose handle/lug, suspected crack, or any seepage.
4. **Reject or retire from heating:** cracked or leaking body, unstable load-bearing handle, flaking unknown interior coating, untraceable water-contact repair, or a condition the maker/restorer will not approve for service.

## Buying Framework

Use this order; it prioritizes serviceability over labels.

1. **Intended job:** boiling water for gongfu service, display/collection, or tea infusion. Do not buy an enamelled iron teapot for stovetop boiling [SRC-011; SRC-013].
2. **Exact interior:** obtain clear photographs and written confirmation of enamel, oxide, lacquer, resin, or other treatment. “Unlined” in a listing is not enough [SRC-011; SRC-014].
3. **Heat source:** verify gas, charcoal, radiant electric, and IH compatibility at model level. Regional identity does not answer this [SRC-012; SRC-014; SRC-015].
4. **Usable capacity and full weight:** size to actual gongfu cadence, not nominal maximum. Compare the maker's working fill with kettle volume, add roughly one kilogram per liter of water to the empty weight, and decide whether that mass can be lifted, stopped, and returned to the trivet repeatedly. Too much reserve can mean stale warm water and awkward pours; too little can encourage extremely low fills or frequent reheating.
5. **Condition:** cracks, seepage, unstable repairs, deep pitting, persistent colored water, or unknown coatings outrank cosmetic patina. Request a seller-authorized leak test for a working purchase [SRC-017].
6. **Handle, lid, and pour:** secure lugs, no handle play, balance at the intended fill, hand clearance, steam venting, lid control, unobstructed spout, predictable stream, and clean cutoff. A non-folding handle affects filling and storage. A seller-authorized cold-water pour test is more useful than judging spout shape from a photograph [SRC-014; SRC-023].
7. **Wall and construction:** compare wall thickness and empty mass relative to capacity. Thin/light casting is a documented Yamagata aim, not a universal quality grade [SRC-004; SRC-012]. Thicker walls usually add vessel mass and may alter heat-up/cooling and pour fatigue, but no located study validates a best thickness or superior tea result. Check uniformity, base stability, and serviceability rather than ranking by thinness alone.
8. **Finish and disclosed method:** ask what mold system, interior treatment, exterior finish, lid material, and handle construction were used. Accept legitimate variation rather than searching for one “correct” recipe [SRC-009; SRC-010; SRC-011; SRC-012; SRC-014].
9. **Provenance:** maker invoice, box/paperwork, prior-owner history, and object-level comparison are stronger than a single mark. Marks alone neither date nor authenticate [SRC-005; SRC-009; SRC-014].
10. **Repair and return path:** prefer a maker/seller who states how cracks, loose handles, coatings, and repairs are handled. Suzuki Morihisa and Oigen direct owners to maker assessment, while Suzuki notes it does not repair other workshops' kettles [SRC-015; SRC-025].

Broad, non-price tiers for the later article:

- **Documented working kettle:** maker-identified, heat-intended, serviceable, with known finish and care instructions. This is the baseline functional tier, whether factory-produced or handmade.
- **Craft-focused kettle:** documented hand mold/pattern/finishing work, lower-volume production, and stronger object-level refinement. The premium pays for labor, design, and provenance—not a guaranteed water effect.
- **Artist/collector kettle:** significance rests on authorship, rarity, age, design, or documented history. Suitability for heating must be evaluated separately and may be lower than that of a modern working kettle.

Factors that can legitimately drive price without promising better water include one-use or fired mold work; hand-impressed or relief decoration; thin-wall control; hand-fitted lid and spout; forged or hollow handle construction; urushi and other labor-intensive finishes; low output; documented authorship; awards or exhibition history; rarity/age; provenance; and after-sale repair. Industrial repeatability and a clear warranty can also be valuable. None of these factors is direct evidence of iron release, heat retention, sweetness, or “best tea” performance [SRC-009; SRC-010; SRC-012; SRC-014; SRC-018].

Red flags: “tetsubin” used for an enamelled teapot; no interior photograph; promises of treating iron deficiency; guaranteed sweeter tea; “ancient” or “handmade” supported only by a stamp; seller refusal to discuss leakage, repairs, or lining; and current product claims treated as independent science.

## Disputed or Unsupported Claims

| Claim encountered | Evidence status | Why it is disputed or unsupported | Article handling |
|---|---|---|---|
| “All tetsubin are Nambu ironware.” | Unsupported | Museum records document Kyoto/Takaoka examples and Yamagata is a separate designated tetsubin-producing tradition [SRC-004; SRC-005; SRC-006]. | Correct directly. Nambu is a major regional tradition, not the category definition. |
| “The tetsubin was invented by one named maker in one exact year.” | Unsupported as a settled fact | The earliest appearance is described as unclear; later sources relay regional origin traditions at different levels [SRC-008; SRC-009]. | Use “traditionally attributed” and a broad Edo-period development. |
| “Tetsubin have a documented 400-year history as a vessel form.” | Unsupported as phrased | Seventeenth-century dates refer to Morioka ironworking, chanoyu-kettle making, or individual workshop lineages; institutional sources place Nambu tetsubin development in the eighteenth century [SRC-005; SRC-015; SRC-023; SRC-024]. | Attribute “roughly 400 years” to the regional/workshop tradition, not to the distinct vessel form. |
| “A true tetsubin has completely bare iron inside.” | Unsupported | Documented heat-intended kettles can have an oxide film or interior lacquer; enamel is the critical teapot distinction in the cited modern production accounts [SRC-011; SRC-014]. | Define by boiling purpose and non-enamel interior, then name the actual finish. |
| “Black interior means black-rust/kama-yaki and proves authenticity.” | Unsupported | Black appearance can result from oxide, lacquer, enamel, cashew/resin, or other treatments [SRC-011; SRC-013; SRC-014]. | Require maker documentation; do not authenticate by color. |

| “Tetsubin softens water by lowering hardness.” | Unsupported | The direct kettle study saw small hardness increases in the tested Nanbu kettles and large increases in two unknown foreign kettles [SRC-018]. | Avoid literal “softens.” If used, reserve “soft/round” for reported sensory language. |
| “Iron ions uniquely remove chlorine, which proves why the water tastes better.” | Unsupported | Iron/chlorine reaction is chemically possible, but the tetsubin study lacks an inert boiling control, residual chlorine fell in lacquered and uncoated kettles, and separate research shows heating itself changes the residual. | “Tetsubin water is always richer in absorbable Fe(II).” | Unsupported | Direct kettle research measured total iron only; pipe literature shows speciation varies with oxygen, pH, oxidants, and scale [SRC-018; SRC-019; SRC-020]. | Do not assign a species or dose without measurement. |

| “Every tea tastes sweeter, smoother, or better with tetsubin water.” | Reported, not demonstrated | Government promotion and workshops repeat the observation, while controlled tea-water research shows effects vary by mineral composition and tea type [SRC-003; SRC-021; SRC-022]. | Present as a variable sensory report and propose controlled comparison. |
| “Cast iron guarantees superior heat retention for gongfu service.” | Unsupported as a comparison | The direct kettle study matched heating rates but did not measure cooling, and no matched-material cooling study was located. Fill, total mass, geometry, lid state, and surroundings all matter [SRC-018]. | Compare actual working fill, filled weight, and cooling curve; do not use a material slogan. |
| “White scale is always good; red rust is always harmless.” | Unsupported as an absolute | Scale mineralogy and adhesion vary; visible/particulate iron can indicate active release. Workshop advice is product practice, not universal safety evidence [SRC-017; SRC-019]. | Distinguish stable film from turbidity, flakes, pitting, cracks, and off water. |
| “A clean-looking interior photograph proves a used kettle is safe.” | Unsupported | Color and appearance cannot identify the finish or oxide phase, leak-test the body, measure release, or establish repair stability [SRC-011; SRC-014; SRC-019; SRC-025]. | Use photographs only to find red flags; require provenance and object-level checks. |
| “A stamp proves age, maker, and authenticity.” | Unsupported | Marks became common in a historical period, unsigned museum objects exist, and modern maker examples may be unstamped [SRC-005; SRC-009; SRC-014]. | Treat marks as one provenance clue only. |
| “All traditional kettles are IH-compatible.” | Unsupported | Workshop specifications conflict at product level [SRC-012; SRC-014; SRC-015]. | Verify the exact base/model and use moderate power. |
| “There is one correct first-use ritual.” | Unsupported | Three established workshops give materially different rinse/boil/hard-water procedures [SRC-015; SRC-016; SRC-017]. | Follow exact maker guidance and state the conflict. |

## Conclusions Safe to Use in the Article

- A tetsubin is a cast-iron water-boiling and pouring vessel; it should be distinguished from a commonly enamelled iron teapot that is not intended for direct heat [SRC-005; SRC-006; SRC-011; SRC-013].
- “Unlined” is best explained as non-enamel and heat-intended, not necessarily untreated bare metal [SRC-011; SRC-014].
- Nambu is a major, well-documented tradition with distinct Morioka and Oshu roots, but tetsubin also belong to other Japanese casting histories, including Yamagata and Takaoka [SRC-003; SRC-004; SRC-006; SRC-009].
- The exact first tetsubin and single inventor remain uncertain; a cautious Edo-period development from earlier kettle forms is defensible [SRC-005; SRC-008; SRC-009].
- Seventeenth-century dates describe regional ironworking, chanoyu-kettle production, or workshop lineages; the located institutional and object evidence places the distinct tetsubin form in the eighteenth century. Do not turn “400-year tradition” into a 400-year vessel-form claim [SRC-005; SRC-015; SRC-023; SRC-024].
- Traditional production can involve a fired loam/sand mold, a separate core and accessory molds, hand-impressed patterning, casting, high-temperature oxidation, coloring, and handle fitting [SRC-009; SRC-010].
- In one institutional sequence, cooling is followed by core removal, interior sand removal, seam fettling, oxidation, leak testing, exterior coloring, and handle fitting; other workshops may vary the tools and inspection points [SRC-009; SRC-023].
- Mold methods, finishes, lid materials, handle construction, and interior treatments vary. Name the workshop practice rather than turning it into a universal rule [SRC-010; SRC-011; SRC-012; SRC-014].
- The best direct study found relatively small water changes and small total-iron release in its four tested new Nanbu kettles. Evaporation explained most hardness rise in those objects. It found much larger changes in two unidentified foreign kettles and proposed release of previously adsorbed Ca/Mg, but the small asymmetric sample prevents broad national or quality conclusions [SRC-018].
- Water chemistry affects cast-iron corrosion and release, while controlled tea evidence supports extraction effects from source-water pH, conductivity, and mineral/ion composition. That supports kettle-specific testing, not a guarantee that tetsubin water is better [SRC-019; SRC-020; SRC-021; SRC-022; SRC-027].
- Sensory claims such as “rounder” or “sweeter” should be reported as observations and tested blind against the same water heated in an inert control [SRC-003; SRC-021].
- Do not say a tetsubin analytically softens water, changes full alkalinity based only on bicarbonate, uniquely removes chlorine, always improves tea, provides a consistent Fe(II) dose, or treats iron deficiency [SRC-018; SRC-019; SRC-020; SRC-026].
- Ordinary heating can change pH and residual chlorine, so an inert heated control is essential. Whether residual disinfectant independently changes tea extraction or taste remains untested here. No located evidence supports superior tetsubin heat retention under matched cooling conditions [SRC-018; SRC-021; SRC-026].
- For use and buying, prioritize intended purpose, known interior finish, heat-source compatibility, capacity/full weight, cracks/leaks, secure handle, controlled venting/pour, provenance, and a repair path [SRC-014; SRC-015; SRC-017].
- Follow the exact maker's first-use and care instructions because established workshops disagree. The shared conservative core is roughly 70–80% working fill unless otherwise specified, moderate heat, headspace/steam venting, prompt emptying, complete drying, and no interior scrubbing [SRC-015; SRC-016; SRC-017; SRC-025].
- After accidental dry-boiling, turn off the heat and let the kettle cool naturally; adding water while it is hot risks violent steam and rapid-cooling fracture [SRC-025].
- Treat intentional oxide, stable mineral scale, active corrosion, and structural/coating damage as different conditions. Persistent colored/off water, loose material, deep pitting, cracks, seepage, unstable handle attachment, or unknown coatings/repairs require stopping routine use and seeking maker or specialist assessment. A photograph alone cannot establish safety [SRC-009; SRC-011; SRC-017; SRC-019; SRC-025].
- Buying should prioritize intended use, working capacity and filled weight, exact finish, model-level heat compatibility, wall/mass relative to capacity, stable handle/lid, a controlled pour, condition, provenance, and repair path. Craft labor and rarity can explain price but do not prove water performance [SRC-009; SRC-012; SRC-014; SRC-018].
