# Early-Period Teapots, Order versus Chaos

## I. The Chaos in Early-Period Teapots

One question that’s often raised by seasoned tea drinkers and teapot collectors is:

> “Why have the prices of aged pu’er teas skyrocketed, while the prices of similarly aged teapots have not appreciated much in comparison?”

One possible reason is due to the difficulty in grasping and mastering early and antique teapot collecting, given the large number of replicas flooding the market. Over time, rumors have been repeated so often that they seem to become fact. An inextricable mix of real and replica pots on the market has rendered it difficult for newcomers to learn how to collect. Mistakes in judgement can be very expensive, and under such circumstances it is difficult to grasp and master the skills of collecting and appreciating these older teapots. Many vendors have further confused the subject of early-period teapots, making up false stories and legends about such pots in order to profit from them.

There are many early-period teapot collectors, and despite having collected for a very long time, they have always found it difficult to accurately determine the different eras the pots come from. Vendors often sound very convincing with many theories and stories, but in actual fact they often know nothing at all. There are many inconsistencies, such as vendors selling very similar looking pots and yet claiming one from the fifties and the other from the seventies. In another instance, two pots from the same era (‘50s) bought from the same vendor at different times can exhibit significant differences in the workmanship. When buyers take these pots home and examine them, they notice that pots supposedly from the same era are very different sometimes. Why?

Perhaps you might have encountered such situations yourself. This is what we refer to as the **“chaos”** in early-period teapot collecting. The origin of chaos is not in the teapot, but in people.

Before the advent of the Internet, all sorts of information about teapots was transmitted by word of mouth. As such, sellers of counterfeit pots could very easily create stories to dupe consumers, and consumers that bought these counterfeits had no way of validating their authenticity. They could only believe the items to be genuine, and, upon discovering the truth, they could either resign themselves to their fate, or continue to delude themselves.

The aim of this book is not to pass any judgement on which teapots are authentic (or not), but to clarify and present a system on what these early-period teapots really are, and set out some reasonable parameters to identify and validate them. To build a system upon which these teapots can be appreciated and collected is of paramount importance.

In fact, understanding these pots is not as difficult as many have made it out to be. If we start to learn about the historical background and conditions of production at the time, combined with the opportunity to see some authentic pots in person, it’s possible to achieve an over **eighty percent hit rate** with early-period tea pots.

## II. The special traits of teapots produced under different conditions during different eras

For a start, we would like to define **“early-period teapots”** as the pots made between **1950s to the end of 1970s** (different people may have different definitions).

- Yixing pots made before 1940s are considered to be early ROC era, or even “late Qing”; in this text, also known as the **“Qing-ROC period.”**
- After the 1980s, with the liberation of designs and artistic expression after the end of the Cultural Revolution, there was a huge increase in the use of personal name seals in teapot making, and the appearance of private Yixing workshops complicates matters.
- Pots from this period onwards will not be discussed much in this book, but rather, in another separate volume.

### Historical background

Yixing Factory #1 (宜興一廠; *yíxīng yī chǎng*) was established in **1958**. In the ‘50s, the communist government had just secured control, and communist ideals were strongest during that period. During that period, all agriculture and industries belonged to the state, and the sentiments then was to put the state and people above all, and the idea of “individuality” was not permitted.

Under such circumstances, everything was made under the principles of:

- **uniformity**
- **mass production**
- **evenness**
- **collectivity**

Teas and teapots produced during this period also fell under the same principles.

Today, when we drink the “label” pu’er teas from these eras, it is possible to assess the leaf quality — is it very refined and made from very delicate, fine tea buds, and from pure material, or is it mass-produced, with a blend of big and small leaves, and with some stems mixed in? In the same way we can apply the same principles when assessing teapots from these eras.

Would we expect delicate workmanship, famous private makers, special limited batches, designated rare clays or personal orders? During the communist era, everything belonged to the state; there was no personal wealth. As long as production standards and quotas were met, there would be food to eat, nor would personal name seals be used on these pots.

### Defining characteristics for assessment

As a result, teapots made during the “early-period teapot” era have a few defining characteristics that can be used for assessment:

1. **Clay**  
   Yixing clay mining, processing and aging was done on a large scale, primarily by one entity, the Yixing Mining Company (宜興礦業公司; *yíxīng kuàngyè gōngsī*). This is different from modern day, where potters typically age and process their own clays.  
   The clay used by potters to make teapots back then was all from the same uniform batches produced by Yixing Mining Company; there was no private, small scale clay processing and aging. Therefore, the uniformity of the raw materials used during this time period is one important aspect to be considered when dating a teapot.

2. **Workmanship**  
   In early-period teapot production, pots were not made in isolation, but rather, by a group of workers in large workshops. Everyone used the same type of molds, workflow, touching up methods and tools, resulting in very uniform, and generally almost-identical products.  
   Some small quirks and habits of certain potters may be observed by looking at the finer details, and variations existed because of skill or personal preferences, but in general, pots produced were very uniform in overall characteristics. It would be a mistake to assess a pot of this era on finer details, such as the size of the knob of the teapot.  
   In the early days (from the ‘50s to ‘60s), teapots were touched up and detailed mostly by hand, and towards the later years, more modern and advanced tools were used, so that by the ‘80s, these “hand-made” traits are almost impossible to find.

3. **Overall shape**  
   As with workmanship, Factory #1 had standardized the size of pots and their shapes/appearances by using the same molds throughout. Since different molds were used during different eras, we should primarily focus on the shape of the pot and differences in workflow, rather than smaller details.

4. **Firing temperature**  
   During these early eras, pots were fired in large batches all at once in the kiln. The Dragon Kilns (龍窯; *lóngyáo*) used before the 1960s were large, firing temperature was difficult to control, and uneven temperatures were common. Over or under-fired products were common, especially in the early 1940s.  
   In later years, with better technology and better kiln designs, under or over firing was significantly reduced. The Dragon Kilns were slow to warm up and slow to cool down, producing a characteristic appearance in the fired pots, as compared to later years when oil or gas kilns were used.
