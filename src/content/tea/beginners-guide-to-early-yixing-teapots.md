One of the most common questions beginners ask is: “Is my teapot authentic factory work?”
While the world of Yixing teapots can feel overwhelming at first, dating a teapot becomes much easier once you 
understand a few essential steps. This guide breaks down a simple, structured method to help you evaluate early 
factory-period Yixing teapots more confidently.

#### 1. Start by Identifying the Possible Era

Before judging authenticity, you must first ask:
“Which era does this teapot belong to?”

The First Yixing Zisha Factory operated for roughly forty years. Over those decades, clay, workmanship, forms, firing techniques, and seal marks changed noticeably. Without knowing the approximate era of the piece in your hand, it’s impossible to compare it accurately.

Consider an example:

The form looks like the 1980s

The base mark resembles the 1960s

The clay body feels like the 1990s

Can such a teapot be factory-made? No.
Authentic features from different periods don’t appear together on the same piece.

If you can’t yet recognize the eras, don’t rush to buy. Instead, spend time reading, studying reference materials, and 
looking at verified examples. A solid foundation will save you many costly mistakes.

#### 2. Once the Era Is Identified, Study That Era’s Characteristics

Each period has its own “fingerprint.”
To evaluate a teapot, study the period’s:

Clay (texture, color, inclusions)

Craftsmanship (tool marks, hand vs. mold work)

Form (proportions, construction)

Seal marks (stamps, layout, carving style)

Firing characteristics (kiln signatures)

Nothing replaces hands-on experience, but if that isn’t possible, rely on high-quality archival references, comparison 
photos, and specialized guides such as the Early Yixing Teapot Encyclopedia.

This is not guesswork — it’s a process of scientific comparison.

For instance, why do we determine that the “China Yixing” (4:6-character) mark predates the 37-character version?

Two key reasons:

Authentic “Tingji” teapots appear only with the six-character mark (1960s) and the 4:6-character “China Yixing” mark — 
never with the 3:7-character mark.

Some 4:6-character teapots share identical molds with six-character pieces, while no such connection exists with 
3:7-character examples.

This is deduction based on observable evidence, not hearsay.

#### 3. A Correct Teapot Should Match in Clay, Craftsmanship, Form, Seal, and Firing

If all the features of the teapot align with the known characteristics of the era, then the piece is likely correct.

For beginners, this is especially important:
Only buy teapots where every major element is consistent with its supposed era.

Avoid pieces with even one or two questionable features, no matter how tempting the price may be.
A skilled faker can imitate a single trait — but matching multiple features across different dimensions is extremely 
difficult.

In collecting Yixing, buying better is more important than buying more.

#### 4. Use Your Teapot—Don’t Lock It Away

After acquiring an authentic factory-period teapot, use it often.
Examine it, handle it, brew with it. Familiarity builds an intuitive understanding of clay, structure, and workmanship 
that photos can never provide.

Resist the urge to “enshrine” it inside a silk box and only take it out on special occasions.
Real learning comes from living with the piece.

Also compare it frequently with non-factory or modern pieces.
With time, the differences will become obvious and effortless to spot.

#### Final Thoughts

These steps come from observing countless questions and common mistakes among new collectors.
With patience, study, and hands-on experience, you can build a strong foundation and truly enjoy the journey into the 
world of early Yixing teapots.

Happy brewing — and even happier collecting!
