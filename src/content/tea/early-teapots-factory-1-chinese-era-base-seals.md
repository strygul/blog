### Scope: “Chinese Era” Base Seals

One important category of late-period Factory #1 Yixing teapots that deserves closer attention is the series bearing so-called **“Chinese era”** base seals. Although these pots are often overshadowed by discussions surrounding the [Green Label, White Label, and Laser Label periods](/tea/early-teapots-factory-1-green-white-and-laser-labels/), they occupy a unique place in the history of Factory #1 and provide valuable insight into the factory’s final decades of production.

Beyond the familiar **Zhong Guo Yixing** (中国宜兴) seal, certain pots produced from the late 1980s onward also carried seals denoting the traditional Chinese cyclical calendar, known as the **Heavenly Stems and Earthly Branches** (天干地支). The most commonly encountered example is the **Geng Wu** (庚午) year, corresponding to **1990**.

The earliest known examples of these date-sealed pots appear from the **Ding Mao** (丁卯) year of **1987**, continuing intermittently until **1998**, the final year before Factory #1 ceased operations. Interestingly, these seals were not used continuously every year. Production appears concentrated between the late 1980s and late 1990s, while examples from the early 1990s remain comparatively scarce.

### Major Chinese Era Seals Known Today

* **1987** — Ding Mao (丁卯)

* **1990** — Geng Wu (庚午)

### Hong Kong Merchants and Dual Versions

The production of these pots was closely tied to demand from Hong Kong merchants. By the mid-1980s, much of Factory #1’s output had already been committed to major Hong Kong trading companies such as Jin Feng and several other large distributors. Naturally, the factory accommodated the varying requirements of these merchants. As a result, pots of identical form, clay, and workmanship might exist in two versions: one bearing the standard **Zhong Guo Yixing** seal, and another bearing a Chinese era seal. Both versions coexisted during the same production periods.

Because orders varied from year to year, some years saw the production of Chinese era seal pots while others did not.

### Chinese Era Seal Pots vs. Standard Zhong Guo Yixing Seals

Collectors often ask whether there are any meaningful differences between Chinese era seal pots and regular Zhong Guo Yixing seal pots. In reality, there are no fundamental differences between the two. They were produced in the same factory, by the same artisans, using the same clay stocks, firing methods, and production standards of the period. During this era, Factory #1 did not permit the use of outside clay materials for mass production. Consequently, these pots adhered strictly to Factory #1 specifications and practices.

There was no special use of premium zhuni or unusually high-grade materials specifically for Chinese era seal productions. They were made using standard Factory #1 clays of the time. However, it is important to note that during this period many non–Factory #1 workshops also existed, including Factory 2, Factory 3, Factory 4, and numerous smaller operations supported by Hong Kong merchants. Some of these workshops also produced imitation Chinese era seal pots.

A genuine Factory #1 pot will generally exhibit the consistency and standards expected of Factory #1 workmanship, clay preparation, and finishing. By contrast, pieces produced outside Factory #1 often show noticeable variations in quality when compared side by side.

### Why Collectors Value Chinese Era Seal Pots

Although these were relatively recent mass-produced wares, Chinese era seal pots hold particular importance for collectors for two key reasons.

First, they serve as excellent reference pieces for studying authentic late-period Factory #1 production. Since many of these pots were commissioned directly by Hong Kong merchants, they accurately reflect the clays, moulds, shapes, and workmanship standards of the era. This is especially valuable because Factories 1 through 4 all produced similar shuiping and standard forms during the late 1980s. Authentic Factory #1 Chinese era seal pots therefore provide useful benchmarks for distinguishing Factory #1 work from that of other factories.

Second, these pots possess a strong sense of historical identity. Following the privatization of Factory #1 in 1998, these wares effectively became the final chapter of the state-owned factory era. While standard Zhong Guo Yixing seals can usually only indicate an approximate production period, Chinese era seals allow collectors to pinpoint the exact year of manufacture. Decades from now, these date-marked pieces may prove especially meaningful as historical representatives of the closing years of Factory #1.

### The 1990 Geng Wu Year — Peak Production

Among all Chinese era seal productions, the most famous by far are the **Geng Wu** year pots of **1990**. Although 1990 was not the first year in which Chinese era seals appeared, it was unquestionably the year with the largest production volume and greatest variety.

During the Geng Wu year, Factory #1 produced not only standard red clay wares but also pieces in black clay, blue clay, and other materials. Some featured engravings such as “Please Drink China Oolong Tea,” while others bore inscriptions commissioned by trading companies, including gifts for the China Fujian Province Tea Import & Export Company, Xiamen Branch. These pieces were often decorated with landscape engravings and carried the base seal “Yixing Made, Geng Wu Year.”

However, according to the author’s research and interviews with former Factory #1 workers, only one authentic Geng Wu year base seal was used in 1990 itself. This seal features a dividing line between the words Zhong Guo Yixing and Geng Wu Year Made (see below—the first-batch Geng Wu base seal). During that year, only two sizes were produced: **12-cup** and **8-cup** versions (referred to in Taiwan as 8-cup and 6-cup sizes).

These original 1990 pots were made using fine, smooth red clay and quickly became highly sought after, further amplified by aggressive promotion from tea ware merchants. Their popularity was so great that additional batches of “Geng Wu Year” pots continued to be produced even after 1990, extending into **1995** and beyond. Later productions appeared in a wider range of forms and seals, and some were outsourced to non–Factory #1 workshops, resulting in pieces that often fail to match the quality and character of genuine Factory #1 wares.

#### Laser labels on Geng Wu pots

One interesting inconsistency sometimes encountered in the market is the appearance of **Laser Labels** on Geng Wu year pots. This creates an obvious chronological conflict. Since the Geng Wu year corresponds to 1990, such pots should belong to the **White Label** era (1987–1992). Therefore, White Labels are entirely plausible. Laser Labels, however, only appeared after **1993**.

How then can a 1990 Geng Wu pot bear a Laser Label?

Some dealers claim these pots were produced in 1990 but remained in Yixing storage until export in 1993, thus receiving Laser Labels at that later date. This explanation is difficult to accept, given the immense popularity of Geng Wu pots at the time and the improbability of significant unsold inventory remaining for years. A more reasonable conclusion is that many Laser Label Geng Wu pots were simply later reproductions produced after 1993.

Indeed, the popularity of Geng Wu pots likely explains why comparatively few **Xin Wei** (1991), **Ren Shen** (1992), and **Gui You** (1993) year-sealed pots are seen today. Rather than creating new annual seal series, merchants and workshops continued reproducing the commercially successful Geng Wu seal.

---

### Distinguishing Authentic 1990 Geng Wu Pots

How then can collectors distinguish authentic 1990 Geng Wu pots from later reproductions?

Beyond evaluating shape, clay, and workmanship, the base seal and label provide important clues. Genuine early Geng Wu seals feature the distinctive dividing line mentioned earlier. When combined with the correct characteristics of early 1990s Factory #1 clay and form, authenticity becomes more convincing. **White Labels** suggest production before 1992, while **Laser Labels** strongly indicate production after 1993.

Fortunately, reproductions of other Chinese era seal years are relatively uncommon. Since most of these pots remain affordable, there has been little incentive for extensive counterfeiting. Collectors familiar with the clays, moulding techniques, and workmanship standards of the era can usually identify authentic examples without too much difficulty.

---

### Accessibility and Value Today

Today, Chinese era seal pots remain reasonably accessible in price, while still offering respectable clay quality and historical significance. For collectors interested in entering the world of Factory #1 wares, they represent an appealing and educational category. As some of the final productions before the closure and privatization of Factory #1, they also carry undeniable historical and collectible value.

---

### The 1998 Wu Yin Year — Final Batch

Finally, among the Chinese era seals, the **Wu Yin** (戊寅) year pots of **1998** deserve special mention. Although most people consider Factory #1 to have effectively ended in 1997, these 1998 pots—judging from their packaging, Laser Labels, clay, workmanship, and overall style—are unmistakably genuine Factory #1 productions.

The author believes these Wu Yin pots represent the final batch produced before Factory #1’s privatization, making them a fitting conclusion to the state-owned factory era and a lasting symbol of the end of one of Yixing’s most important periods in history.
