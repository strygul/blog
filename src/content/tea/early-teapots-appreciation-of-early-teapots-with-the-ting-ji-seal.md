Collectors of early Yixing teapots will be familiar with the “Ting Ji” seal. It does not denote a specific form, but any pot bearing the small “Ting Ji” mark stamped inside the base. The seal is approximately 2.5 mm per character and appears as a raised (positive) impression. Its clarity varies.

The mark originates from the dingzhu—a protruding support used during sealing. When the pot is inverted for application of the external base seal, this rod supports the interior to prevent deformation. Because “Ting Ji” is carved onto the dingzhu, pressure from the external stamping transfers the mark into the inner base. As a result, placement may be off-center or uneven.

### Dating and External Base Seals

“Ting Ji” pots appear from the 1960s to the mid-1970s. Dating is confirmed through external base seals, which fall into two categories:

- Standard 1960s six-character seals (e.g., “Jing Xin Hui Meng Chen,” “Jing Xi Nan Meng Chen,” “Yixing Hui Meng Chen”)
- The 1960s–70s 6/4 “Zhong Guo Yixing” seal

### Forms, Materials, and Workmanship

While the shuiping form is most common, other shapes—such as xiaoyao and bianyuan—also bear the mark. Both red and purple clay were used, though red clay predominates; purple clay examples are rarer. Across forms and materials, these pots share consistently high workmanship. Compared to contemporaries, they are often among the most refined Factory-era pieces, with precise finishing and minimal flaws. This quality contributed to their early popularity among collectors.

### Attribution

Attribution is difficult, as authorship was rarely marked during this period. Based on research, the seal is likely associated with Factory 1 artisan Wang Tingmei. Some examples bear “Ding Mei” inside the lid and “Ting Ji” inside the base. “Ding Mei” is believed to be Wang Tingmei’s childhood name, confirmed through sources in Yixing.

### Identification

“Ting Ji” pots entered the Taiwan market early and became widely discussed due to their distinctive seal and superior craftsmanship. This popularity led to extensive forgery. Many replicas circulate, often at inflated prices. The following criteria help with identification:

1. Period consistency
All genuine “Ting Ji” pots date to the 1960s–mid-1970s and must match the materials, workmanship, and standards of that era:

- Six-character seals should align with 1960s production
- 6/4 “Zhong Guo Yixing” seals should align with late 60s–70s characteristics

If a pot does not conform to its supposed period, it can be dismissed.

2. External base seal types
Only two seal types exist: the 1960s six-character seal or the 6/4 “Zhong Guo Yixing” seal:

- The 7/3 “Zhong Guo Yixing” seal appears only in the mid-1970s
- By that time, “Ting Ji” marks were no longer used

Therefore, no authentic “Ting Ji” pot carries a 7/3 seal.

3. Seal consistency
There is only one authentic “Ting Ji” seal. Many replicas show incorrect character structure. High-quality fakes may replicate the seal closely, making comparison with verified examples essential (see Fig. 2).

Before the 1970s, the “Ting Ji” mark appears only inside the base. Genuine examples show superior finishing and reflect the standards of their time. As early recognition drove demand, replicas became widespread. Careful comparison—of clay, workmanship, and form—remains essential.

Handling more authentic pieces is the most reliable way to understand the qualities that define early Yixing teapots.
