# Why Early Yixing Teapots Need Proper Cleaning

Yixing purple clay is known for its unique dual-porosity structure. While this characteristic enhances tea brewing, it 
also makes the clay especially prone to absorbing tea residue over long-term use. Over time, these deposits can clog the 
pores of the clay body. Because tea residue is organic matter, it may eventually mold, affecting not only the flavor and
performance of the tea but also potentially raising hygiene and health concerns. For these reasons, regular and 
appropriate cleaning is essential.

Most collectors understand that the clay body is the most critical factor when evaluating early Yixing teapots. Yet in 
practice, many old pots—both inside and out—are heavily coated with tea oil and stains. When a teapot is covered in 
residue, it becomes extremely difficult to properly observe the clay itself. Therefore, whether buying or selling an 
early teapot, it is best to clean it thoroughly first so the clay body can be clearly examined and accurately 
identified.

A common misconception is that an old teapot must look dirty. In reality, even a teapot that is over a hundred years old
can remain clean and hygienic if it has been well cared for. Age is never determined by how dirty a pot appears. On the 
contrary, the dirtier a teapot is, the harder it becomes to assess its true qualities. Teapots are evaluated based on 
clay, workmanship, form, seal, and kiln characteristics—never by whether they look “dirty.”

---

## How Should We Clean an Early Yixing Teapot?

Cleaning methods can be divided into three levels:

- If **Level 1** works, there is no need to proceed further.
- If **Level 2** works, **Level 3** is unnecessary.
- If even **Level 3** fails, my personal recommendation is to give up the pot—or not purchase it at all. Methods beyond 
- this point involve professional-level procedures that are generally not worth the risk or effort for an ordinary collector.

---

## Level 1: Basic Cleaning

### Tools
- Yellow Scotch-Brite sponge (**do not use the green one**)
- Mild dish detergent
- Small brush for the spout (eyelash brush or straw brush; eyelash brushes work best)

### Method
Soak the teapot in clean water for a full day. Then wash both the interior and exterior with mild dish detergent, using 
the sponge and brush as needed. If the pot is not heavily stained, this method is usually sufficient.

### A Note on Boiling and Barium Additives
For brand-new, unused teapots made after the 1980s—whether factory or non-factory wares, floral designs or standard 
forms—many clays contained barium carbonate (BaCO₃), as noted in scientific research. Because barium ions are soluble 
in acid and harmful to the human body, it is recommended to boil these newer teapots during initial preparation to help 
reduce (though not completely eliminate) barium residue.

For teapots made before the 1970s, barium-related concerns are generally not present. In such cases, boiling is 
optional, and thorough cleaning is sufficient.

---

## Level 2: Advanced but Safe Cleaning

### Tools
- Melamine sponge (“magic sponge” or nano sponge)
- Baking soda (sodium bicarbonate, NaHCO₃)
- Citric acid

If dark tea stains remain after Level 1 cleaning, a melamine sponge is highly recommended—it is significantly more 
effective than a standard sponge.

For white mineral deposits (typically calcium carbonate), soak the teapot in hot water (preferably purified water) with 
citric acid for **3–7 days**, depending on severity.

If stubborn tea stains persist, soak the teapot in hot water with baking soda for **2–7 days**. After soaking, repeat 
the Level 1 cleaning process using the melamine sponge.

Other cleaning agents—such as denture-cleaning tablets or certain enzymatic cleaners—function similarly and are also 
considered Level 2 tools. Some of these produce hydrogen peroxide during use. Since hydrogen peroxide is water-soluble 
and unstable, thorough rinsing with clean water is sufficient; no special detox procedures are necessary.

> ⚠️ **Important Warning**  
> Do **not** mix cleaning agents. Baking soda, citric acid, denture tablets, enzymatic cleaners, and similar products 
> must be used **one at a time** to avoid unexpected chemical reactions.

---

## Level 3: Strong Alkaline Cleaning (Use with Caution)

### Tool
- Washing soda (sodium carbonate, Na₂CO₃), commonly used in making alkaline rice dumplings

Washing soda is far more alkaline than baking soda and must be handled with care. Store it out of reach of children and 
**never** keep it in the refrigerator to prevent accidental misuse.

### Method
Dissolve **1 teaspoon** of washing soda in **300–500 ml** of water. Soak the teapot for **1–3 days**, then clean it 
using Level 1 tools. This method is significantly more powerful than baking soda.

After treatment, rinse the teapot thoroughly with clean water and preferably soak it in fresh water for several days 
before use. Always wear gloves and prioritize safety.

---

## Beyond Level 3

If all three levels fail, other options—such as chemical treatments or re-firing—do exist. However, these methods are 
labor-intensive and may pose risks to both personal health and the teapot itself. In most cases, they are simply not 
worth pursuing.

---

## Daily Maintenance

The guiding principle of teapot care is simple:

**Clean the teapot on the same day it is used.**

After removing the tea leaves, rinse the pot inside and out with hot water, then wipe it with a clean dry or slightly 
damp cloth. That is all it takes.

With proper daily care, even Qing dynasty teapots can remain clean, hygienic, and beautifully maintained. Whether new or
old, a well-kept teapot can be both elegant and clean.
