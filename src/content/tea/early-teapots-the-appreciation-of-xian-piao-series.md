## Xian Piao (线漂): From Late Qing Classics to Factory #1 and Taiwan’s “Ba Le”

It was extremely difficult to trace the origin of the name “Xian Piao”, we only know that when Factory #1 was established at the end of the 1950s, there were pots made in this shape, with characters ”线漂“（Xian Piao） stamped inside of the lids.

According to precious research, we believe that names of earlier teapots generally follow the name of ancient pots, such as the name of “Tang Po”, in the famous Desaru Shipwreck there were pots with “Tang Po” characters stamped on the base. So we may be able to assert that F1 was giving names to the various pot shapes no without reason, and instead they much more likely explore older names and older pot shapes.

In Factory #1’s “Xian Piao” teapots was only made in the early period of F1 and had two characters ”线漂“ (Xian Piao) stamped inside of the lid. In later period, onwards mid 60s, the "Xian Piao" seal inside the lid never appeared again.

### The 1970s Taiwan Renaming: “Ba Le” (Guava)

In the 1970s, when Yixing Pots started to enter Taiwanese market, the Taiwanese people didn’t know the original pot names given by Factory #1, so they named the pots according to shape, and since the shape was very similar to the commonly seen Taiwanese fruit - Guava, the pot was named “Ba Le”, it means guava in Taiwanese dialect.

This pot shape became one of the Five Shape Pots, and in the early days if you call it “Xian Piao”, no one will know what you are meaning, but if you call it Ba Le, everyone will understand.

---

### A Shape With a Long History

In fact, “Xian Piao” have a very long history, the shape have wide bottom that tapering to the top, low center of gravity which gives it stability, easy to handle and efficiency in concentrating tea fragrance.

So that, we can say that this pot fulfils many tea brewing criterias, from the workmanship to the physics and over all shape and curvature are unique and beautiful.

This pot shape in the end of the Qing Dynasty was a rather common and mature shape, doesn’t matter it was made in branded workshop like Fu Ji or other small workshops, refined, delicate and mature products can be found.

---

### Collecting Note: How to Judge Authenticity vs Replicas

On the market there are many replicas of Mo Yuan Zhai and Fu Ji pots, and some of these replicas are shockingly expensive.

To determine if pot can be dated to the end Qing to Roc era, firstly should start from the clay material, than workmanship, seal, and use 3 aspects together to inspect the pots.

As to clay material aspect, just like we had mentioned ealrier, before use the pots should have a flat matte appearance and shouldn’t have that glazed glow, in end Qing high-end Zuni pots are very delicate and thin, workmanship is detailed, and the overall detailing are all handmade.

Most of the replicas today cannot reach the same thinness or the same handmade workmanship.

The best way is to handle the authentic item personally, this will greatly reduce the chance of making mistakes in the future.

### Factory #1 “Xian Piao”: Early Period to the End

After we finished study the end Qing to ROC “Xian Piao”, we should now look at the “Xian Piaos” made by Factory #1 in early period.

The “Xian Piao” pots, as described earlier, is beautiful shape and it is comfortable for brewing tea, this pot shape was continually made by Factroy #1 all the way from beginning to the ending in 1998.

There are variations of the “Xian Piao” shape but all of them adhere to the characteristics of particular eras they are made.

---

### Earliest Factory #1 (Up to Early 60s)

Let us talk about the earliest “Xian Piao” of F1 up to the early 60s, during this period, most of the “Xian Piao” are 6 cup, and some of them were 8 cups (by Chinese cup size, not Taiwanese). Other sizes were never have been encountered/made.

As to “Xian Piaos” of this period, fraction of them will have Xian Piao stamped inside of the lid, and even fewer have both Six Cup and “Xian Piao” seals.

The base seals of that era would definitely be the 60s standard 6 character seals like Jing Xi Hui Meng Cheng Zhi, Yixing Hui Meng Cheng Zhi, Jing Xi Nan Meng Chen Zhi.

Amongst these seals, we can find 4 Leg Xi, Big Xi, Nan Meng Chen, Yixing Hui, but can’t find Small Xi.

This is probably according to the later appearance of Small Xi seal, relative to the other 6 character seals.

As for overall pot shape and curvature, the 60s “Xian Piao” are the most similar to the end Qing to ROC ones, except that the lid is much more flatter, whilst the lids of end Qing to ROC are puffier and half spherical.

8 Cup “Xian Piao” vs 6 Cup, the 8 cup is rounder, whilst the 6 cup has a sharper decline in contour, this leading to “slimmer” appearance.

The clay material used in this era is typical to the red clay used in the 60s, this material is regarded as one of the best red clays in Yixing history (zhuni aside).

Even the red clay of Qing dynasty didn’t have the same density and quality.

The modern red clay today, in terms of clay quality or effect on tea, isn’t a patch on.

For beginners to practice recognition of clay from this era, it is important to clean the pot and rid from tea stains, so that he can see the density and texture, the warm and not glaring matte surface.

After usage over a period of time, the pot seasons and starts to glow, makes it difficult for the beginner to recognize.

### 1970s: Seals, Fine Differences, and NZWH
<figure class="figure-center">
  <img src="/tea/posts/early-teapots-the-appreciation-of-xian-piao-series/70s.png" alt="Xian Piao, 1970s" loading="lazy" decoding="async" />
  <figcaption>Xian Piao, 1970s</figcaption>
</figure>

“Xian Piaos” up to 1970s, there weren’t any big changes in the overall pot shape, if you compare a 60s and 70s pot you will see that the overall shape is nearly the same, but in the finer details there are some differences, like there are much lesser hand-correcting workmanship signs inside of the lid of the 70s pots, the air hole may be bigger.

But the biggest difference is, of course, the base seal, in the 70s, the main seals was always Zhong Guo Yixing, there was 6/4 seal, 7/3 seal and 5/5 seal.

At the end 70s, there are Nei Zi Wai Hong (inner purple - outer red) pots.

NZWH refers to the pot being made of purple clay but drizzled on the outside with a fine red clay.

Some are coated from each side - inside and outside.

Due to the scarcity and the high price of red clay, usage of NZWH methods will help to reduce costs.

One point worthy to mention is that the red clay coated on the outside of the pots are extremely finely ground, so that these pots sometimes looks more beautiful than pure red clay pots after brewing.

The NZWH methods continued into the 80s until the maturing of the clay doping methods, e.g. clay can now be colored in any ways, different degrees of redness can be achieved, and there was no longer any need to produce NZWH pots anymore.

### Late 70s to 80s: Green Label Era

In the 1970s, apart from the Zhong Guo Yixing seals, in the end of 70s to early 80s, during the green label era also used Jing Xi Hui Meng Chen 6 character seals, but the style of the seal is different, the characters are not prim and proper, but cursive.

There were also some producer which retained the Zhong Guo yixing seal in the 80s, the most obvious difference between the 70s and 80s is that the moulds used into the 80s is different, the pot lid became puffier, the rim of the lids became thicker, the lid walls became thicker, the overall pot appears coarser, but as long as they was made from correct red clay or zisha, they still be extremely good for tea.

---

### Conclusion

In conclusion, “Xian Piao” pots from the olden days till now, there has been continuity in shape amongst the early tea pots, these pots are well loved for easy handling.

No matter which era the “Xian Piao” comes from, they each have good collectability and value, each era having their own characteristics such as clay material, mould, according to their period of manufacturing.

In purchasing do not rush into it, you should listen more, observe more, can make side by side comparisons to see the difference, and if you know or understand the pot, you can purchase it, if you don’t know how to distinguish – skip it!

Don’t listen stories by the vendors, whether they tell you that they collected this twenty or thirty years ago, whether it came out from an ancient warehouse or left after their grandfathers etc. These stories are not important.
