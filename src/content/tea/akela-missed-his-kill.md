I recently got my hands on a teapot that was supposed to be from the late Qing / ROC period, only to discover that it 
was — however well-made — a modern replica. It came from a trusted seller, from whom I had already bought several 
genuine Qing teapots, and my guard was low. I didn't spend enough time investigating the provided images or asking 
questions, and instead relied on his good word that he believed it to be an old ROC piece. I can't really blame him 
either: the replica was of superb quality, and only an experienced expert could tell the difference.

When I unwrapped the teapot, I had a feeling that the clay was off. It didn't have the same color and texture as my 
other late Qing and ROC teapots. But the delicate craftsmanship, the traces of fully hand-made work, and the 
hand-inscribed mark all left me confused, and I ended up asking the community for help.

Only by collecting information piece by piece — from kind people who responded to my posts on Facebook, Reddit, and 
Discord groups, and from reaching out to a few authoritative figures in the field — could I slowly construct a picture 
of the teapot's true origin. Along the way, I learned a great deal about the differences between Qing and modern 
teapots, the hard way.

Even experienced collectors get fooled. The technological progress of the last few decades allows replicas to be made 
that are almost indistinguishable from the originals. The better the replica, the harder it is to uncover. And in most 
cases, you need more than one clue to identify a teapot correctly. So what should you actually look for? Let's discover
it on the example of the two teapots that I have: an authentic Qing teapot, and the replica I just mentioned.

## A Note on Origin: Thai Export Pots

Before going further, a word on what kind of pots are in the scope of this post. 

Both teapots come from Thailand, and both belong to the 
tradition of polished export Yixing — pots made in Yixing specifically for the Southeast Asian market, primarily 
Thailand, from the 19th century through the early 20th. These pots were typically fitted with brass mounts (handle, lid 
rim, spout tip) by Thai craftsmen after arrival, and the clay surface was polished to a smooth, almost soapstone-like 
sheen — quite different from the matte, "raw" finish you see on most domestic Chinese Yixing.

For collectors, this is an important sub-category. The brass fittings on the large Qing pot are not a later addition 
meant to fake age — they are part of the pot's original identity. The polishing, too, is not surface wear or accidental 
shine: it was a deliberate finishing step, done in Yixing or Thailand depending on the workshop, to suit the aesthetic 
preferences of the Thai-Chinese tea-drinking community.

This makes the comparison between the two pots even more useful, because we are comparing like with like — both are 
Thai-export polished pots, separated only by the gap of a century or so of craftsmanship.

## Clay Speaks for Itself

Clay should be the first thing to look at, as it is the hardest dimension of a teapot to fake or imitate convincingly. 
An experienced and trained eye can often tell the difference just from pictures. My own limited experience with Qing 
teapots only allows me to do so reliably when I have the pots in my hands.

It is hard to explain in words exactly what to look for when comparing clays — so much of it is a visual and tactile 
experience, the kind of thing the texture gives away the moment you hold it. But I will still try to explain how I 
experience old clays, using the two pots below as examples.

On the right is the large tiliang hu (overhead-handle pot) that I believe to be Qing. On the left, the small modern pot 
I keep around for comparison. At first glance they may look quite similar — both purple-brown, both speckled, both 
clearly Yixing zisha. But spend a moment with them, and the differences begin to surface.

### Color and Tone

The Qing pot has a softer, slightly muted purple-brown — warm, but not vivid. There's a "dusty" quality to it, matte 
rather than saturated. The modern pot, in contrast, is a more vivid, redder brown, and the color reads as more uniform 
across the body. Old clay tends to look slightly desaturated; modern processed clay often looks more saturated, because 
the iron content has been more evenly distributed (or, in some cases, adjusted).

  <figcaption>The Qing tiliang hu up close — irregularly scattered mica flecks embedded in a clay matrix with its own texture and tonal variation, surface glowing rather than shining</figcaption>

The pale specks you see in both pots are mineral inclusions — mostly mica and quartz — that are natural to Yixing clay. 
A common claim is that old clay shows more varied, irregularly distributed inclusions, while modern processed clay shows 
uniform, evenly sized ones. There is something to this, but it is less reliable than it sounds: good modern clay — and 
skilled modern potters working with old clay stock — can absolutely produce varied inclusions too. In fact, if you look 
closely at my modern pot, its speckles also vary in size. So "uneven speckles" on its own is not a safe marker of age.

What I notice on these two pots is not so much the speckles themselves, but the clay around them. On the Qing pot, the 
matrix between the inclusions has its own subtle texture and tonal variation — small shifts in shade, faint pitting, a 
sense that the clay has "structure." On the modern pot, the matrix is smoother and more even, which makes the inclusions 
stand out more crisply against it, almost as if they had been placed on the surface rather than embedded in it. The 
polishing also seems to have leveled the speckles flush with the body, which adds to that "printed" quality.

So: look at the inclusions, but also look at what is between them. The matrix often tells you more than the specks do.

### Surface Quality

The Qing pot's surface has a soft sheen — a kind of inner glow that comes from years of patina building up in the porous 
old clay. The modern pot is glossier, harder-looking, more "finished" — the shine sits on the surface rather than 
emerging from within.

### Tonal Variation

If you look at the Qing pot carefully, you'll notice subtle shifts in tone across the curve of the body — slightly 
different shades on top versus the sides. This is consistent with wood-kiln firing, where temperature varies across the 
kiln and across the pot itself. The modern pot reads as a single continuous color — the hallmark of an even electric or 
gas kiln.

The lids make the contrast even clearer. The Qing lid (left in this image) shows that warmer, slightly reddish tone 
with a "moist"-looking matte surface even in direct sun. The inclusions are irregular and sit within the clay body, not 
just on top. The modern lid (right) is cooler and more purplish in tone, the surface drier and flatter, and the speckles 
stand out more crisply against the smoother matrix.

The teapot body in the background of this photo, by the way, matches the Qing lid — same warm tone, same clay character. 
The original lid must have been lost or broken at some point, and someone commissioned a replacement that approximates 
the original clay without quite matching it. It's a useful side-by-side, almost like a controlled experiment.

## Polishing Tells Its Own Story

Once you know both pots are polished, you can also read the way they were polished — and this is where another 
difference jumps out at me.

On the Qing pot, the polish is even, deep, and quiet. It doesn't draw attention to itself. The light catches the surface 
as a soft, continuous glow, and if you look closely, the polishing follows the natural curvature of the body. There are 
no directional marks, no fine parallel lines, no abrupt transitions. The whole surface feels of a piece. This is 
consistent with hand-polishing — a slow process where the craftsman would have used progressively finer abrasives, often 
finishing with leather, cloth, or a wax burnish, working with the form rather than against it.

On the modern pot, you can see what look like very fine "scratches" or directional lines on the surface — micro-marks 
that catch the light at certain angles and suggest the polish was done mechanically. A rotating wheel or buffing pad 
leaves exactly that kind of trace: faint parallel scoring, sometimes barely visible, but enough to give the surface a 
slightly "busy" character compared to the calm of the Qing pot. The shine is also harder and more reflective, sitting 
on top of the clay rather than seeming to rise from within it.

It's a subtle thing, and easy to miss if you aren't looking for it. But once you see it, you can't unsee it. The Qing 
polish feels like a finished surface; the modern polish feels like a finished coating.

## Construction Marks: What the Inside Tells You

Clay and polish are what your eye sees first, but the inside of the pot is often where the truth lives. Old Yixing pots 
were made entirely by hand using the paidashen (拍打身筒) method — paddle-and-slab construction, where slabs of clay are 
beaten into shape against a paddle and then joined. This process leaves traces:

- Tool marks from a bamboo or wooden rib inside the body
- Slight asymmetry where the body meets the base
- A hand-pressed seam, sometimes still visible as a faint ridge
- Finger impressions inside the spout joint or around the handle attachment
- Small irregularities in the wall thickness

A modern handmade pot can imitate most of these marks, but usually too cleanly. The tool marks are sometimes too 
regular, the seams too neat, the wall thickness too uniform. A slip-cast modern pot (the cheap kind) is even easier to 
spot — it will have a visible mold seam running vertically inside, and the interior will be uniformly smooth in a way no
hand-built pot ever is.

When in doubt, look inside.

## Firing and Sound

Old Yixing was fired in dragon kilns (龙窑) — long, sloped, wood-fired kilns that produced uneven temperatures across the 
firing chamber. This is part of why old pots show subtle tonal variation across the body. It also affects the sound the 
pot makes when tapped gently with a fingernail or a wooden chopstick.

A Qing pot, in my experience, gives a low, slightly dull, "wooden" ring — soft and short. A modern pot, often fired in 
electric or gas kilns at higher and more consistent temperatures, rings higher and clearer, almost like porcelain. This 
isn't an absolute rule — some over-fired old pots ring high, and some under-fired modern pots ring low — but combined 
with the other clues it adds another data point.

## A Tight Lid Can Be a Warning Sign

Another feature that gave me pause was the lid: it fit so well that it formed an almost vacuum-tight seal — cover the 
air hole with a finger, and the water stops mid-pour. That kind of precision can be achieved in two ways.

<figure class="full-size">
  <img src="/tea/posts/akela-missed-his-kill/small-pot-lid-hand.png" alt="The lid of the modern replica held in a hand, showing the domed top, the round knob, and the collar below — the pot visible in the background" loading="lazy" decoding="async" />
  <figcaption>The modern pot's lid held in hand — the collar below the dome is the part that seats into the pot opening and forms the near-vacuum seal</figcaption>
</figure>

The first is meticulous hand-fitting: a skilled maker patiently adjusting the lid to the body. This has always been 
possible, and a fine old pot can certainly have a well-fitted lid.

The second, and more telling, is double-firing — a technique where the pot is fired, the lid lip is then ground down to 
match the opening precisely, and the pot is fired again, locking in the perfect fit. Yixing clay shrinks relatively 
little, so a lid fitted this way can seal almost like a vacuum.

The thing to know is that double-firing as a systematic technique belongs to the modern Factory era. Occasional 
re-firing did happen in older times — usually as a "Hail Mary" to rescue an unusable pot, not as standard practice — but
the deliberate, routine double-firing that produces those famously tight modern lids is a comparatively recent 
development. So when a pot that claims real age has a suspiciously perfect, vacuum-tight lid, it is at least worth a 
second look.

As always, this is a signal, not a verdict. A tight lid alone proves nothing — plenty of genuine antiques have loose 
lids, and a master could fit one by hand. But combined with everything else I was seeing, it was one more clue pointing 
the same way.

## The Seal: A Cautionary Tale

<figure class="full-size">
  <img src="/tea/posts/akela-missed-his-kill/modern-pot-seal-closeup.png" alt="Close-up of the hand-carved seal on the lid of the modern pot — two characters in traditional seal-script style, cut with a knife into clay before firing" loading="lazy" decoding="async" />
  <figcaption>The lid of the modern pot — a hand-carved seal that reads convincingly at first glance</figcaption>
</figure>

Here is the bottom of the modern pot. The seal is hand-carved, not stamped — you can see this in the way the strokes are 
cut into the clay with visible knife marks, rather than pressed in with a uniform metal die. The carving is genuinely 
skilled: the strokes are sharp, the depth is consistent, the spacing of the characters is balanced, and the script style 
follows traditional seal-script conventions. If you came across this seal in isolation, without the rest of the pot to 
compare against, it would not raise an immediate red flag.

And that, exactly, is the problem.

A well-carved seal on a modern pot is one of the most effective deception tools in the Yixing replica trade. Skilled 
carvers in Yixing today can reproduce period-appropriate seal styles with great accuracy, and many of them learned their 
craft from the same families and lineages that produced the originals. The technical execution is not the giveaway — the 
context is.

A few things I now look at, after this experience:

**The clay around the seal.** Notice how the surface of this pot, even on the bottom, shows that uniform, 
machine-polished sheen with faint directional marks. On a genuine old export pot, the bottom is often less polished than 
the body — sometimes left almost raw — because it was the part not seen during use. A bottom polished to the same finish 
as the body is a small but telling sign of modern production.

**The relationship between seal and clay.** When a seal is carved into wet clay before firing, the edges of the strokes 
show a very slight "raised lip" where the clay was displaced by the knife. On this pot, the edges look clean and crisp — 
almost too crisp. On older pots, the lips of the strokes are often softer, partly from age, partly from the clay being 
slightly less refined and more "giving" under the blade.

**The feet.** This pot has three small ru yi feet (如意足), a traditional Chaozhou and Thai-export feature. They are 
well-formed, but they too show the same polished finish as the rest of the pot — and again, on a genuine old pot, the 
feet are often the place where you find honest, unpolished clay revealing its true character. Always check the feet.

**The seal style itself.** The carving here is clean, balanced, technically perfect — more consistent with late-20th to 
early-21st century Yixing workshop carving than with the slightly looser, more idiosyncratic carving you find on most 
genuine late Qing / ROC export pots. Old seals often have small irregularities: a stroke that is slightly off-center, a 
character that is a touch larger than its neighbor, the trace of the carver's hand. This seal feels too even.

The lesson here is the one I mentioned earlier: a seal can only confirm what the rest of the pot already tells you. It 
cannot, on its own, rescue a pot whose clay and finish point elsewhere. When the seal is the most convincing part of the 
pot, that itself should give you pause.

## What I Took Away

Buying this modern replica was an expensive lesson, but a fair one. It taught me three things I want to leave you with.

First, no single clue is enough. Clay alone, seal alone, polish alone — each can be faked, and as I learned with the 
inclusions, even the "rules" we repeat to each other don't always hold. You need to look at the pot as a whole and ask 
whether all the clues point in the same direction.

Second, trust is not a substitute for inspection. Even an honest seller can be wrong, and even a careful seller can be 
deceived upstream. Always ask for additional pictures, ask for the inside of the pot, ask for the underside. A seller 
who is confident in their attribution will not be offended by your questions.

Third, the only real way to train your eye is to handle real pots. Books and forum posts can take you part of the way, 
but until you have held a genuine Qing pot and a modern one side by side, the differences I have described in this post 
will remain abstract. Find a good dealer, visit museums, attend tea gatherings where collectors share their pieces. The 
eye learns by seeing.

I am still learning myself. This blog is partly a way for me to put my learning into words, and partly an invitation to 
others to share what they know. If you spot something I got wrong, or have your own experience to add, I would love to 
hear from you.
