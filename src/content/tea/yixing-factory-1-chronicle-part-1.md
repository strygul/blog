If you collect Factory #1 pots, you eventually want the big picture: not just which seal belongs to which decade, but *why* the factory looked the way it did when your pot was made. The original Chinese chronicle answers that question in relentless detail—year by year, kiln by kiln, export fair by export fair.

This is the first half of that story: from the scattered workshops of the mid-1950s through the moment Yixing became a household name among tea drinkers in Hong Kong and Taiwan. Think of it less as a textbook and more as the backstory behind the pots on your shelf.

For seal marks, labels, and period “fingerprints,” pair this with [Yixing Factory 1 Seals](/tea/f1-seals), [Green, White, and Laser Label Eras](/tea/early-teapots-factory-1-green-white-and-laser-labels), and [“Early Teapots”: Order versus Chaos](/tea/early-teapots-order-versus-chaos).

### 1954–1957: Gathering the Masters

After years of disruption, Yixing’s purple-sand trade did not restart as a single factory. It restarted as a rescue operation.

In 1954, fifty-nine workers regrouped as the Shushan Workshop Purple Sand Production Group (蜀山工场紫砂生产组). That October, seven of the great names—Zhu Kexin, Pei Shimin, Wu Yun'gen, Shi Fusheng, Fan Zhenggen, Shao Liuda, and Fan Zude—formed a workshop under the Tangdu Production Cooperative to fill a CNY 7,000 order from the China Artists Association: Squirrel and Grape, Bamboo Spring, Helix, Dingshu Spring, two-section bamboo segment, and other high-end sets. This was not mass-market pottery. It was a statement that the old craft still lived.

Gu Jingzhou helped plan the cooperative’s factory. Central Academy professor Gao Zhuang, who often traveled to Yixing on behalf of the Artists Association, became a close friend of Zhu Kexin—the two met in the summer of 1956, not 1954 as some timelines imply.

By December, thirty-one small kiln households and thirty-six workers had opened a factory on Shushan South Street. Eleven firings that year produced 440,000 pieces—two and a half times 1949 output. The government brought back masters Ren Ganting, Wang Yinchun, Jiang Rong, and Wu Chungeng as instructors. Recruitment pushed the local workforce above 130. Purple sand was selling again, at home and abroad.

1955 was the year the pieces started to click together. Workshops at Qianshu, Shushan sand-goods firms, and the South Street factory merged into the Shushan Pottery Production Cooperative (蜀山陶业生产合作社). Sixty standard forms—Fish-Dragon, Pine-Bamboo-Plum, Lotus Core, Crabapple, Han Jun, Eight-Sided, Lion Ball, and others—rolled off the line, mostly carved mid-grade wares priced between CNY 0.50 and 1.20. Output hit 750,000 pieces. More importantly, the factory took on sixty-one apprentices, including twenty-six educated youth, training under Ren Ganting, Gu Jingzhou, Zhu Kexin, Wang Yinchun, and Wu Yun'gen. Many of those students would define the next half-century of Yixing.

1956 brought two changes collectors still feel today. First, Jiangsu named seven masters—Ren Ganting, Zhu Kexin, Wang Yinchun, Wu Yun'gen, Pei Shimin, Gu Jingzhou, and Jiang Rong—as provincial technical instructors. Second, the factory introduced plaster molds for standardized six-cup shuiping pots. Uniform size at scale: 900,000 pieces, 282,000 exported through the Canton Fair to the Soviet Union, Indonesia, Burma, and Eastern Europe. A teenager named Wang Yinxian walked through the gates that year; she would later study under Wu Yun'gen, Zhu Kexin, and Gu Jingzhou, train in Beijing, and become one of the factory’s leading figures.

In 1957, Ren Ganting, Zhu Kexin, and Gu Jingzhou were received by Vice Chairman Zhu De at the National Congress of Arts and Crafts Workers. At the Canton Fair, Yixing showed 255 varieties—tea sets, flower pots, drum stools—and sold more than CNY 270,000 worth. Output passed one million pieces. Yixing was back on the map.

### 1958–1959: The Factory Is Born—and Immediately Overreaches

Collectors usually say Factory #1 was founded in 1958. That is the year the name Yixing Purple Sand Craft Factory (宜兴紫砂工艺厂) actually appeared.

The Great Leap Forward had already begun. In April, the Shushan cooperative, Yixing Hexin Ceramic Factory, and twenty-eight household workshops from Shangyuan and Qianluo merged into one entity with 2,067 staff—including 299 young apprentices. A Purple Sand Ceramics Middle School opened in August; the first apprentice intake from 1955 were now junior instructors teaching the next wave.

This is also when commodity pots began carrying the “China Yixing” (中国宜兴) base stamp—a landmark for anyone trying to date export ware. Gu Jingzhou threw himself into technical reform; by 1959 he was deputy head of research and helping design large tea services for the Jiangsu Hall of Beijing’s Great Hall of the People.

The factory’s ambitions matched the politics of the hour. Ya Ming designed the Yingbin and Ya Ming Square pots for the Jiangsu Hall. Shanghai painters commissioned decorated wares. Peony and Satellite pots shipped to more than fifty countries. Ren Ganting was named a national model worker.

But scale came at a cost. Some wares shifted to slip casting. The Waixi kiln was converted from sand ware; workshops moved west of the Shushan River. Coal dust replaced scarce firewood. Quality slipped even as per-kiln output rose. By late 1960, the factory was drowning in unsold stock—and the layoffs would begin.

---

### 1960–1965: Shrinking, Then Sharpening

The early 1960s are the factory’s hangover after the Leap.

Staff fell from more than two thousand to 578 by the end of 1963—barely a quarter of the 1958 peak. Xijiu Purple Sand Factory closed. Factory #1 tried slip-cast “luxury” shapes—lotus leaf, toad lotus seed—that nobody wanted, then pivoted to blunt, practical pots for the countryside: Fengdeng, Nongjia Le, Large Shouxing. Downdraft kilns slowly replaced dragon kilns. The government invested CNY 456,000 in two 100 m³ downdraft kilns and, by the late 1960s, mechanized clay prep with Raymond mills, mixers, and vacuum pug mills.

Not everything was decline. In 1964, an eccentric wheel for bodies reportedly raised efficiency tenfold; lids were iron-mold pressed before hand finishing (workers hated the quotas, and the method lasted only a few years). Japan wanted Youquan side-handle and Jingquan sets; everyone else got six-piece gift boxes. By 1965, output had recovered to 1.09 million pieces—forty-five percent above 1955—with value more than five times higher.

In April 1966, the Central Laboratory opened. The chronicle credits it with pushing first-grade rates from 76% to 90.25%. Within months, politics would matter more than quality control.

---

### 1966–1969: Teapots for Twenty Cents

The Cultural Revolution hit Yixing like everywhere else. From October 1966, production narrowed to tea sets and flower pots. Some craftspeople made glazed beer mugs and industrial ripple plate for export to Australia. Teapots were condemned as “Four Olds.” In department stores, a pot could sell for CNY 0.20.

And yet export did not stop. Shuiping and plain tea ware still went to Japan and Hong Kong—often the same forms collectors prize today, made in one of the darkest periods for the craft’s reputation at home.

On 28 December 1968, master carver Ren Ganting died at seventy-eight. With him went a generation of knife work that Factory #1 would spend years trying to match.

---

### 1970–1977: Japan, Ball Holes, and a Thousand-Year Answer

The 1970s are when the modern export pot takes shape—literally.

Japan asked for ball filters (球孔) in the spout; the main export port shifted from Guangzhou to Shanghai. Factory #1 designed fifty new forms for Guangdong Tea & Native Produce Import/Export and made 82,400 string-knob gall-bladder pots for France. Orders from Japan and Hong Kong—often re-exported to the United States—included miniature flower pots and custom Japanese samples. Production climbed every year.

1972 was symbolic: Small bamboo-segment and Kexin pear-form sets went to Japan as state gifts for Prime Minister Kakuei Tanaka. Teapots were export stars again.

Investment followed demand. A CNY 290,000, 54 m tunnel kiln replaced downdraft firing; vacuum pug mills arrived. Zhu Kexin, Gu Jingzhou, and Jiang Rong led development of silver inlay, patterned clay, crackle, and porcelain-bead decoration—and new lines such as Yongmei, Tibi, Lotus Root Wine Set, Jiyu, and Flat Bamboo Handle. Wang Yinxian, He Daohong, and others spent 1975 in a Central Academy training program at the Dingshan Provincial Ceramic Research Institute.

Meanwhile, archaeology answered a question the market had been asking for decades. In July 1976, a kiln site at Lijiao Yangjiaoshan pointed to purple-sand production from the mid–Northern Song onward—confirmation, with Nanjing University, that Yixing’s story did not begin in the Ming dynasty alone.

1977 brought loss and recognition: master Pei Shimin died at seventy-eight *(the source garbles the year as “2年”)*, while an Yixing Purple Sand Ceramics Exhibition in New York drew rave reviews. Exports through the decade reached 550,000 pieces; pots and sets were roughly sixty percent of the total, most with ball filters, shipped to Europe, the Americas, and Australia.

### 1978–1982: Awards, Crowds, and the First Wave of “Zisha Heat”

If the 1970s rebuilt the pipeline, the late 1970s turned on the tap.

In 1978, Zhu Kexin, Gu Jingzhou, and Jiang Rong became Arts and Crafts Masters; the factory averaged 150 new designs a year.

1979 was the victory lap. Wares shown in Shanghai and at the Forbidden City; Xu Hantang and Bao Zhongmei’s 15-piece Square-Round Coffee Set entered the Palace Museum, while another set sold to an overseas Chinese collector for CNY 1,200. Exports hit 805,000 pieces; new varieties jumped from fifty to 120+ per year; unit export value rose from USD 0.10 to USD 0.50. Tourists poured in—three thousand a year on average, sometimes more than five thousand—bringing CNY 700,000–800,000 in foreign exchange annually through the mid-1980s.

Then Hong Kong discovered Yixing all over again.

In September 1981, Gu Jingzhou, Gao Haigeng, Xu Xiutang, and Song Baiyin demonstrated at the 6th Asian Arts Festival in Hong Kong. The crowd’s reaction, according to the chronicle, was close to frenzy. Documentary crews filmed Purple Sand Craft Pottery (紫砂工艺陶) in five languages. Fangyuan (方圆) became a registered brand; tunnel kilns got automatic firing recorders. 200 cc high-grade small pots—Mini Heme, Jiyu, Xiaolongtuan, Fish-Dragon in three sizes—sold 30,000 per year to Southeast Asia and Taiwan.

1982 felt like a national event. Exhibitions at Beijing’s Meridian Gate and the History Museum; 200,000 pieces on sale sold out in days. Fangyuan Brand Advanced Purple Sand Tea Sets won a National Gold Award. Hong Kong dealers began rotating Purple Sand Exhibitions year after year. The phrase “zisha heat” (紫砂热) stops being metaphor and starts being economics.

Gu Jingzhou was rated Arts and Crafts Master in 1982, promoted to Senior in 1989, and in April 1988 became the first purple-sand artist named China Arts and Crafts Master—a title that would later attach to Jiang Rong, Wang Yinxian, Lu Yao Chen, Xu Xiutang, and others.

### 1980–1982: The Pots Taiwan Could Not Stop Buying

Two side stories from this window explain pots you still see at every tea table.

The Fujian oolong sets. From the late 1970s through the early 1980s, Fujian Tea Company placed huge orders for shuiping stamped with the China Native Produce & Animal By-Products Import & Export Corporation, Fujian Branch, Xiamen base mark—hongni and qingshuini, mostly “ten-cup” size (Taiwan collectors often call the same capacity “eight-cup”). Bodies carried 请饮 / 中国乌龙茶—“Please Drink / Chinese Oolong Tea”—in traditional characters, with a small tea boat and six matching cups. Taiwan prices went so high that, by the late 1980s, copyists were making the pot alone without boat or cups. If you own a *Qing Yin* pot, this is its factory origin story. See [“Early Teapots”: The Qing Yin Series](/tea/early-teapots-the-qing-yin-series).

The Special Arts Class. Hong Kong collector Luo Guixiang of Shuangyu Co. visited Yixing eight times, documented makers, and ordered seventeen high-end pots priced by artist reputation. Gu Jingzhou’s Special Arts Class copied historical masterpieces from Luo’s samples, with senior craft artists lecturing weekly. That classroom is the direct ancestor of today’s “master pot” market.

### 1983–1986: Stamps, Clones, and the Name on the Bottom

Success bred competition—and stamp politics.

Before ROC year 73 (1984), many bases read 中国宜兴, 荆溪惠孟臣, 荆溪×制, or 宜兴紫砂. On Taiwan, wording that sounded too “mainland” drew attention from the Garrison Command. Factory #1 changed its stamps; 1983–1984 saw pots with Guihai (癸亥) and Jiazi (甲子) year marks in regular script—the opening act of the [Chinese era base seals](/tea/early-teapots-factory-1-chinese-era-base-seals) collectors study today.

1983 also brought Xie Ruihua’s lecture at the Taipei Palace Museum and Taiwan’s first detailed Yixing forum with Hong Kong’s Ye Rongzhi—the moment many Taiwanese collectors first heard a coherent factory history. Japan’s Ronghua Co. ordered seven museum-grade replicas at CNY 500+ each, made by assigned factory artists.

1984 was the museum year on both sides of the strait: Taipei History Museum’s Ancient Tea Ware show (mostly antique Yixing), Hong Kong’s Flagstaff House Museum of Tea Ware opening, and Li Changhong demonstrating at New Orleans. Li Changhong / Shen Quhua’s *Bamboo Slip Tea Set* and Gu Shaopei / Tan Quanhai’s *Longevity Vase* won gold at Leipzig. Hongni shuiping replaced older slip-coated “purple outside, red inside” export standards. Factories #2 through #5 and Gaocun came online; Yixing stopped being a one-factory town.

Then came the pivot that reshaped the market. After dropping the “China Yixing” stamp, factory pots were harder to tell apart from Taiwanese wares—and sales fell. Factory #1 responded by putting personal name seals on bases. The chronicle is blunt: that decision directly fueled the modern “master pot” boom. High-grade 200 cc pots multiplied to 400+ types; Hong Kong firms (Jinfeng, Yingtai, Ocean Purple Sand, Shuangyu) published catalog books and ran name-artist shows. The heat was no longer regional. It was international.

1986 closed the era with both loss and expansion. Zhu Kexin died in March at eighty-three. Factory #3 opened in Chuanfu; Factory #2 held an Yixing Purple Sand Culture Festival. Xu Xiutang and Li Changhong’s Bingyin Great Luck pot won national first prize. Even Pinglin Tea Museum in Taiwan ordered a hundred “red outside, purple inside” pots built for baozhong tea—the chronicle claims they held heat so well that other oolongs brewed in them tasted like “coffee tea.”

### Where Part 1 Ends—and What Comes Next

The chronicle’s 1987 entries already point forward: 2,620 workers across four new plants, 12.9 million pieces, exports to fifty-four countries, USD 6.3 million in foreign exchange. Factory #1 alone earned USD 1.5 million. Sets entered Zhongnanhai. Old commodity lines—Yunyu, Longyan Wine Pot, small shuiping gift sets—were retired.

That is the cliff edge of Part 2, which carries the story through the late 1980s and the factory’s final state-owned years before privatization in 1997–1998 ([Chinese era base seals](/tea/early-teapots-factory-1-chinese-era-base-seals) and [Green Label dating](/tea/early-teapots-factory-1-green-white-and-laser-labels) pick up from there).

A few dates worth remembering:

- 1958 is the formal birth of 宜兴紫砂工艺厂—what collectors call Factory #1. The 1954–1957 story is the runway, not a separate “Factory #1” before the merger.
- Export “Made in China” green oval labels on this site are usually dated 1977–1986—slightly earlier than the peak export statistics clustered in 1979–1982.
- The Chinese original repeats itself often and even embeds a 2011 forum comment in the 1966 section. I merged those duplicates and corrected obvious typos (such as Pei Shimin’s death in 1977) without turning this into a footnote museum.
