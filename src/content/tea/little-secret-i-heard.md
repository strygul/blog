ust heard from a collector with more than 50 years of experience that when a dry Yixing teapot is filled to about 80% 
with hot water, it produces a faint bubbling sound. And the older the teapot, the louder and more noticeable the sound 
becomes.

Naturally, I rushed to try it out on my own teapots. I started with a hongni teapot from the mid-70s and a low-fired 
hongni from the early 80s.

And it worked.

Both teapots from the 70s and 80s produced the bubbling sound described. I could not, however, detect that the older 
teapot had a more noticeable sound. In fact, I believe the Green Label teapot was louder. Moreover, when I tried another 
teapot from the mid-70s, it barely made any sound at all.

So what should we conclude from this?

I do not believe this trick is a reliable way to recognize authentic teapots. Nevertheless, it contains a grain of truth.

Old teapots may produce a louder bubbling sound for several reasons.

Long-term use and repeated heating and drying cycles can create micro-fractures, open capillary pathways, and increase 
connectivity between pores. All of this can lead to a more synchronized release of trapped air, making it more audible.

In addition, very old pots that have sat unused for a long time may be extremely dry internally. When hot water hits a 
very dry porous body, it can trigger a stronger wetting reaction and more rapid air displacement.

Therefore, if you try this trick and your teapot does not make any sound, do not rush to dismiss it as inauthentic. 
There may be other factors at play besides age, such as firing temperature or usage history.

This is not a reliable age detector, but rather an interesting way to observe how a teapot interacts with water. It can 
reveal whether the pot is relatively open-pored or dense, whether it absorbs water easily, and whether it has been fully 
dried.
