---

### 1990–1991: Exhibition Diplomacy and National-Level Visibility

In the early 1990s, Factory #1 and its sister factories were still operating as a coordinated state-era system, but the emphasis had shifted from pure export volume toward branded cultural prestige.

The 1990 entries in the Chinese chronicle are dominated by events: named-master delegations to Trinidad and Tobago, Japan demonstrations, major Beijing exhibitions, and prize-winning wares at national ceramics competitions. The pattern is clear: Yixing was being promoted not just as industrial craft, but as a flagship of Chinese decorative arts.

By 1991, that push had spread overseas and into tea-culture festivals. The chronicle records first prizes at Hangzhou's International Tea Culture Festival and a major Singapore exhibition of top Yixing names including Gu Jingzhou and Jiang Rong.

---

### 1992–1994: Scholarship, Conferences, and Museum Legitimacy

These years matter because Factory #1's story moved from workshop memory into print, conference, and museum channels.

In 1992, *Yixing Zisha Zhenshang* (宜興紫砂珍賞) was published in Hong Kong, edited by Gu Jingzhou with Xu Xiutang and Li Changhong as deputy editors. The same year, the first "International Symposium on Zisha Culture" was held at Factory #1 with domestic and international scholars.

In 1994, China Post issued a four-stamp Yixing set featuring Shi Dabin, Chen Mingyuan, Shao Daheng, and Gu Jingzhou forms. Around the same period, both sides of the strait saw high-visibility museum activity, including the Taipei and Hong Kong circuits described in the chronicle.

### 1995–1996: Peak Prestige Before Structural Change

The chronicle records heavy international programming in 1995: named-artist exhibitions in Malaysia and Taiwan, plus film documentation projects. In 1996, another round of China Arts and Crafts Master recognitions included major Yixing names tied to Factory #1's training lineage.

One date in this period is not disputed: Gu Jingzhou died in 1996. This matches standard biographical references and helps anchor the end of the factory's "founding masters still active" era.

### 1997–1998: The End of the Old Factory #1 System

The source article's final line says "1997: Factory #1 was officially restructured into private ownership," then immediately adds an editor's correction: the more accurate terminal year is 1998, with Taiwan-side distributors also recalling 1998 as the end of the old distribution rights.

That 1997-1998 overlap is exactly how most collector circles describe the transition: restructuring began across 1997 and was effectively complete by 1998. Rather than force a single date, this post keeps the two-year window explicit.

What ended was not Yixing itself, but a specific institutional era: the centrally managed "factory pot" system that had defined quality control, training pipelines, and stamp conventions for four decades.

### Verification Notes and Disputed Claims

- **Factory #1 end date:** Teart Chat's own note corrects a strict 1997 reading toward 1998; secondary collector histories usually describe a 1997-1998 restructuring window.
- **Flagstaff Museum chronology:** The museum opening year (1984) and K.S. Lo's core donation (1981) are verified by Hong Kong institutional references.
- **Gu Jingzhou anchors:** 1988 national title and 1996 death are consistent across mainstream bios and industry references.
