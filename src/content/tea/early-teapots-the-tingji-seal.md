Collectors who have dabbled with early teapots for a long time will have heard of pots with the **Ting Ji** [庭記] seal. 
This does not refer to a specific type of pot, but to **any pot that has the characters “Ting Ji” stamped on the base 
inside the pot**. The seal is small (each character approximately 2.5 mm) and is a positive engraved seal, so the 
characters appear in relief. In some pots it is more obvious than in others. One reason for the variation is that the 
seal was carved on the **Ding Zhu** [定柱], a protruding round rod that supports the pot from the inside when the 
external base seal is applied. The pot is inverted over this rod so that it does not deform under the stamp. Because 
“Ting Ji” is engraved on the Ding Zhu, the pressure from applying the external seal on the outside causes the Ting Ji 
to be pressed into the clay from within. The impression can sometimes be poorly applied or off-centre.

### Only One Correct Tingji Seal

Based on collected information, there is **only one correct version** of the Tingji seal. If the Tingji seal does not match this known version, further examination is generally unnecessary.

- and **"Tingji pots"** are simply teapots that carry this seal.


### Era and Base Marks: 1960s to Mid-1970s

Pots with the Ting Ji seal **started appearing in the 1960s and ended around the mid-1970s**. This can be discerned from the external base seal: all known Ting Ji pots have one of two types.

1. **1960s standard six-character seals**, including “Jing Xin Hui Meng Chen” [景興惠孟臣], “Jing Xi Nan Meng Chen” [景熙南孟臣], and “Yixing Hui Meng Chen” [宜興惠孟臣].
2. **1960s–1970s 6/4 “Zhong Guo Yixing”** [中國宜興] seal.

The external base seal is **confined to these two types only**. After Factory No. 1 was established, these seals were used consecutively; the 6/4 “Zhong Guo Yixing” seal came into use in the late 1960s. The **7/3 seal appeared only in the mid-1970s**, by which time Ting Ji was no longer used. **There is no 7/3 “Zhong Guo Yixing” Ting Ji pot.**

### Do Not Rely on Seals Alone

Ting Ji pots entered Taiwan’s teapot market early. Because of the unique seal and the better workmanship, they became one of the most discussed among early teapot collectors—and **replicators often forge this seal**; the market has many fake “Ting Ji” pots at high prices. Evaluation cannot rely on seals alone. The following three points help identify authentic pieces.

#### 1. Era characteristics

Pots with the Ting Ji seal were made from the 1960s to the mid-1970s, so they **must match the characteristics of that period**. If the pot has a six-character base seal, compare its material and workmanship to 1960s pots; the six-character seal on a Ting Ji pot should be exactly the same as on other 1960s pots. If it has the 6/4 “Zhong Guo Yixing” seal, compare clay, workmanship, shape, and standards to 1970s pieces. Pots from the same period share the same era characteristics. If these criteria are not met, there is no need to study the piece further.

#### 2. Only two types of external base seal

The external base seal must be either the **1960s six-character seal** or the **6/4 “Zhong Guo Yixing”** seal. If the base seal falls outside these two categories, treat the piece with caution.

#### 3. Only one Ting Ji seal

Many replicas have a poorly carved Ting Ji seal—the character body is visibly different. Some high-end fakes replicate the seal well, so without having seen a genuine example it is easy to be fooled. The best approach is to **compare the pot to images of authenticated Ting Ji pots**.

In addition, the **five essential elements** (clay, craft, shape, marks, kiln) must all align with 1960s–mid-1970s Factory No. 1 characteristics for a piece to be considered credible.

### Variants Under the Tingji Name

Apart from the commonly seen **shuiping** [水平] standard pot, Ting Ji appears on other forms such as **xiaiao** [小矮歐], **bianyuan** [扁圓], and others with different cup sizes. Clay can be **red clay** or **purple clay (zisha)**; a larger proportion of Ting Ji pots are red clay, and purple-clay Ting Ji pots are rarer.

Regardless of form or clay, Ting Ji pots share a **high level of workmanship**. Compared with similar pots from the same era, most Ting Ji pots are among the more refined Factory Era pieces: touching up and finish are very precise, and poorly finished Ting Ji pots are hard to find. This is why they became so popular among early teapot collectors.

Forms and clays represented include:

- **Standard teapot forms** (e.g. shuiping)
- **"Guava" / line-floating styles**

Research suggests that during the 1960s–70s a famous Factory No. 1 artisan **Wang Ting Mei** [王庭梅] was responsible 
for the Ting Ji seal. In that period pots were not routinely signed by the maker, so records are scarce. Some Ting Ji 
pots also have **“Ding Mei” [定妹]** stamped on the inside of the lid; verification in Yixing has identified **Ding Mei 
as Wang Ting Mei’s childhood name**.

### Understanding “Tingji” Within This Framework

- **“Ting Ji” refers to the seal** stamped on the base inside the pot.
- **“Ting Ji pots”** are any pots carrying this seal, not a single shape.
- The unifying factor is the **Ting Ji seal** together with **1960s–mid-1970s Factory No. 1 characteristics**.

### Summary

- **Ting Ji** is a small positive seal (~2.5 mm per character) on the base inside the pot, impressed via the **Ding Zhu** support used when applying the external base seal; it can be faint or off-centre.
- There is **only one correct Ting Ji seal**; replicas often differ in character form or use the wrong base seal type.
- Ting Ji pots date from the **1960s to the mid-1970s**. External base seals are **only** (1) 1960s six-character seals (e.g. Jing Xin Hui Meng Chen, Jing Xi Nan Meng Chen, Yixing Hui Meng Chen) or (2) 6/4 “Zhong Guo Yixing”. **There is no 7/3-seal Ting Ji pot.**
- Shapes include shuiping, xiaiao, bianyuan, and others; clay is often **red** (more common), sometimes **purple/zisha** (rarer). Workmanship is typically very precise.
- **Wang Ting Mei** is believed responsible for the seal; **Ding Mei** on the lid (his childhood name) appears on some pieces and has been verified in Yixing.
- Authentication: match **era characteristics**, use **only the two allowed base seal types**, and **compare the Ting Ji seal** to authenticated examples; all **five elements** (clay, craft, shape, marks, kiln) must fit 1960s–mid-1970s Factory No. 1.

Before the mid-1970s, only Ting Ji was stamped on the inside base of these pots. They are representative of the period and worth collecting, but fakes are plentiful—new collectors are advised to compare specimens and cross-reference; fakes typically fail on material, workmanship, and shape. Handling and comparing pots remains the best way to appreciate why early teapots are so valued.
