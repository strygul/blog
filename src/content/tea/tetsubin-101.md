Tetsubin occupy a particularly comfortable place in tea folklore. Ask what they do and the answer arrives in sensory
language: the water becomes rounder, sweeter, softer, thicker, less sharp. These are useful words. They describe things a
careful drinker can look for. But they are not measurements, and repeating them does not tell us which kettle, which
water, which heating schedule, or which tea produced the impression.

That distinction matters more in gongfu than it might anywhere else. We control leaf weight, vessel volume, infusion
time, and temperature with almost comic precision. A kettle can quietly change several of those conditions at once: the
water may spend longer at heat, evaporate more, pick up or deposit material, and reach the leaf at a different
temperature. Meanwhile the source water was already changing extraction before the iron entered the picture.
[Controlled tea studies](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/11/1/80) show that water composition can alter colour,
turbidity, extraction, and sensory response—and that the result depends on the tea.

So the interesting question is not whether *tetsubin water* is good. That category is too broad to answer. The useful
question is narrower: **what does this documented kettle, in this condition, do to this water under this service
routine?**

This guide begins with the object because the object sets the limits of every later claim. We will separate a tetsubin
from an iron teapot, follow one documented casting process, learn what can and cannot be read from condition, look closely
at the only direct kettle-water study located for this article, and end with practical use and buying checks. The aim is
not to drain the romance from iron. It is to give the romance something solid to stand on.

## What a Tetsubin Is

A tetsubin is a cast-iron kettle with a body, side spout, lid, and overhead handle, made to heat and pour water. An
[eighteenth-century example in the Kyoto National Museum](https://knmdb.kyohaku.go.jp/30077.html) already has the
recognisable arrangement: cast-iron body and spout, handle lugs cast into the shoulders, and a separately fitted copper
handle. The leaves do not infuse in it. At the gongfu table it supplies hot water to the gaiwan or teapot.

This sounds obvious until you search for one. English listings routinely use *tetsubin* for enamelled cast-iron teapots
whose silhouette is similar but whose job is different. A modern iron *kyūsu* is made for infusing and serving tea;
[Iwachu's production account](https://iwachu.co.jp/pages/teapot-kitchenware-process) shows a glass-like enamel applied to
the interior. [Oigen's finish guide](https://oigen.jp/pages/finish) likewise distinguishes enamelled iron teapots and
warns against heating them because the enamel can crack. A *chanoyu-gama*, by contrast, heats tea water but has no pouring
spout or permanently fitted overhead handle; water is ladled from it.

The word *unlined* causes a second round of confusion. In this article it means **non-enamel and approved by the maker for
boiling water**. It does not mean that the interior must be naked, bright iron. Heat-intended kettles may carry an oxide
film, interior lacquer, or another documented treatment. One current
[Oigen kettle](https://oigen.jp/products/tetsubin_ryumon), for example, is intended for heating while its interior is
described as lacquered. A black interior could be oxide, lacquer, enamel, resin, old deposits, or mixed corrosion
products. Colour alone tells you very little.

Before accepting the name on a listing, ask three questions:

1. Is this exact object intended to boil water or to infuse tea?
2. What exactly is on the interior?
3. Which heat sources does the maker approve for this exact model?

If the seller cannot answer them, the exterior shape and the word *Japanese* do not rescue the purchase. Nor does
*Nambu*: Nambu is an important regional ironware tradition, not the definition of tetsubin.

## How Tetsubin Developed

Tetsubin history often begins with the claim that the vessel is 400 years old. It is a tidy sentence and an untidy piece
of chronology.

Japan has older casting traditions, older iron cauldrons, and older *chanoyu* kettles. In Morioka, Horie's
[technical history of Nambu ironware](https://www.jstage.jst.go.jp/article/sfj/70/5/70_255/_pdf/-char/ja) records the
appointment of Koizumi Goroshichi Kiyoyuki as a *kama* specialist in 1659. The traditional Koizumi account later credits
a descendant with reducing the tea-kettle form and adding a spout and handle. That is predecessor history and an
attributed origin story. It is not a securely dated invention certificate for the tetsubin.

The firmer object evidence begins in the eighteenth century. Kyoto National Museum catalogues an unsigned tetsubin from
that period, and the Association for the Promotion of Traditional Craft Industries places the development of Nambu
tetsubin in the same century, after the establishment of Morioka ironworking and tea-kettle production. The
[California Academy of Sciences research archive](https://researcharchive.calacademy.org/research/anthropology/tetsubin/hist.html)
is appropriately cautious: the first appearance is unclear, while a connection with the spread of sencha is plausible.
The safest formulation is therefore that the recognisable spouted, overhead-handled iron kettle emerged from older
casting and kettle traditions during the Edo period.

Nambu itself contains more than one history. Morioka's domain-supported kettle casting and the Oshu/Mizusawa tradition
developed along different lines; Horie reports that their production associations joined under the Nambu ironware name
in 1959. That modern umbrella should not be mistaken for a single ancient origin.

Nor was the object confined to Iwate. The government's
[Yamagata casting profile](https://www.tohoku.meti.go.jp/s_densan/yamagata_01.html) identifies tetsubin among the products
of a separate regional tradition, with its own emphasis on thin, light casting. A
[Takaoka City Museum record](https://online.bunka.go.jp/heritages/detail/517787) documents a late-Edo-to-Meiji kettle by
Kanamori Sahei. These examples do not map every centre of production; they are enough to break the false equation
*tetsubin = Nambu*.

Nambu ironware and Yamagata cast metal both received Traditional Craft designation in 1975. That designation is an
administrative recognition of regulated regional craft criteria, not a universal quality stamp for anything carrying a
regional word in a sales description. The historical clocks remain separate: roughly four centuries can describe a
regional ironworking or workshop lineage, while the securely documented tetsubin form belongs to the eighteenth century
and after.

## How One Is Made

The key to understanding a cast kettle is the empty space inside it. Molten iron cannot simply be poured into a
kettle-shaped hole; that would make a solid iron weight. The foundry needs an outer mold for the silhouette and a
temporary internal core—the *nakago*—for the hollow. The narrow clearance between the two becomes the kettle wall. Core
placement therefore governs wall thickness, mass, and whether the finished body is reasonably even.

Horie documents one traditional Nambu sequence in useful detail. It is a process worth learning, provided it is not
mistaken for the only legitimate recipe.

The maker prepares the body and bottom molds, the two-part sand-and-clay core, and separate molds or models for the lid,
spout, handle lugs, and lid knob. Successively finer layers of fired loam form the body mold. If the kettle carries an
*arare*—hailstone—pattern, each negative point is impressed into the damp mold; Horie records roughly 2,000 impressions
on one example. The core halves are joined and dried, the mold is smoked, and the parts are assembled with the core held
inside the outer form. Iron is then poured into the gap.

After cooling, the mold is opened and the temporary core broken out. Remaining sand is removed from the interior; flash
at the mold joins is knocked back or filed; the surface is brushed and finished. The
[craft association's production sequence](https://kougeihin.jp/craft/0701/) then shows oxidation treatment, leak
inspection, exterior colouring, spout finishing, and fitting of the separately forged handle. Inspection points and
tools differ among workshop accounts, but the broad order makes the object legible: form the void, cast the wall, remove
the temporary material, then turn a rough casting into a vessel that can hold, vent, and pour boiling water.

Surface treatment is another place where a specific practice often mutates into a universal claim. Horie describes
heating the cast body and lid at approximately 800–1000 °C for about twenty minutes. In that process an Fe3O4-rich oxide
film forms; it is retained on the interior and removed from the exterior as needed before colouring. Oigen describes its
own approximately 900 °C oxidation process and says most of its kettle interiors retain that surface. The resemblance is
real. The exact temperatures, duration, terminology, and later finishes remain workshop-specific.

The same is true everywhere else. Molds may be fired loam, other sand systems, or one-use detailed forms. A lid may be
iron or copper. A handle may be solid or hollow, fixed or designed to move. Exteriors may use natural lacquer, cashew
coating, iron-vinegar colouring, or tea liquid. Plain casting can involve as much control as elaborate relief work.

Process knowledge is valuable because it gives you better questions. It does not give you an authentication spell. An
aligned *arare* pattern does not by itself prove that every point was impressed by hand. A faint cleaned parting line
does not date a kettle. And *handmade* describes labour and craft value; it does not establish what the kettle will do to
water.

## How to Read a Tetsubin

Collectors naturally look first at signature, patina, shape, and decoration. A gongfu practitioner should begin
somewhere less glamorous: imagine the kettle full of near-boiling water, suspended by two small lugs, with steam seeking
an exit and the entire load rotating forward into a pour.

Read the object in that order.

**Purpose and interior.** Confirm maker-stated boiling use and obtain the maker's exact word for the interior: oxidized,
*kama-yaki*, lacquered, enamelled, resin-coated, or something else. A photograph may reveal a glaring problem; it cannot
identify a dark layer chemically.

**Body and leakage.** Inspect the base, spout root, shoulders, lug junctions, and any old repair. For a working purchase,
ask for a seller-authorised cold-water leak test. Cracks, seepage, deep cavities, and unknown water-contact repairs matter
more than attractive patina.

**Handle and lugs.** Look for movement where the handle meets the body, enough clearance for the hand, and balance at the
intended fill. Thick-looking handles may be hollow; slim ones may be solid. Treat every metal handle as hot regardless of
construction. A fixed handle is not a defect, and a moving handle is only useful if it moves without levering loose lugs.

**Lid and steam path.** A good fit helps control the object, but a boiling kettle must vent. Current maker instructions
warn that a closed lid can produce spitting or send hot water from the spout. Check how the lid is meant to sit during
heating and how it will be controlled during the pour.

**Spout.** The passage should be open, the lip clean, and the root free of cracks. A beautiful curve is not proof of a
clean cutoff. If the seller allows it, a cold-water pour test tells you more than a profile photograph.

**Capacity and mass.** Nominal volume is not working volume. Add roughly one kilogram for each litre of water to the
empty kettle, then ask whether that load can be lifted, stopped, and returned to the trivet through an entire session.
Thin, light walls are a documented Yamagata aim, not a universal rank. Thick walls may add mass and change heating and
cooling behaviour, but no controlled study located for this article identifies one ideal wall.

**Surface and condition.** An adherent maker-documented oxide surface or stable mineral scale can be expected. Small,
stationary orange points are not automatically structural failure. The useful distinction is not black versus white
versus red; it is adherent versus loose, stable versus spreading, clear water versus persistent coloured or off-smelling
water, and sound structure versus cracking, seepage, deep pitting, or unstable attachment. If material is flaking, water
remains coloured, or the structure moves, stop routine service and seek the maker or a qualified restorer.

Finally, read marks as leads rather than verdicts. Horie reports that maker marks became common in Nambu work around the
middle Meiji period, while the eighteenth-century Kyoto kettle is unsigned and a current maker notes that some of its
own models or production years may lack a stamp. A mark can begin provenance research. It cannot finish it. Box,
paperwork, prior-owner history, repair record, and comparison with documented objects all carry more weight than a
famous name pressed into iron.

This is also the limit of remote advice. Photographs can screen for a blocked spout, obvious crack, flaking layer, or
poorly disclosed repair. They cannot leak-test the base, load the handle, measure release into water, identify an oxide,
or authenticate workshop and age.

## What It May Do to Water

This is the section where tetsubin writing usually becomes either advertising or counter-advertising. The evidence
supports neither extreme.

Begin one step upstream. Water already changes tea without an iron kettle. In a
[controlled gaiwan study](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/11/1/80), different waters changed green-tea colour,
turbidity, EGCG extraction, bitterness, sweetness, and consumer preference, while black tea responded differently. A
separate [Food Chemistry study](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.foodchem.2007.05.098) found that mineralisation changed organic
carbon and polyphenol extraction. Another controlled comparison across
[green, oolong, and black teas](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.foodchem.2016.11.110) found effects from whole-water pH,
conductivity, and ion differences, with tea-dependent outcomes. None used a tetsubin. Together they establish the
important first link: **change the water and you can change the extraction and the cup**.

What, then, has been measured directly in iron kettles?

The most relevant paper located is a
[2004 Japanese study of six new kettles](https://www.jstage.jst.go.jp/article/jsfe2000/5/2/5_2_105/_pdf/-char/ja).
Four were Oigen-supplied Nanbu kettles—two forms, each with and without interior lacquer. Two were commercially purchased
foreign kettles whose makers and materials were not identified. The researchers heated low-hardness Hiroshima tap
water, starting at pH 7.00 and hardness 19.00 mg/L, adjusted the burners for similar temperature-rise rates, and reported
three runs. They measured pH, hardness, bicarbonate, residual chlorine, and total iron. They did **not** include an inert
non-iron kettle on the same heating schedule.

After thirty minutes, the four Nanbu kettles produced comparatively small changes. The two Marunambu examples reached
pH 8.35 and 7.76, and hardness 25.0 and 28.1 mg/L. Once evaporation was considered, most of the hardness rise was
concentration rather than a dramatic kettle effect. The highest total-iron result discussed for an uncoated Nanbu kettle
was about 0.03 mg/L. That is **total iron**: the study did not establish how much was dissolved, particulate, Fe(II), or
Fe(III).

The two unidentified kettles behaved very differently. After thirty minutes they reached pH 9.40 and 9.00, hardness
44.0 and 53.0 mg/L, and bicarbonate 24.6 and 30.4 mg/L. Rinse controls led the authors to propose that calcium and
magnesium previously adsorbed from tap water were being released. This does not show that their alloy leached those
minerals, and it certainly does not show that nationality explains the difference. It shows that six individual objects
did not form one behavioural class.

Water from the two kettle groups also produced different amino-acid and tannin extraction in a matcha preparation. That
is a measured chemical result, not a blinded taste result, and matcha prepared after thirty minutes of heating is not a
stand-in for repeated gongfu infusions.

The limitations are part of the finding, not footnotes to hide. Six is a small and asymmetric sample. The foreign
kettles were insufficiently identified. Sequential sampling reduced the water volume. Bicarbonate was measured, but not
a full alkalinity titration. Samples were cooled before pH measurement. There was no matched inert heating control, no
cooling comparison, no repeated-infusion protocol, and no blinded sensory panel.

Adjacent corrosion research explains why larger variation is credible without telling us its kettle-sized magnitude.
Health Canada's [technical review of iron in drinking-water systems](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/publications/healthy-living/guidelines-canadian-drinking-water-quality-guideline-technical-document-iron.html)
identifies pH, alkalinity, dissolved oxygen, disinfectants, chloride, sulfate, organics, temperature, flow, and existing
scale as controls on iron corrosion and release. A controlled
[cast-iron pipe study](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7520071/) likewise found that chloride, sulfate, and
dissolved inorganic carbon changed iron release. These are useful mechanisms. An old pipe at room temperature is not a
boiling kettle, so its numbers cannot be imported into tea service.

The evidence therefore falls into four clean categories:

- **Demonstrated:** the six tested kettles produced object-specific changes under that study's protocol; some released
  measurable total iron. Source-water composition can independently change tea extraction and sensory response.
- **Plausible:** finish, oxide and scale state, source water, prior use, heating time, and evaporation may make one
  tetsubin behave differently from another. The chemistry supports the mechanism; controlled gongfu measurements do not
  yet supply a general direction or size.
- **Reported:** practitioners, workshops, and promotional sources describe water as rounder, softer, sweeter, or
  mellower. Those words are reasonable hypotheses for a tasting comparison, not analytical definitions.
- **Unsupported:** that tetsubin universally lower hardness, uniquely remove residual disinfectant through iron,
  deliver a consistent iron species or dose, retain useful heat longer than matched alternatives, pair best with a
  particular tea family, or improve every tea.

Residual chlorine illustrates the causal problem neatly. It declined in the 2004 kettle runs, including in lacquered
and uncoated vessels. But a separate
[non-kettle heating study](https://doi.org/10.5985/jec.9.29) found that heating conditions themselves altered tap-water pH
and free residual chlorine. Without an inert control, the iron contribution cannot be isolated. Free chlorine is also
not the same thing as chloramine, and the located tea studies do not independently isolate a residual-disinfectant effect
on extraction or taste.

The honest synthesis is more useful than the slogan: **a tetsubin may change water, but the direction and magnitude
belong to the kettle-water-protocol system, not to the word *tetsubin*.**

## Using One at the Gongfu Table

Start cold. Identify the maker and model if possible, confirm the finish and approved heat source, and inspect the body,
spout, lugs, handle, lid, and any repair. An undocumented antique is not the place to improvise an aggressive
restoration.

First-use instructions vary enough to make one universal ritual indefensible. Suzuki Morihisa advises filling about 70%,
boiling on medium-or-lower heat, and discarding once or twice until the water clears.
[Iwachu](https://iwachu.co.jp/pages/care-tetsubin) says roughly 80%, medium heat, and two or three cycles. Oigen documents
a separate [hard-water scale-building routine](https://oigen.jp/pages/start_ironkettle01) for its products. These are
established workshops disagreeing about their own objects, which is precisely why the exact maker's instructions outrank
internet consensus.

The shared conservative core is narrower:

1. Inspect the kettle cold and rinse with water without scrubbing the interior.
2. Use the maker's working fill—commonly around 70–80%, not brimful—and leave steam headspace.
3. Heat moderately on a source approved for the exact model, with the lid removed or offset as instructed.
4. Discard first-use water until it is clear and without off odour.
5. Empty promptly and dry completely according to the maker's method.

During service, the tetsubin remains the water supply beside the brewing vessel. Do not put leaves in it. A larger
reservoir is not automatically more convenient: every litre adds roughly a kilogram, and water held warm between short
infusions can drift away from the temperature and freshness you intended. Choose a fill that can be controlled through
the whole session, not merely lifted once.

Heat compatibility is model-level information. One workshop may approve gas, charcoal, radiant electric, or coil heat
for a particular kettle while rejecting induction; another sells induction-compatible work. Even where induction is
permitted, makers warn against strong or rapid heating. Match the base to the appliance and use moderate power unless the
exact instructions say otherwise.

Steam management is part of pouring, not a separate safety footnote. Leave headspace, maintain the instructed vent path,
keep the spout away from people, use a dry heat-resistant mitt, and secure the lid before tilting. Body, knob, handle, and
escaping steam should all be assumed hot. Pour only at a rate the individual spout can stop cleanly.

There is one emergency rule worth memorising. If the kettle is accidentally boiled dry, turn off the heat and let it
cool naturally. **Do not refill it while hot.** Oigen's
[care guidance](https://oigen.jp/pages/q-a) warns that sudden water contact can create violent steam and rapid-cooling
fracture. Once fully cold, inspect before any cautious reuse; damage or abnormal water belongs with the maker or a
qualified restorer.

After the session, empty the kettle rather than storing water in it. Remove or offset the lid and allow the interior to
dry completely. Some makers permit a very brief gentle reheat; others prefer residual heat alone. The common rule is
complete drying, not prolonged empty heating. For storage, dry ventilation is universal enough; wrapping practices are
not.

Routine interior scrubbing and detergent are poor defaults. Suzuki Morihisa, Iwachu, and Oigen all tell owners to leave
stable interior oxide or scale alone and avoid scrubbing. That convergence is maker guidance, not proof that every white
deposit is protective. Use four practical condition categories:

- **Intact oxide or documented interior finish:** leave it alone.
- **Adherent mineral scale:** monitor it and follow the maker's care instructions; do not scrape it merely because it is
  pale.
- **Loose or spreading corrosion:** persistent coloured/off water, powder, flakes, or deepening pits justify stopping
  routine service and seeking assessment.
- **Structural or coating failure:** cracks, seepage, moving lugs, unstable repairs, or a lifting unknown layer mean do
  not heat the kettle.

Some workshops prescribe simmered tea as a tannin treatment for rust, while Oigen gives a longer and different routine.
These are maker-specific practices. No controlled cross-finish study located here establishes a universal tannin cure,
and a darkened spot does not prove that structural corrosion has stopped. Acid descaling and abrasive restoration should
likewise wait for instructions specific to the documented finish.

If you want to know what the kettle does to your tea, make the comparison deserve an answer. Use the same source water,
tea, dose, infusion schedule, and serving vessels. Match temperature **at the leaf**, not merely the moment each kettle
clicked off. Heat the control water in an inert vessel on a comparable schedule. Randomise or blind the cups if another
person can help. Record words such as *rounder* and *sweeter* as observations, then repeat the test on another day.

That home comparison can tell you whether a difference is useful in your own service. It cannot establish a universal
tetsubin effect—and it becomes much more informative once expectation, temperature, and source water are prevented from
answering on the kettle's behalf.

## A Compact Buying Framework

Buy the service job first and the story second. The following order is intentionally practical:

1. **Intended job.** Decide whether you need a daily water boiler, a display or collection object, or a tea-infusing
   vessel. Do not ask one object to be all three.
2. **Exact interior.** Obtain clear photographs and written confirmation of enamel, oxide, lacquer, resin, or another
   treatment. *Unlined* is not enough.
3. **Exact heat source.** Verify the model against your actual gas burner, charcoal setup, radiant element, coil, or
   induction hob. Region and age do not answer this.
4. **Working capacity and filled weight.** Start from the maker's usable fill, add approximately one kilogram per litre
   to the empty weight, and judge repeated lifting and cutoff—not nominal capacity on a product page.
5. **Condition.** Cracks, seepage, deep pitting, unknown repairs or coatings, unstable attachments, and persistently
   abnormal water outrank cosmetic patina. Request a cold leak test for a working purchase.
6. **Handle, lid, and pour.** Check lug security, hand clearance, balance, lid control, steam path, spout opening, stream,
   and cutoff at the intended fill.
7. **Wall and construction.** Compare mass with capacity, base stability, and the maker's stated method. Neither thinness
   nor heaviness is a universal quality grade.
8. **Disclosed method and finish.** Ask how the mold, surface, lid, and handle were made. Legitimate answers vary.
9. **Provenance.** Prefer invoice, box, papers, ownership history, documented comparison, and disclosed repairs over one
   stamp.
10. **Repair and return path.** A maker or seller who explains how cracks, coatings, loose handles, and returns are
    handled is offering part of the kettle's practical value.

This produces three useful buying categories without pretending that price is a water measurement.

A **documented working kettle** is heat-intended, serviceable, and supported by clear finish and care instructions. It
may be factory-produced or handmade. This is the baseline for daily use.

A **craft-focused kettle** adds labour-intensive mold work, patterning, fitting, finishing, lower-volume production, and
object-level refinement. Those things can be worth paying for because skilled labour, design, and provenance have value.
They do not promise a larger or better water effect.

An **artist or collector kettle** may derive its importance from authorship, rarity, age, exhibition history, or design.
Its suitability for repeated heating remains a separate question and may be lower than that of a modern working kettle.
A scarce object can be an excellent sculpture and the wrong daily appliance.

One-use molds, hand-impressed decoration, thin-wall control, fitted lids and spouts, forged handles, laborious finishes,
low production, documented authorship, provenance, and after-sale repair can all explain a premium. Industrial
repeatability and a clear warranty can also be valuable. None is direct evidence of sweetness, iron release, or superior
heat retention.

The red flags are consequently simple: an enamelled teapot sold as a stovetop tetsubin; no interior photograph; a seller
who will not discuss lining, leaks, or repairs; guaranteed taste language; an *ancient handmade* story supported only by
a stamp; or prestige presented as independent water science.

## What a Tetsubin Cannot Fix

A tetsubin cannot make unsuitable source water analytically soft. *Soft* as a sensory word may describe a real
impression, but hardness is calcium and magnesium concentration. In the direct study, hardness rose slightly in four
tested Nanbu kettles and much more in two unidentified kettles. Bicarbonate results from the same study do not substitute
for a full alkalinity measurement.

It cannot turn correlation into a mechanism. Residual chlorine declined during kettle heating, but ordinary heating
also changes pH and chlorine residual, and free chlorine is not chloramine. A total-iron result cannot be silently
rewritten as a specific dissolved iron species, a stable dose, or nutritional guidance.

It cannot promise the best tea pairing or superior service heat. Controlled non-iron research shows that teas respond
differently to water composition; no controlled tetsubin study located here compared tea families across repeated
gongfu infusions. The direct kettle study measured heating curves, not matched cooling performance. Working fill, total
mass, geometry, lid state, and room conditions all matter.

And the object cannot authenticate itself. A black interior does not identify magnetite. A stamp does not settle age or
maker. *Handmade* does not establish water performance. A four-century regional lineage does not make the distinct
vessel form four centuries old. *Traditional* does not make every induction hob safe.

None of this makes the reported sensory observations worthless. It makes them questions worthy of a controlled tasting.
A tetsubin earns its place first as a sound, usable, well-made hot-water tool: one whose weight suits the session, whose
pour can be controlled, whose finish and condition are known, and whose craft qualities remain satisfying before any
promise about sweetness is made. If it also produces water you prefer under a fair comparison, that is the right kind of
answer—specific, repeatable, and yours.
