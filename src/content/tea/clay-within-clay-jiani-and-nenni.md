Ask a room of collectors what their pot is made of and you'll get a clay name: *zini*, *zhuni*, *duanni*, *dicaoqing*. Those names carry the romance, the price tags, and most of the arguments. But none of them is the ore. They are the celebrities—and like most celebrities, they owe everything to two unglamorous parents that never get mentioned by name.

Those parents are **jiani** (甲泥, "armour clay") and **nenni** (嫩泥, "tender clay"). One is the hard host rock that the famous purple seams hide inside; the other is the soft clay that makes the famous reds, and that quietly props up almost everything else on the wheel. Understand these two, and the whole confusing family tree of "zisha" snaps into focus.

---

### First, what "zisha" actually is

"Zisha" (紫砂, "purple sand") sounds like it should name a single magical substance. Geologically it's nothing so tidy. The raw material from Yixing is an ordinary-sounding **clastic sedimentary rock**—silt and mud, compacted and lithified—laid down at the bottom of an ancient lake. A neutron-activation study in the *Microchemical Journal* puts it plainly: the clays are "composed of reddish-purple sediment created by the erosion of a Devonian-age (350 mya) lacustrine sediment bed," and "silty mudstone is the dominant lithology."

That "Devonian, ~350 million years" line gets repeated a lot, so one honest caveat: 350 Mya actually sits right on the Devonian–Carboniferous boundary, and the host beds straddle the Upper Devonian Wutong Formation and the Lower Carboniferous Gaolishan Formation. "Devonian" is a scholarly shorthand the primary literature itself uses—not a precise age. The point that matters is the *kind* of rock: a layered lake-bed deposit, not a vein of something exotic.

Yixing's own potters never used the word "zisha" for the ground in the first place. The traditional local scheme sorts the workable clays into **three** families by where and how they sit:

- **baini** (白泥) — white clay, for glazed and everyday wares;
- **jiani** (甲泥) — the "armour" clay, a hard, unweathered, purple-dominant siltstone, colloquially called *shigu* (石骨), "stone bone";
- **nenni** (嫩泥) — the "tender" clay, a soft, fine, easily weathered mudstone.

Everything a teapot person cares about comes out of the second and third of those. The mines themselves are classified the same way: the **jiani mines**—Huanglongshan (黄龙山, "Yellow Dragon Mountain"), its neighbour Qinglongshan, plus Dongshan, Xishan, Nanshan and Hufu—and the **nenni mines** at Zhaozhuang (赵庄) and Hongwei/Xiangshan. Huanglongshan is the famous one, the source of perhaps 80% of the prized purple clay.

### Jiani: the armour clay that hides the treasure

Here is the fact that reorganises everything. **Zini—the purple clay, the backbone of Yixing teaware—is not its own deposit. It is a seam inside the jiani.**

The canonical description, repeated almost word-for-word across the Chinese reference literature, is that *zini is an interlayer (夹层) within the jiani strata, a thin-bedded, lenticular body generally a few tens of centimetres to about a metre thick, buried hundreds of metres down*. The jiani is the thick, dull, workaday host rock—the stuff that becomes jars, vases, kiln furniture and cheap brown pottery. The purple zisha is a thin filling between its pages.

And it nests further. The green clay, **lüni** (绿泥), is itself a lens *inside the zini* seam. Hence the phrase every Yixing dealer eventually quotes at you—*泥中泥，岩中岩*, "clay within clay, rock within rock." It isn't poetry. It's stratigraphy: armour-clay, with a purple seam in it, with a green seam in *that*.

Mineralogically, jiani and the prized zisha that grows inside it are made of the same short list of ingredients. X-ray diffraction of common zisha raw materials—jiani, hongni and dicaoqing among them—shows a mix of **mica (illite/hydromica), kaolinite, α-quartz, and some hematite** (*Ceramics International*, Wu et al., 2013). The proportions shift from seam to seam, and that shift is most of what separates a "famous" clay from "ordinary" jiani body clay. Whether the difference between everyday jiani and prized zini is truly a *chemical* one or simply a matter of which seam in the same formation you happened to dig—that's a question the published science hasn't fully settled. Fire either, and what comes out is a brown-to-purple stoneware, because of the iron it carries.

The classic firing window for jiani body clay runs roughly 1100–1220 °C with modest shrinkage (around 6%); good zini sinters best near 1180 °C. Those numbers come mostly from trade and vendor sources rather than peer-reviewed work, so hold them loosely—but the broad picture (a forgiving, high-firing, low-shrinkage body) is consistent everywhere.

A quick word on **duanni** (段泥, also written 团泥, *tuanni*): the soft buff-and-grey clay so many "duan" pots are made of is usually described as a naturally co-occurring blend of zini and lüni from the same jiani system. Its exact pigeonhole is genuinely debated, and I'll leave it where the honest sources leave it—near the jiani greens, classification unsettled.

### Nenni: the "assistant of pottery vessels"

If jiani is the rock that hides the purple, nenni is the clay that makes everything *workable*—and that, separately, makes the famous reds.

Nenni is a tender mudstone: soft, fine-grained, weathered, slippery to the touch. On its own it was historically the raw material for cheap everyday pottery, and it fires to a plain brick-red. Its real importance is what it does in a blend. Because it's rich in plastic clay minerals (kaolinite and illite) and very fine, a little nenni added to a zisha body **raises the plasticity and lowers the sintering temperature**—which is precisely what lets a maker throw and assemble larger, thinner, more complicated forms without the body tearing or slumping.

This is not a modern trade secret. It's in the foundational Ming text on Yixing pots, Zhou Gaoqi's *Yangxian Minghu Xi* (《阳羡茗壶系》, c. 1640):

<p class="footnote">嫩泥，出趙莊山，以和一切色，上乃粘脂可築，蓋陶壺之丞弼也。<br/>"Nenni comes from Zhaozhuang Mountain. It harmonises every colour; sticky and buildable, it is the <em>chéngbì</em>—the assistant minister—of the pottery vessel."</p>

That word, **丞弼 (*chéngbì*)**—the trusted deputy who makes the ruler's work possible—is the single best description of nenni I know. It rarely gets to be the star. It is what lets the stars stand up.

And here is where the original article I started from goes wrong, in an instructive way. It files the red clay **hongni** in among the *jiani*-mine clays. The geology says otherwise. **Hongni and zhuni—the celebrated red clays—sit at the bottom of the nenni layer**, not in the jiani. The lower stratum of the Zhaozhuang nenni mine yields the iron-saturated material (石黄 *shihuang*, 朱砂泥 *zhushani*) that becomes red-firing clay. Zhuni in particular is freakishly iron-rich—on the order of **14–18% iron oxide**, against roughly 7–12% for ordinary zisha—which is why it fires that deep cinnabar and why it shrinks so violently.

So the corrected picture is more symmetrical—and more interesting—than the version on the tin. The two ores nobody names don't just underpin Yixing ware in general; **they split the entire celebrity roster between them.** Jiani fathers the purples and greens (zini, lüni, duanni). Nenni fathers the reds (hongni, zhuni). Every named clay you've ever paid a premium for is a child of one of these two.

### Why the dull stuff decides how the pot behaves

It's worth pausing on *why* this geology translates into the things collectors actually feel in the hand—because the mechanism is genuinely elegant, and it kills a few myths on contact.

The behaviour of a zisha body comes down to a tug-of-war between three mineral roles, all confirmed by materials-science work on the clays (e.g. the 2016 *Journal of Ceramics* study from Beijing University of Technology and the Palace Museum):

- **Kaolinite** is the plasticiser and the sinter-former—it lets the wet clay hold a shape and then fuse in the kiln. Nenni brings extra of it; that's its whole job.
- **Quartz** is the skeleton. It's non-plastic—the Chinese term is 骨架作用 ("framework function") or 瘠化 ("leaning-out")—and it's what keeps the body from shrinking and warping as it dries and fires. Zisha's characteristic open, breathable, double-pore structure owes a lot to this quartz framework.
- **Iron oxides**—hematite, with some goethite/limonite—are the colour, and crucially they are *native to the rock*. XRD on authentic Benshan zhuni finds it to be a natural mineral compound with high hematite content. Nobody adds iron to make real zisha red; the iron was in the lake bed 350 million years ago.

That last point is the hinge for everything that follows. The reason counterfeiters *add* iron oxide is precisely that genuine zisha contains it naturally. The fakes are imitating a mineral fact.

If you want the downstream consequences for a finished pot—porosity, the way a seasoned surface develops its *baojiang* sheen, how all of this interacts with tea—I've written about the clay-as-experienced-by-the-drinker elsewhere; see the [beginner's guide to early Yixing teapots](/tea/beginners-guide-to-early-yixing-teapots) and the notes on [clay in the 1960s–70s](/tea/early-teapots-clay-60s-and-70s) and on [understanding niangao clay](/tea/early-teapots-understanding-niangao-clay).

### Depleted, restricted, but not extinct

The romance around old clay has a real basis: after centuries of digging, Huanglongshan is largely worked out. The often-repeated "2005 ban" is real but routinely oversimplified, so here's the careful version.

The *Microchemical Journal* study states that "after centuries of extensive mining activities, Yixing's Zisha clay sources have been gradually depleted. In 2005, the China Yixing Municipal Government issued legislation to limit the quantity of raw materials extracted from the deposit." Note the word: **limit**, not abolish. A peer-reviewed ethnoarchaeology study (Gao & Quinn, 2023, UCL/Oxford) adds the sequel: "one of the Huanglongshan mines has been closed to individual exploitation since 2005, but a state-run company began limited exploitation in 2010." The community timeline fills in the middle—a tightening from a 2004 restriction, through the 2005 legislation, to the mines being physically walled off and reopened under state control around 2010.

So "moratorium" overstates it. Mining never fully stopped; it was nationalised and rationed. Genuine Huanglongshan clay is *expensive and finite*—quoted at over 70,000 RMB a tonne by 2016—but it is neither gone nor mystical. Stockpiles laid down before the restriction are real, and ordinary zisha continues to be processed. Depleted, not extinct.

### The myths the two ores let you see through

Once you hold the geology in your head, the marketing folklore around Yixing becomes much easier to read. A few worth retiring:

<div class="info-box">
<strong>"Zhuni is the rarest, finest <em>zisha</em>."</strong> &nbsp;Zhuni isn't a grade of the purple zisha at all—it's a high-iron clay from the bottom of the <em>nenni</em> layer. That doesn't make it lesser; it makes it a different lineage, with its own ruinous shrinkage and tiny kiln window. Calling it "the king of zisha" mixes up two separate ores.<br/><br/>
<strong>"Famous clays come from special, separate mines."</strong> &nbsp;The famous purples are thin seams inside the same armour-clay that becomes flowerpots. The prestige is about <em>which centimetre of the formation</em> the material came from, not a secret deposit.<br/><br/>
<strong>"Bright green (or blue) <em>lüni</em>."</strong> &nbsp;Natural lüni fires to a pale beige-yellow. A neon green is chemically impossible without added chromium oxide; a vivid blue needs cobalt. There is <em>no</em> naturally blue zisha. These are tell-tales of a 化工壶 ("chemical pot"), ordinary clay tinted with metal oxides.<br/><br/>
<strong>"Cheap mass-market 'zisha' is just lower-grade Yixing clay."</strong> &nbsp;Often it isn't Yixing clay at all. The ethnoarchaeology fieldwork found mould-pressed pots commonly made from iron-rich clay trucked in from Zhejiang and Anhui, machine-milled and vacuum de-aired in <em>days or hours</em> rather than the months of weathering real zisha gets—because genuine Huanglongshan clay is too costly to waste on them.
</div>

The most humbling myth-buster, though, comes from the lab. The same archaeometric study that nailed down the geology also tried to tell authentic zisha apart from look-alike regional clays—and found you *can't*, not by eye and not by major-element chemistry. Under XRF and SEM-EDS the imitations match; after a hot firing, both authentic zisha and ordinary clay show only the same two mineral phases, quartz and mullite. Only high-resolution **trace-element fingerprinting** (neutron activation, reading arsenic, antimony, and the rare earths) could reliably separate them.

Sit with that for a second. If a provenance lab with a reactor needs trace-element data to be sure, then the vendor's confident "this is definitely Huanglongshan dicaoqing" is, at best, an informed bet. Which is the right note to end on: the two ores no one talks about don't just make the pots. They quietly explain why so much of what's *said* about the pots should be taken with a pinch of—well, silty mudstone.
