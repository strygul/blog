This post is about identification of Factory 1 seals from the late 1950s–1960s

## Known Seal Types from the Late 1950s–1960s

Based on organized research by Taiwanese collectors, teapots produced by Yixing Factory No. 1 from approximately 1958 
through the 1960s can be grouped into several commonly recognized seal types:

- **Four-Leg Xi (四腳溪)** – *at least* 3 variations
- **Yixing Hui (宜興惠)** – *at least* 1
- **Jingxi Nan / Nan Meng Chen (荊溪南 / 南孟臣)** – *at least* 1
- **Da Jiao Xi (大腳溪)** – *at least* 4
- **Xiao Jiao Xi (小腳溪)** – *at least* 1
- **“China Yixing” (中國宜興)** – one early version

The phrase *“at least”* is used because no surviving archival documents conclusively confirm these as the only 
legitimate seals from the period. Although unlikely, additional variations may still exist. Potential outliers should 
not be dismissed prematurely if their clay, craftsmanship, and firing characteristics align with known 1960s standards.

---

## Understanding the So-Called “Hidden Line”

Collectors sometimes refer to a faint vertical mark—commonly called the **“hidden line” (暗線)**—that appears in the 
background of certain seal impressions. On some Da Jiao Xi seals, for instance, the character 「製」 may display a subtle 
vertical crack running upward through its center.

Some newcomers mistakenly treat the presence or absence of this feature as a strict indicator of authenticity. This 
approach is unreliable.

The hidden line is **not** an intentional mark. It is caused by natural fissures within the carved stone seal, which 
become more pronounced due to long-term use and thermal expansion and contraction. When stamped onto moist clay, these micro-cracks transfer as faint lines.

Key observations include:

- Some teapots display the hidden line, while others—sometimes even earlier examples—do not.
- The **same seal**, used on the **same day**, may produce impressions with or without the line depending on clay 
- moisture and stamping pressure.
- Similar lines appear across Da Jiao Xi, Four-Leg Xi, and Nan Meng Chen seals; they are not unique to a single variant.
- Because the line is merely a crack imprint, it is **easy to imitate** and therefore insufficient as a sole determining 
- factor.

The hidden line may assist in comparative study, but relying on it exclusively leads to misjudgment. The essential 
foundation of evaluation remains: **clay, craftsmanship, form, seal, and firing**.

---

## A Real Example: Two Seals, One Crack—Two Different Results

A comparative case illustrates the limitations of using the hidden line as a decisive criterion. A set of early standard 
teapots included two Nan Meng Chen pieces bearing impressions from the **same seal**:

- One seal impression clearly showed the hidden line.
- The other displayed **no visible hidden line**.

This contrast demonstrates how clay moisture content and stamping force influence whether the stone’s micro-crack appears 
in the final impression. The situation parallels well-known variations such as the “broken-frame” Four-Leg Xi seal, where 
differing impressions do not indicate authenticity or falseness.

A seal impression on **clay** behaves very differently from the same seal stamped on **paper**; clay reveals the complex 
interaction between material, pressure, and tool condition.

---

## Conclusion: Let the Evidence Speak

The hidden line is a useful secondary reference but never a definitive one. Its presence does not guarantee authenticity, and its absence does not imply a fake. Only a holistic assessment—integrating **clay**, **workmanship**, **form**, **seal**, and **firing**—provides a reliable foundation for evaluating early Yixing teapots.

Authentic early-period pieces require thorough, multi-angled observation. As comparative study deepens, distinctions become clearer and more consistent, allowing evidence—not assumptions—to guide judgment.
