This page collects and contextualizes information about Yixing Zisha teapots drawn from various sources, including scholarship, social media communities, and specialist collectors. It is intended as a reference guide — a living document for students of the craft who want to understand the potters, shops, and seal conventions that shaped Yixing ware across three centuries.

---

## Part I: Notable Potters and Craft Artists

Yixing's reputation rests on the shoulders of individual masters whose names appear again and again in collector literature. Understanding who they were — and when they worked — is the first step toward reading a pot accurately.

##### Zhu Hongjun (朱鸿钧, b. 1948)

A National Senior Craft Artist who earned his title in 2013. Born in Dingshu Town, he began his training in 1980 and subsequently studied under two celebrated masters: Xu Hangtang (徐汉棠) and Gu Shaopei (顾绍培). He also trained in the allied discipline of teapot decoration and carving under Tan Quanhai (谭泉海), developing a command of both form and surface ornament that marks his mature work.

##### EPudu (萼圃督)

A ceramic artist of the mid-Qing dynasty. Little biographical detail survives, but works bearing this mark are valued as mid-Qing period pieces.

##### Gan Cheng (淦成)

Active from the late Qing into the Republic of China period. His career spans the political upheaval that reshaped the Yixing industry, making his work a bridge between the imperial era and the modern workshop system.

##### Yang Pengnia (楊彭年, 1772–1854)

One of the most celebrated names in Yixing history. Yang Pengnia was a master craftsman from Yixing who collaborated closely with the scholar-official Chen Mansheng (陳曼生) to produce the iconic "Mansheng Teapots" (曼生壺) — a series of eighteen classical forms that set the standard for literati-style Yixing ware. This fusion of scholarly calligraphy and skilled pottery remains among the most admired achievements in the entire tradition.

##### Wang Nanlin (王南林)

A master craftsman active in the mid-Qing dynasty.

##### Jiang Zhenxiang (蒋貞祥)

Active in the late Qing, with works extending into the Republic of China era. He later founded the Yutai Pottery Shop (see below), combining his skills as a craftsman with the business acumen of a shop owner.

##### Wang Xichen (王熙臣)

A Republic of China–period artist affiliated with the Qimin Pottery Company (啓民陶器公司), a firm that represents the more organized, commercially structured face of early twentieth-century Yixing production.

##### Shao Quanzhang (邵全章, 1913–1993)

A late Qing–to–early-Republic potter from Shangyuan Village, Yixing. Shao Quanzhang came from a distinguished lineage: his grandfather Shao Futing (邵夫廷) and father Shao Yunfu (邵云甫) were both celebrated potters, and the family traced its craft identity back to Shao Daheng (邵大恒), a master of the Jiaqing period (1796–1850). Working alongside Wang Yinchun (王寅春) and Shen Xiaolu (沈孝陆), Shao Quanzhang produced Duoqiu pots and classically styled antique-form vessels that continued the family's long tradition.

##### Chen Mingyuan (陳鳴遠, 1622–1735)

One of the most historically significant potters in the entire Yixing canon. Active during the Qing dynasty, Chen Mingyuan is celebrated for his extraordinary technical range — his work encompasses teapots, brushpots, brush rests, and naturalistic sculptures of fruit, vegetables, and insects rendered in clay with remarkable fidelity. His pieces are among the most collected and most imitated in the tradition.

##### Anji (安吉)

A master of teapot making in the late Qing dynasty.

##### Zhou Yongfu (周永福)

A late Qing–dynasty potter.

##### Wang Dongshi (王東石)

A well-regarded potter in the early Republic of China period, known for elegant classical forms.

##### Fan Dasheng (范大生)

A Republic of China–period master who achieved international recognition: his work won gold medals at both the London Art Exhibition and the Singapore Nanyang Industrial Fair in 1935. The Fan family has a deep and continuous tradition in Yixing; other notable members include Fan Zhangen (範章恩), Fan Dingfu (範鼎甫), Fan Zhuangnongjia (範庄農家), Fan Jinfu (範錦甫), and Fan Zhuang (範占). Fan Dasheng is especially associated with compiling the family's accumulated knowledge of teapot-making history.

##### Cheng Shouzhen (程壽珍, 1865–1939)

One of the most sought-after names among international collectors. An adopted son of Yu Tingyu (卲友廷), Cheng Shouzhen reportedly limited himself in his later years to just three teapot forms — a discipline that concentrated his mastery to remarkable effect. He won numerous international awards and used the trade name *Zhen Ji* (真記). Collectors should be aware that his son Pengen (盤根) sometimes used Shouzhen's *Bingshui Daoren* (氷水道人) signature — a source of genuine confusion in the market.

##### Shao Jingnan (邵景南)

Active during the Daoguang Emperor era (1820–1850). His pen name *Lu Pi* (留佩) appears as an inscription on many of his vessels, and pots bearing this mark are commonly referred to as "Lu Pi jars" in collector parlance.

##### Hui Yigong (恵逸公)

A prominent figure from a distinguished mid-Qing family. He is traditionally paired with Hui Mengchen (恵孟臣) as "the two Huis" — a reference to the two great masters of the Hui lineage whose small, refined vermilion clay pots became the definitive vessel for Gongfu tea practice in southern China and throughout the diaspora.

##### Hu Yaoting (胡耀庭)

The regular large-format teapot artist for the Tiehuaxuan (鉄画軒) shop (see below), producing substantial pieces that displayed the shop's characteristic iron-needle decorative style at an impressive scale.

## Part II: Pottery Shops and Their Marks

From the mid-Qing dynasty onward, individual potters increasingly worked in relationship with commercial shops that commissioned, branded, and distributed their output. Understanding these shops — and the seals they used — is essential for dating and attributing Yixing ware.

##### Moyuanzhai (墨縁斎)

An ink-and-arts supply house turned pottery business name, active from the middle to the end of the Qing dynasty. Pieces attributed to Moyuanzhai are relatively common in Japanese collections, suggesting an active export trade. The earlier pieces bear nail-engraved seals; later examples use square stamped seals — a useful dating indicator.

##### Qing De Tang (清徳堂)

Named after the studio hall of the celebrated early Qing dynasty art patron Song Lou (宋荦, 1634–1713). As one of the most prestigious names in Qing connoisseurship, the Qing De Tang signature continued to appear on purple sand teapots from Song Lou's era through the end of the Republic of China period. Early examples display single-sided square seals in relief seal script; later work shifts to twin-sided square seals.

##### The Quanyin Pottery Shop (権寅陶器店)

Founded in 1890 by Shao Shuangda (邵赦大), a noted late Qing teapot maker who eventually devoted himself to shopkeeping. Teapots made by Shao himself bear the lid seal *Shao Shuada zuozhe* (邵赦大作者); commissioned pieces carry the shop seal *Quanyin-she ji* (権寅赦記).

##### The Yutai Pottery Shop (裕泰陶器店)

Founded during the Guangxu period by Jiang Zhenxiang (蔣貞祥), dealing primarily in purple sand. The shop's commission seal reads *Yangxian Jiang Yutai zhi* (陽羨蔣裕泰制) on the base, with an oval lid seal reading *Yutai* (裕泰). Jiang's own personal seal also appears on pieces he made directly.

##### Wanfeng Pottery Shop (萬豊順記)

Also founded during the Guangxu period, by Jiang Wanfeng (蔣萬豊), on Shushan South Street. Commissioned teapots bear the seal script stamp *Wanfeng* (萬豊), with some pieces also carrying a four-character standard script seal *Wanfeng Shun Ji* (萬豊順記) and small engravings reading *Wanfeng* (萬豊).

##### Ge Dehe Pottery Shop (葛德和)

A long-established firm with roots in Dingshan. In the 10th year of the Qing dynasty (around 1860), the Ge family expanded to Baitago. By 1910 (Xuantong 2), descendant Ge Wingyun (葛翼雲) opened a Shanghai retail shop at Lao Beimen Min Guo Lu, and in 1912 opened a pottery business in Nagoya, Japan, in joint venture with a Japanese merchant — one of the earliest known Yixing-Japan commercial connections. Products bore a large *fang* seal (方印) in standard script reading *Ge De He* (葛德和), with no individual potter's signature.

##### Fu Ji (福記)

Founded in the late Qing dynasty by master potter Cheng Shoufu (程寿福, 1853–1926), also known as Chen Shoufu (陳綬馥), who settled in Huaijia Village and became an adopted member of the Hui (惠) lineage. He inscribed his personal pieces with *Xiangfan* (香凡) or *Jingxi Hui Mengxin* (荊溪惠孟臣). During the Guangxu era, he opened a shop jointly with a Chaozhou merchant, specializing in small vermilion clay pots marked *Fu Ji* (福記) below the handle. These pieces were widely exported throughout Southeast Asia. The fourth generation of the Fu Ji lineage continues to produce teapots in Yixing today.

##### Yiguzhai (藝古斎)

Founded in 1905 by Zhao Songting (趙松亭), one of the most commercially creative figures in Yixing history. Zhao specialized in a "nostalgic style" that fused archaic forms with the tastes of Shanghai's poetry and collecting circles. He produced small vermilion clay pots and tub-shaped vessels for the Southeast Asian market — particularly Thailand — where they became extremely popular.

In 1925, Zhao and his son Zhao Qiantai (趙乾泰) rebuilt the historic Long Kiln, renaming it the Fuxing Kiln. There they produced a horizontal vermilion clay pot called the "gong pot" (貢壺) bearing the inscription *Gongju* (貢局). A luxury variant polished to a high luster with gold leaf on the mouth rim, lid rim, and legs was made exclusively for the Thai market. Zhao's stable of famous potters included Cheng Shouzhen (程寿珍), Jimei (儲銘), and Pan Degun (潘德根).

##### Tiehuaxuan (鉄画軒) Purple Sand Pottery Shop

Established in Shanghai in 1900 by Dai Guobao (戴国宝), a former master potter who had specialized in painting floral patterns on ceramics using iron needles. In the early period, three characters (*鉄画軒*) were knife-engraved vertically in either standard or running script. Later, the shop developed two distinct seal formats: a large square or round base seal reading *Tiehuaxuan zhi* (鉄画軒制) for larger pots, and a smaller elongated base seal in standard script for smaller pieces. Lid and handle marks bear maker signatures; the owner's inscription reads *Dai Shi* (戴氏).

##### Wu Desheng (吳德盛) Pottery Shop

Founded in 1916 by Wu Hanwen (吳漢文) near Chengjiao Bridge in Yixing. The shop's markings evolved considerably: early pieces carry a small square seal *Wu De Sheng* (吳德盛) on both base and lid; middle-period pieces use a larger square seal *Wu De Sheng zhi* (吳德盛制) or a round seal *Yi Xing Wu De Sheng zhi* (宜興吳德盛制). In the late 1920s, Wu Hanwen introduced the celebrated "Jin Ding Trademark" (金鼎商標) — a square seal featuring a three-legged *ding* vessel at center. A further variant, the *Song He Xuan* (松鶴軒) square seal in standard script, was also added in later years. All Wu Desheng and Jin Ding marks ceased use by the end of 1939, when the shop failed following the outbreak of war.

##### Chen Dinghe Pottery Factory (陳鼎和陶器廠)

Founded in 1916 by Chen Yuanming (陳元明), with branches in both Shanghai and Yixing. The factory produced an unusually wide range of purple sand wares: teapots, tea bowls, warming drum pots (with attached cups for hot water), brush caddies, brush washes, and flower vases. Export pieces were marked in English ("C.T.H.CO") alongside the Chinese seal, indicating a deliberate international trade strategy. One particularly notable export piece — a skin-colored gourd-shaped teapot with a lion-shaped lid handle — survives in a private American collection.

##### Mao Shunsheng (毛順生) Pottery Factory

Founded in 1932 by Mao Shunsheng (also known as Shunxing, 順興). Factory marks appear in several variants: *Mao Shunxing zhi* (毛順興制), *Mao Shunxing chupin* (毛順興出品), *Mao Shunxing ben chang zao* (毛順興本廠造), and *Mao Shunxing taogi chang zao* (毛順興陶器廠造). The factory employed some of the most celebrated potters of the era, including Zhu Kexin (朱可心), Wang Yinchun (王寅春), Wu Yungen (吳雲根), and Shen Xiaolu (沈孝鹿), with calligraphic inscriptions by Ren Genting (任淦庭).

##### Liyong Pottery Company (利用陶器公司)

Founded in 1915 by Zhou Wenbai (周文伯) and Shao Weiru (邵偉如), with a retail presence at the Liyong Pottery Shop (利永陶器店) in Yuyuan, Shanghai. The company's seals evolved through several phases: early relief seal script reading *Yi Xing Li Yong Gong Si* (宜興利用公司); later hollow relief and relief versions of *Li Yong Gong Si* (利用公司); a Shengxie (盛協) square seal in standard script during the late 1920s; then a clerical script *Yangxian Mingcha* (陽羨名茶) seal. By the 1930s the company had established its most famous trademark, the *Liangyou Shangbiao* (良友商標), or "Good Friend" trademark, in triangular or double-line square format.

##### Yongtai Company (永泰公司)

Founded by Jiang Zuoxin (江祖臣), operating a pottery business in Shanghai from the early twentieth century until Jiang's death in the 1930s. Note: Jiang Zanqing (江案卿) is a distinct individual, not the same person as Jiang Zuoxin — a conflation that appears in numerous sources.

## Part III: Special Clay Types and Decorative Traditions

##### Wuni (烏泥) — Dark Gray Unglazed Pottery

An unglazed dark gray pottery fired from dark brown clay, developed during the Guangxu period of the late Qing dynasty. Closely related are the "Wrapped Tin Pots," unique to the late Qing Jiaqing–Daoguang era (approximately 1796–1850), which feature a tin exterior cladding over a purple sand interior — a technically distinctive hybrid form.

##### Shao Jingnan's "Lu Pi" Jars (留佩)

The inscription *Lu Pi* (留佩) is the pen name of Shao Jingnan (邵景南), a Daoguang-era potter (1820–1850). Vessels bearing this inscription are known in collector circles as "Lu Pi jars" and are treated as a coherent category of attributable Daoguang work.

## Part IV: Dating by Seal Inscriptions

Understanding seals is not merely an academic exercise — it is the practical foundation of attributing and dating Yixing teapots. The following survey covers the most significant categories.

##### Dragon and Flower Seals (Guangxu 7–16, 1881–1890)

The appearance of dragon and flower seal inscriptions on teapots can be pinpointed to the period from Guangxu 7 to 16 (1881–1890) — a decade when collecting purple sand teapots became fashionable among both domestic connoisseurs and foreign buyers. The large twin-sided dragon seal appeared first, modeled on dragon designs from Guangxu-period coins. The smaller single-sided dragon seal followed slightly later, sharing the same design vocabulary. Flower seals appear in round and square formats: round versions feature a treasure design within a floral outer frame; square versions carry bird, animal, or character designs within the floral border.

##### The Kezhai (愙斎) Inscription (Guangxu 21–27, 1895–1901)

Teapots bearing the relief seal inscription *Kezhai* (愙斎) date to the period between 1895 and 1901. The inscription was the collector's mark of Wu Dacheng, a distinguished Qing calligrapher and epigrapher who assembled a celebrated collection of purple sand vessels. The base carries the *Kezhai* seal in relief; the lid bears a small mark identifying the individual potter.

##### Fan Zhuangnongjia (范莊農家)

The Fan family has produced celebrated teapot masters continuously from the Ming and Qing dynasties to the present. The mark *Fan Zhuangnongjia* (範庄農家) is one of several Fan family inscriptions encountered in the market. Associated works are attributed within the broad continuum of the Fan lineage's output.

##### The Nanyang Bazaar (南洋勧業会) Commemorative Mark (1910)

In April 1910, China's first major international industrial exposition — the Nanyang Industrial Exposition — was held in Nanjing under Qing government sponsorship. Teapots produced as commemorative gifts bear a seal-engraved inscription on the base: *Ji You Nanyang Quan Ye Hui Ji Nian Pin* (已酉南洋勧業会記念品). The lid of one documented example bears the inscription *Guangyu* (光裕), identifying the producing workshop.

##### The Gongju (貢局) Inscription (from 1925)

The *Gongju* (貢局) mark is associated with Zhao Songting's Yiguzhai operation following the reconstruction of the Fuxing Kiln in late 1925. The earliest examples place the two-character inscription *Gong Ju* or *Gong Hu* (贡壶) on the base. Later versions add framing lines above and below the characters. In some cases the mark appears on the lid rim, engraved with a sword-cut along the edge. The surface treatment varies: natural clay, sand-polished luster, or gold leaf on the rims and legs for export luxury pieces. The *Gongju* signature was adopted by other shops and makers as well, including Fu Ji (福記) and Changji (昌記).

##### Weihai-Wei Tongqingshun (威海衛同慶順造)

A specialized category of purple sand ware ordered by Weihai merchants of Shandong Province and decorated locally after delivery. The pots are distinguished by their heavy application of metal ornament — bell designs, dragon-lily combinations, and intricate noodle-pattern inlay — covering spouts, necks, slip holes, and lids. Many examples bear dual inscription in both Chinese and English: *威海衛同慶順造* alongside *TUNG KING SHUN FACTORY. WEHAIWEI. NO.1*.

##### The Purple Sand Pottery Public Association (1915–1946)

Established officially in 1915 (renamed from "Public Office" to "Public Association" in 1923), this body was the industry's collective quality mark for over three decades. Early seals read *Yi Xing Zi Sha* (宜興紫砂) in hollow or relief standard script. The Association was dissolved at the end of October 1946 (Republic of China year 35) and replaced by a producer cooperative, ending thirty-one years of institutional mark use.

##### Mouse Mark — Thai Export Pots

A distinctive subset of Yixing export ware bears a mouse mark on the back of the base. These were made specifically for the Thai market, with inscriptions in Siamese commemorating the 110th anniversary of the Thai dynasty (dated 1892). They provide an unusually precise case of dated Yixing export production.

## Part V: The Cooperative Era and Yixing Factory #1

The establishment of China's People's Republic in 1949 fundamentally reorganized the Yixing pottery industry. Private shops gave way to cooperatives; cooperatives eventually merged into a single state factory.

##### Hong Kong Xiangxing Tea Company Orders (1978–1979)

Among the better-documented late Yi Factory commissions are those placed by Hong Kong's Xiangxing Tea Company (香港祥興茶行) in 1978–1979. Standard teapots in 4-cup (190cc), 6-cup, and 8-cup sizes were produced, as well as non-standard 4- and 6-cup variants. The base carries the mark *Zhongguo Yixing* (中国宜興); the lid interior bears a black-glazed inscription reading *Xiangxing Tea Company* (祥興茶行) — a dual-marking convention identifying both origin and customer.

##### Tang Du Pottery Production Cooperative (湯渡陶業生産合作社, 1952–1955)

Founded in February 1952 by seven master potters including Pei Shimin (裴石民), Wu Yungen (吳雲根), Zhu Kexin (朱可心), and others. The cooperative's seal reads *Yi Xing Tang Du Tao Ye Sheng Chan He Zuo She Chu Pin* (宜興湯渡陶業生産合作社出品) — inscribed right to left, opposite to the modern convention. This direction of script is itself a dating indicator.

##### Shushan Pottery Production Cooperative (宜興蜀山陶業生産合作社, 1955–1958)

Established from late 1955 to early 1956 through a partnership with potters in the Qianqiu area. For the first six months it continued to use the Tang Du seal; thereafter it adopted its own mark: *Yi Xing Shu Shan Tao Ye Sheng Chan He Zuo She Chu Pin* (宜興蜀山陶業生産合作社出品), also in right-to-left script. This seal remained in use until April 1958, when Yixing Factory #1 was established.

##### Yixing Purple Sand Crafts Factory / Yi Factory #1 (宜興紫砂工芸廠, 1958–1997)

The state factory established in April 1958 produced what collectors today call "cooperative pots" (from around 1958), "Cultural Revolution pots" (1966–1976), and "Yi Factory pots" (1977–1997). Knowing which seal, mark format, and label design belongs to which sub-period is fundamental to dating any factory-era piece.

## A Note on Sources and Accuracy

The information assembled here draws on specialist collector communities, Japanese and Chinese scholarship, and first-hand market observation. Yixing studies remain an active and contested field: attributions are revised, new seals are documented, and historical records are occasionally corrected as research deepens. This guide should be taken as a working reference, not a definitive authority. When in doubt, consult primary sources, handle authenticated pieces, and seek expert opinion.
