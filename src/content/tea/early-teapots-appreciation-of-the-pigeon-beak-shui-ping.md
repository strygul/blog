# The “Pigeon Beak” Shui Ping Teapot: Origin, Design, and Collecting Notes

Among collectors of early Yixing teapots, the **“Pigeon Beak” Shui Ping** is one of the most recognizable designs. 
However, newcomers often find the name confusing. A pigeon’s beak is thin and sharp, while the spout of this teapot 
is **round, plump, and bulbous**. The name seems misleading at first glance.

In reality, the story behind the name is more nuanced.

---

# The Origin of the Name “Pigeon Beak”

Early Taiwanese collectors observed that the spout of certain Shui Ping teapots had evolved from the traditional 
straight form into a **fuller, bulging shape**. Rather than resembling a beak, the spout reminded them of the **neck or 
throat of racing pigeons**, which Taiwanese enthusiasts often raised for competition.

In Taiwanese Min-Nan (Hokkien), the term used was **“Hun Jiao Kui”**, referring specifically to the **bulging sac of a 
pigeon’s neck**. When translated into Mandarin Chinese, however, there was no exact equivalent expression. As a result, 
the term was simplified and translated loosely as **“Pigeon Beak.”**

Although the name persisted, the spout actually resembles the **pigeon’s neck more than its beak**.

---

# Japanese Influence on the Development of the Pigeon Beak

The emergence of the Pigeon Beak Shui Ping is closely tied to **export demand from Japan**.

In the **late 1950s**, Yixing Factory No. 1 was established and began large-scale production. Most of the teapots 
produced by the factory were intended for export, primarily to two major markets:

- Overseas Chinese communities in **Southeast Asia**
- **Japan**

By the **1960s**, Factory No. 1 aimed to expand production significantly. In doing so, it modified teapot shapes and 
technical features to meet the preferences of different markets.

## The Sencha Brewing Problem

Japanese consumers preferred **Sencha**, which typically consists of **short, broken tea leaves**. When brewed in 
traditional teapots with a **single-hole filter**, these small leaves frequently clogged the opening, making pouring 
difficult.

To solve this issue, Factory No. 1 developed two filtration innovations:

1. **Row-of-holes filter (Pigeon Beak design)**
2. **Ball filter (known in Taiwan as the “Beehive filter”)**

Both designs used **multiple small holes** to improve water flow and reduce clogging.

---

# Which Filter Came First?

Evidence suggests that the **row-of-holes filter preceded the ball filter**.

Two observations support this conclusion:

### 1. Surviving Teapots from the 1960s

Most surviving teapots from the **1960s** with multi-hole filters use the **row-of-holes (Pigeon Beak) design**, 
whether side-handled or rear-handled.

### 2. Appearance of Ball Filters

The **earliest ball filters** appear mainly in the **1970s**, particularly around the Cultural Revolution period.

From a functional perspective, the **ball filter is superior**, providing better water flow and lower risk of clogging. 
If both designs had appeared simultaneously in the 1960s, the ball filter would likely have replaced the row-of-holes 
design quickly.

Because this did not happen, it is reasonable to conclude that **the Pigeon Beak filter came first**.

---

# Export Reality: Not All Japanese Export Pots Had Multi-Hole Filters

A common misconception online is that all teapots exported to Japan in the **late 1960s and 1970s** used multi-hole 
filters.

This is not accurate.

Although many export teapots did feature multiple holes, **single-hole filters were still widely used**.

Examples include:

- **12-cup Shui Ping** – sometimes multi-hole
- **8-cup Shui Ping** – occasionally multi-hole
- **6-cup, 4-cup, and 2-cup Shui Ping** – mostly single-hole filters

Therefore, the presence of multiple holes alone **does not automatically indicate a Japanese export pot**.

---

# Characteristics of 1960s Pigeon Beak Teapots

Representative examples from the **1960s** share several key characteristics.

## Size

All known examples from this period are **12-cup Shui Ping teapots**.

Two handle styles exist:

- **Rear-handled (standard)** — more common
- **Side-handled** — produced specifically for Japanese customers

This variation clearly demonstrates that Factory No. 1 **adapted designs to suit export markets**.

## Seals

The base seals are the same as those used on other 1960s Shui Ping teapots, including:

- **Jing Xi Nan**
- **Four-leg Xi**
- **Yi Xing Hui**

There were **no seals unique to Pigeon Beak teapots**.

Some examples may also contain the **Ting Ji inner seal**, which appears across various early teapots.

## Workmanship

Typical features of 1960s production include:

- Thin lid lip
- Clean inner lid finishing
- Small, precise air hole
- Careful shaping

Clay materials are typically:

- **Red clay**
- **Purple clay**
- Occasionally **black clay**

---

# The 18-Hole Filter Pattern

One of the defining characteristics of 1960s Pigeon Beaks is the **18-hole filter**.

The holes are arranged in **four vertical columns**:

- First column: **4 holes**
- Second column: **5 holes**
- Third column: **5 holes**
- Fourth column: **4 holes**

The holes are **closely spaced and neatly aligned**.

Importantly, **14-hole filters do not appear in this period**.

---

# The Spout Shape

The spout of 1960s Pigeon Beaks has a distinctive profile:

- Slightly **longer**
- Smooth and **streamlined**
- Elegant **curvature**

This curvature gives the pot a **lively, perky appearance**. The overall silhouette is refined and balanced.

Collectors note that this subtle elegance is **extremely difficult to replicate**, making the spout profile an 
important indicator of authenticity.

---

# Differences in 1970s Pigeon Beaks

Teapots from the **1970s** appear similar at first glance, but closer inspection reveals differences.

## General Features

- Thin lid lip remains
- Small knob
- Red or purple clay

## Subtle Changes

- Spout slightly **shorter**
- Curvature **less elegant**
- Overall aesthetics **slightly reduced**

These differences likely reflect **changes in production molds**. While the basic shape could be reproduced, the subtle 
charm of earlier pieces became harder to maintain.

Side-handled versions also continued into the **1980s**, often bearing the **“Zhong Guo Yi Xing”** base seal.

Despite these changes, the **18-hole filter remained standard** during the 60s–70s.

---

# Pigeon Beaks in the Late 1970s and 1980s

During the **Green Label era (late 70s–80s)**, several noticeable changes occurred.

## Base Seals

Instead of the precise seals of the 1960s, these pots often feature **“messy” six-character seals**, typical of the 
period.

## Mold and Shape

- Less refined shaping
- Thicker lid lips
- Slightly blurred aesthetic

These changes reflect the **rapid expansion of production** in the 1980s.

## Clay Materials

Because the famous **Huanglongshan red clay deposits** could not meet rising demand, new mines were opened and 
**different clay blends** began to appear.

As a result, clay from this period often differs from that used in the 60s–70s.

---

# The “Queen” of Early Teapots

Collectors sometimes say:

> **“Please Drink China Oolong Tea (Qing Yin) is the king of early teapots, while the Pigeon Beak is the queen.”**

- **Qing Yin pot** – iconic **6-cup teapot of the 1970s**
- **Pigeon Beak** – representative **8-cup teapot of the 1960s–70s**

During this time, Taiwanese tea drinkers favored **high mountain oolong**, and the **8-cup size** was ideal for brewing.

The multi-hole filter prevented clogging, while the graceful design made the pot both **practical and beautiful**. As a 
result, Pigeon Beak teapots became extremely popular among tea drinkers and collectors.

---

# Beware of Forgeries

Because of their popularity, Pigeon Beak teapots are **the second most frequently forged early teapot**, after the 
Qing Yin.

On the market today, one can find many variations that do not match historical examples, including:

- Ball filters
- Six-cup versions
- Dual spouts
- Short-neck spouts
- Unusual clay types

Many were produced by smaller factories or modern workshops.

For **daily tea brewing**, it is not necessary to buy an early teapot—these pieces are expensive. A well-made teapot 
using pure clay will work perfectly.

However, if you intend to **collect early teapots**, careful study is essential. Examine verified examples, compare 
workmanship from the same era, and whenever possible **handle authentic pieces in person** before making a purchase.

Doing so greatly reduces the risk of buying a forgery.
