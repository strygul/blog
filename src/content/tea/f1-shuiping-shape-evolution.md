<a href="/tea/f1-seals">In the previous post,</a> we explored the seals used across different Factory 1 (F1) eras. As you may recall, while seals 
are an important piece of the puzzle, they are not enough to reliably determine the age of a Yixing teapot. Many seals 
overlap between eras, and even worse, the growing hype around F1 teapots has led to widespread forgery.

This is why relying solely on seals can be misleading.

To accurately date a teapot, we must pay close attention to other clues—details that are far harder to imitate.

One of the most reliable of these clues is the shape of the teapot itself.

In fact, the molds used in Factory 1 changed from decade to decade, and these differences directly shaped the aesthetic 
character of each period. When you place four F1 teapots side by side—one each from the 60s, 70s, 80s, and 90s—the 
contrast in their lines and proportions becomes immediately obvious.

The four examples shown here are typical representatives of the shuiping (水平壶) form from each era. This doesn’t mean 
that every teapot produced in those decades will look exactly like these; small variations are normal. Still, each era 
has its own distinct visual language—its own “character”—that consistently shines through.

And once you train your eye to recognize these subtle cues, you’ll find that the shape alone can often tell you far more 
about a teapot’s age than any seal ever could.

#### What to Look For

When observing early F1 pots, focus on these elements:
- Body lines
- Knob shape
- Lid profile
- Lid's lip thickness
- Clay body and texture

### 1960s: The Golden Standard

<figure class="full-size">
  <img src="/tea/posts/f1-shuiping-shape-evolution/60s.jpg" alt="1960s shuiping teapot" loading="lazy" decoding="async" />
  <figcaption>60s shuiping</figcaption>
</figure>

60s shuiping teapots are beloved for their low, rounded bodies—full, rich, and gently expanding outward with a sense of 
natural weight and flow. The craftsmanship during this decade reached an exceptional level; only highly skilled artisans 
could execute these forms with such precision and elegance. Because of this, 60s pots have long been favorites among 
collectors and continue to hold a reputation that has hardly faded with time.

Key Features of 1960s Shuipings
- A full, convex belly that stretches into an almost perfectly balanced three-dimensional ellipse
- The pot’s widest point positioned roughly at the midpoint of the body
- A thin lid lip, often referred to as the “sword lip”
- A small, neatly formed round bead knob
- Lid lines that flow gracefully into the body, creating a unified, harmonious silhouette

From any angle, a 1960s shuiping feels complete and refined—a functional object elevated into a quiet work of art. It’s this timeless aesthetic that makes these early pieces endlessly captivating, even decades later.

### 1970s: Balanced, Graceful, and Smoothly Transitional

<figure class="full-size">
  <img src="/tea/posts/f1-shuiping-shape-evolution/70s.jpg" alt="1970s shuiping teapot" loading="lazy" decoding="async" />
  <figcaption>Early 70s shuiping</figcaption>
</figure>

Compared with the artistic boldness of the 60s, 70s shuipings are a touch more restrained. Yet what they may lose in dramatic flair, they make up for with superb balance and proportion. The overall impression is one of cohesion and quiet refinement.

Key Features of 1970s Shuipings:
- A taller body compared with the 60s version
- The maximum width of the pot shifted slightly higher up the body
- A gentle, spherical flow from knob → lid → shoulder → body → foot
- The smoothest and most seamless lid-to-body transition of all decades

Collectors often praise the 70s for the harmony of their structural lines. If you’re searching for a classic example, 
look for a 70s red-clay shuiping often associated with brewing Chinese oolong teas—the Qingyin form. It stands as one 
of the most iconic shapes of the period and remains a favorite among Yixing enthusiasts.

### 1980s & 1990s: Teapots with "Russian Lips" and Less Harmony

If the lid is like a hat, then the 60s and 70s pots “wear the hat” beautifully. But in the 80s and 90s, the lid begins 
to look mismatched—sometimes even clumsy.

_Why?_
The lid lips of 80s and 90s pots became noticeably thicker. This creates a discord between the lid and the body. 
Designing in a rugged, heavy style is not a problem in itself—but when the “hat” becomes bulky while the body doesn’t 
follow the same design language, the imbalance becomes visually distracting.

As you could see with your own eyes, "everything was better before"... 

Fortunately, after Factory 1 closed its doors in 1997, a wave of private workshops began to emerge. Over the past decade, 
the art of Yixing zisha has experienced a true renaissance, driven in large part by growing domestic and international 
interest. Many of these workshops produce genuinely beautiful and well-crafted teapots, contributing to a vibrant new 
era of creativity.

However, with modern techniques and technology becoming more advanced, it has also become much easier to forge nearly 
every aspect of an authentic Yixing teapot—including the highly sought-after Factory 1 pieces. This makes relying on 
a single feature, such as a seal or clay color, increasingly risky.

To avoid fakes, it’s essential to evaluate a teapot as a whole. Look at every cue it offers—its shape, craftsmanship, 
clay body, firing characteristics, proportions, and details. Only when all these elements align can you be confident 
that the teapot in your hands is truly what it claims to be.
