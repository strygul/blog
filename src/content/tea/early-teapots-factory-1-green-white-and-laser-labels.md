### Scope: What Counts as “Early” Here

Strict collectors often treat the Green Label era as the outer limit of “early” Factory #1. Decades after privatization, though, interest has widened to later Factory #1 output. This piece uses a broader definition: Factory #1 from the late 1970s through **1997**, before privatization—spanning the **Green Label**, **White Label**, and **Laser Label** periods.

---

### Green Label (“Egg-Shaped Label”)

The Green Label is an oval sticker: green ground, gold **MADE IN CHINA** text. It is generally dated **1977–1986**. It is not a Factory #1–exclusive factory mark; it is an **export country-of-origin label** from the period after the Cultural Revolution, when raising output and earning foreign exchange were national priorities—and Factory #1’s priorities too.

#### Factory changes in the Green Label era

To diversify beyond the classic Shuiping line, Factory #1 expanded shapes and **variegated** wares. The well-known **1982 Xiao Pin** small pots belong to this phase. Molding remained standard, but molds were revised for **speed**: detail was sacrificed compared with 1970s tooling. Green Label pots therefore often look **cruder** than 1970s pieces—one of the main ways to separate the two periods.

#### Export channels and Taiwan

Most Yixing of the era went to Taiwan, Hong Kong, Japan, and Southeast Asia. Hong Kong’s largest wholesalers moved goods onward. Cross-strait politics meant **Taiwan banned many “Made in China” imports**; bases stamped **中国宜兴 / Zhong Guo Yi Xing** were sometimes **ground off** for re-export. As Taiwan’s market grew, Factory #1 worked with merchants on pots **without** that base seal, using instead **景溪惠孟臣** six-character seals or **景溪「姓」製** four-character seals so Hong Kong could re-export without customs friction.

Stickers satisfied **origin labeling** for non-Taiwan destinations. For Taiwan-bound goods via Hong Kong, stickers could be **removed** before sale—so presence or absence of the green sticker **does not map one-to-one** to production date.

#### Why some collectors cite 1981 or 1983 as the end of Green Label

Factory records show the **Fang Yuan (方圆)** trademark registered in **1981**—leading some to assume White Labels replaced Green Labels immediately. In practice, **Fang Yuan** often appeared on **packaging first**; surviving examples show **Fang Yuan on the box** while the pot still carries a **Green Label**, so White Label stickers did not begin the day the brand was registered.

Others tie **1983** to an international gold award for Fang Yuan tea ware and assume White Labels started then. The award timeline is plausible; **White Label adoption still lagged**, for reasons tied to factory branding (below).

### Two Signature Green Label Categories

#### Nei Zi Wai Hong (内紫外红)

**Purple clay body**, outer surface (sometimes interior too) coated with fine **red** clay. The workflow existed from late Qing into the ROC; under Factory #1 it spread from the **mid-1970s** because the market favored red clay, and premium red suspension collected during refining was applied over purple bodies. Large volumes appeared in the **Green Label** window; later, new mines and chemical additives largely ended this pattern—so heavy Nei Zi Wai Hong is often read as **characteristic of the Green Label era**.

#### Jing Xi “Name” Made (景溪「姓」製)

Base chops implying a **surname** “made in Jing Xi” (the old name for Yixing). One common reading is **workshop or group** identification via the lead craftsman’s surname—not necessarily one named artisan’s hand work. These variegated pieces often show **finer** clay and touch-up than ordinary mass-market pots of the same years—premium SKUs for the era and a bridge to the **“famous artisan”** wave of the 1980s–90s.

---

### General Green Label Traits

Beyond those categories, speed-focused production yields **thicker, heavier, rougher** profiles than 1970s pots—lid rim, lid wall, body. Six-character base seals are **messy and varied**; **Zhong Guo Yi Xing**, poem chops, and other variants appear. Clay tends to be **relatively straightforward**: traditional materials plus prized oddities such as **Nian Gao** and **Black Star** clay—before chemical colorants dominated.

---

### White Label (Four-Edge Label)

Used from **1987** into the early **1990s** (commonly cited **1987–1993**). The sticker carries **Fang Yuan Brand / 方圆牌** and **Yixing China 紫砂**. Several variants exist (color, presence of a white rule under the brand name). Material is **paper laminated with plastic**, which **delaminates** with age—hard to fake convincingly. Early versions had **pure white** lettering (“White Label”); later prints used metallic silver or gold tones.

**Purpose differed from Green Label**: Green addressed **export rules**; White distinguished **Factory #1** from **Factories 2–4** (1980 onward) as Yixing scaled. Because the White Label also carried **China**, it could **replace** the Green export sticker—helping Green Labels disappear from new retail.

#### White Label workmanship and clay

Structurally, White Label continues Green Label: **Shuiping** vs **variegated**. Shuiping molds resemble Green Label tooling with **incremental refinement**—not 1970s fineness, but **sharper** than Green Label. Clay is generally **still good** if you choose carefully, though processing and firing can read **shinier and flatter** than Green Label after side-by-side comparison.

Variegated output **explodes** in variety versus Green Label. From the 1980s, potters could use **personal name seals**, feeding diversity and the rise of **celebrity artisans**.

#### “No label” between Green and White?

Some collectors describe a **label-less** gap. Export-focused Green Label practice explains much of it: stickers went on for **some destinations**, not at the kiln; Taiwan-bound pieces often had stickers **stripped**. Many pots never had a green sticker—so “no label” is not necessarily a separate chronological era.

---

### Laser Label (1993–1997)

Round **laser** stickers identify Factory #1 pots in this window—again versus other factories. The switch aimed at **counterfeits**: laser labels were harder to imitate than paper.

Workmanship **slips** versus White Label for many pieces: **Shuiping** molds weaken as the line loses centrality; variegated pots grow **sloppier**. Clay ranges widely—**straight Yixing**, blends, colored bodies—with uneven results; some beautiful pots **perform** poorly in use as porosity and aging behavior diverge. A frequent narrative: after White Label, **top clay and craftsmen** skew toward **ming jia / “famous artisan”** production—starving the commodity Factory #1 line ahead of **privatization**.

---

### Ranking Eras (Broad Strokes)

Across labels, **variety of shapes increases** after Green Label. For **workmanship** and often **clay**, the loose hierarchy collectors cite is **Green > White > Laser**. Pieces from these periods remain **findable**; handling real examples beats theory alone. **Beginners** often start with **White or Green Label** pricing and clay quality versus many modern copies relying on chemical coloring.

**Labels help but are not sufficient**: reproduction stickers exist, and post-privatization **Fang Yuan** branding continued. Cross-check **base seals** (e.g. Green Label’s chaotic six-character chops, Nei Zi Wai Hong), **molding and clay**, and **sticker aging** together.

---

### Analogy to Pu-erh Packaging

The progression mirrors how collectors talk about **pu-erh** eras: **Green Label** sits alongside **1980s thick-paper** cakes; **White Label** with **1980s thin-paper** wraps; **Laser Label** with **1990s machined thin-paper / laser-era bricks**. Brewing **1980s thick-paper** tea with an **1980s Green Label** pot is one way to pair **tea and teapot from the same rough vintage**—a different emotional thread than mixing periods decades apart.
