Collecting Yixing teapots is both thrilling and demanding. The challenge lies in the overwhelming number of counterfeits
circulating in the market, a result of the growing global interest in Yixing pottery. Unfortunately for new Western
collectors, there is still very little reliable information available in English on how to distinguish genuine zisha
teapots.
After spending countless hours researching—and a fair amount of money on fake or low-quality pots—I gradually gathered
bits of knowledge that helped me identify true Yixing teapots. Here, I want to share what I’ve learned about recognizing
the seals used by Yixing Factory 1.

While seals alone cannot guarantee a teapot’s authenticity (they too can be forged), studying them remains an essential
step in identifying genuine Yixing pottery. With careful observation, an experienced collector can often tell whether a
teapot truly is what it claims to be simply by examining the smallest details of its seal.

To assist new collectors, I’ve compiled in this post a selection of seals from authentic Factory 1 teapots, representing
various production eras. The credit for organizing and refining this knowledge belongs not to me but to many generous
members of the tea community who helped guide my research. My contribution lies mainly in making this information more
accessible to fellow zisha enthusiasts who, like me, are just beginning their collecting journey.

Before diving into the categorization of seals by era, it’s important to note that the proposed timeframes are
conditional. They reflect the socio-political developments in China that directly influenced Yixing teaware production,
rather than pinpointing the exact years when a particular seal was in use.

Because it is nearly impossible to assign precise year-by-year boundaries to most seal types (with only a few
exceptions),
collectors and researchers often refer to them by decades — the ’60s, ’70s, or ’80s. However, this decade-based
shorthand
can obscure the historical context that profoundly shaped the craftsmanship, materials, and forms of the teapots
themselves.

For this reason, using social and political context as the basis for periodization offers a more accurate and meaningful
way to understand the evolving relationship between Yixing pottery and the times in which it was made.

## 1958 to 1966: The Early Stage of the Cultural Revolution

#### JingXi Hui MengChen (六汉字 荆溪惠孟臣)

Jingxi is an older name for part of Yixing where lived the legendary Ming-dynasty potter Hui Mengchen whose work
inspired generations of Yixing artisans. This particular seal appears in three distinct variants, each distinguished by
subtle differences in the calligraphy of the middle character Xi on the right side — the “four-leg Xi,” the “big Xi,”
and the “small Xi.”

#### JingXi Nan MengChen (六汉字 荆溪南孟臣)

The inscription “荆溪南” (Nan Jingxi) literally translates to “South Jingxi.” From the 1990s onward, another seal began
appearing on Yixing teapots — “北孟臣” (Bei Jingxi), meaning “North Jingxi.” These marks functioned as quality
designations.

In the market context of the time, the label “South” was associated with superior craftsmanship and finer clay, whereas
“North” denoted standard or mass-market teapots. The terms served a role similar to a trademark or regional quality
mark, signaling origin and grade within the Yixing pottery tradition

## 1966 to 1976: The Cultural Revolution

In the late 1960s, the “China Yixing” (中国宜兴) seal began to appear, gradually replacing the earlier six-character
version.
From then on, the four-character “China Yixing” seal became increasingly common, making it difficult—if not
impossible—to
determine a teapot’s age based solely on its seal from this period.

Among the various examples, subtle differences in character style, proportions, and layout ratios help distinguish one
seal from another.

#### Zhong Guo YiXing (中國宜興) 6:4

“Zhong Guo” literally translates as “Central Nation” — the name China uses for itself. The seal’s proportions, 6:4,
define its characteristic rectangular shape.

This is perhaps the most common seal encountered during this period and many other, appearing on countless Yixing
teapots produced for both domestic use and export. There are several variations of it, the key difference lies in the
depiction of the 興 character (bottom left). Look closely at how the lines connect (or don't) — each variation reveals
a slightly different style.

## 1977 to 1982: The Green Label Period

Products from the Yixing Zisha Craft Factory (also known as Factory 1) began carrying official labels in 1977. Between
1977 and 1982, the teapots produced there featured a distinctive green label proudly bearing the words “Made in China.”
![Yixing Factory 1 Green_Label_Teapot](/tea/posts/1/green_label_teapot.jpg)

During this period, many seals overlapped with those used in the preceding Cultural Revolution era. You’ll often
encounter marks such as Zhong Guo YiXing 6:4, 7:3, square, or key-shaped seals — making it quite challenging to identify
a teapot’s exact era based on the seal alone.
